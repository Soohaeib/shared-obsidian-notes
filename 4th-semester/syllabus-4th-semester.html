Syllabus 4th Semester

Course Code: BCC 206

Course Title: Insurance and Risk Management

Marks: 100 Credit: 3 hours (Written: 75, Internal Evaluation: 20, Viva: 05)

Course Objectives:

Insurance and Risk Management is a core course of business administration discipline of University of Chittagong. It is designed to provide an introduction to the basic principles of risk and insurance prevalent in financial decision making.

  1. To acquaint students with the basic knowledge of the principles of risk and insurance and the methods of risk management found in strategic planning.
  2. To provide students with a basic knowledge of general, life and health insurance.
  3. To help students develop a sound personal insurance program for meeting the major risks in our society.
  4. To give students some basic principles of insurance buying, with an emphasis on how to develop a strong insurance and financial estate planning program.
  5. To offer a systematic framework for analysing any insurance contract.
  6. To learn about the ethical issues facing business and an appreciation of the framework for making ethical decisions.

Course Contents:

  1. Introduction to Risk Management: Concept, Types of Risk, Risk Faced by Businesses and Individuals; Risk Management, The Risk Management Process and Methods, and Business Risk Management Organisation, Factors affecting risk.
  2. Objective of Risk Management: The Need for a Risk Management Objective, Understanding the Costs of Risk, Firm Value Maximisation and the Cost of Risk, Individual Risk Management and the Cost of Risk, and Risk Management & Societal Welfare.
  3. Risk Identification and Measurement: Risk Identification; Basic Concepts from Probability and Statistics; and Evaluating the Frequency & Severity of Losses.
  4. Risk Management by Individuals and Corporations: Risk Aversion and Demand for Insurance by Individuals - the Effects of Insurance on Wealth, Risk Aversion, Other Factors Affecting an Individual's Demand for Insurance; and Business Risk Management - Shareholder Diversification - Closely Held Business - Why purchase insurance when shareholders are diversified.
  5. Introduction to Insurance: Definition and Basic Characteristics of Insurance, Requirement of an Insurable Risk, Adverse Selection and Insurance, Insurance and Gambling Compared, Insurance and Hedging Compared, Types of Insurance, Benefits and Costs of Insurance to Society.
  6. Life Insurance: Life Insurance Product Overview, Traditional Products: Term, Endowment, and Whole Life Insurance, Product Innovation: Universal and Variable Life Insurance, Tax benefits from Life Insurance Policies, Annuity Contracts, Life Insurance Pricing, How much Life Insurance should be covered, and Life Insurance Cost Comparisons.
  7. Fire Insurance: Meaning - Principles, Elements of Contract - Classification of Fire Insurance Policies - Policy Conditions - Determination of Premium - Fire Claims.
  8. Marine Insurance: Meaning - Subject Matter - Importance - Marine Insurance Contract - Types of Marine Policies - Policy conditions - Marine Losses - Claims and their Settlement.
  9. Other Insurance: Health, Automobile, Social Insurance, and other Miscellaneous Insurance Policies.
  10. Insurance Practices in Bangladesh: Insurance Acts and Regulations of Bangladesh, Organization and Functions of Jiban Bima Corporation and Sadharan Bima Corporation, Role of Private Insurance Companies, and Problems & Prospects of Insurance Business in Bangladesh.

Basic Texts:

  1. Harrington Niehaus – Risk Management & Insurance (2nd Edition)
  2. George E. Rejda – Principles of Risk Management and Insurance (10th Edition)
  3. M. N. Mishra – Insurance Principles and Practices (22nd Edition)

Reference Books:

  1. Azizul Huq Chaudhuri – Risk and Insurance (2nd Edition)
  2. Shahiduddin Ahmed – Insurance Business in Bangladesh: A Study of the Pattern, Problems & Prospects
  3. Publications of Insurance Development and Regulatory Authority (IDRA)
  4. Publications of Government of Bangladesh regarding laws relevant to insurance, rules & regulations issued from time to time.
  5. Publications of Jiban Bima Corporation & Sadharan Bima Corporation.

Course Code: BCC 207

Course Title: Business Ethics

Marks: 100 Credit: 3 hours (Written: 75, Internal Evaluation: 20, Viva: 05)

Course Objectives:

The course aims at understanding business ethics, its application, and management. To achieve the objectives, the course will highlight the theories of business ethics. The course will also elaborately explain the roles and responsibilities as well as rights and privileges of different stakeholders in the context of business ethics.

Course Contents:

  1. Introduction to Business Ethics: Definition, Importance, Globalization – a key context of business ethics? Sustainability – a key goal for business ethics?
  2. Theories of Business Ethics: Normative ethical theories – Individual vs. Institutional morality, Questioning vs. accepting capitalism, Justifying vs. applying moral norms, Traditional ethical theories – Consequentialist Theories: Egoism, Utilitarianism, Non-consequentialist Theories: Ethics of duties, Ethics of rights and justice, Limits of Traditional Theories, Contemporary ethical theories – Virtue ethics, Feminist ethics, Discourse ethics, Postmodern ethics.
  3. Making Decisions in Business Ethics: Ethical decision, Models of ethical decision-making, Individual influence on ethical decision-making, Situational influence on decision-making.
  4. Managing Business Ethics: Business ethics management, Setting standards of ethical behavior: designing and implementing codes of ethics, Managing stakeholder relations, Assessing ethical performance, Organizing for business ethics management.
  5. Shareholders and Business Ethics: Introduction: reassessing the importance of shareholders as stakeholders, Shareholders as stakeholders: understanding corporate governance, Ethical issues in corporate governance: Executive accountability and control, Executive remuneration, Ethical aspects of mergers and acquisitions, The role of financial markets and insider trading, The role of accountants, Reforming governance.
  6. Employees and Business Ethics: Employees as stakeholders, Ethical issues in the firm-employee relation, Employing people worldwide: the ethical challenges of globalization.
  7. Consumers and Business Ethics: Consumers as stakeholders, Ethical issues, marketing, and the consumer, Globalization and consumers: the ethical challenges of the global marketplace, Consumers and corporate citizenship: consumer sovereignty and the politics of purchasing, Consumer sovereignty, Ethical consumption.
  8. Suppliers, Competitors and Business Ethics: Suppliers and competitors as stakeholders, Ethical Issues and suppliers, Ethical issues and competitors, Globalization, suppliers, and competitors: the ethical challenges of global business networks, The corporate citizen in the business community: ethical sourcing and fair trade.
  9. Civil Society and Business Ethics: Introduction: what is civil society? Civil society organizations as stakeholders, Ethical issues and CSOs, Globalization and civil society organizations, Corporate citizenship and civil society: charity, collaboration, or regulation? Civil society, business, and sustainability.
  10. Government, Regulations and Business Ethics: Government as a stakeholder, Ethical issues in the relation between business and government, Globalization and business-government relations, Corporate citizenship and regulation: business as a key player in the regulatory game.

Course Code: BCC 208

Course Title: Entrepreneurship Development

Marks: 100 Credit: 3 hours (Written: 75, Internal Evaluation: 20, Viva: 05)

Course Objectives:

Entrepreneurship skills accelerate economic growth by transforming a population burden into a demographic dividend. The main motto of offering this course is to inspire students to explore their potential to become entrepreneurs and to equip them with guiding principles to become effective entrepreneurs. In order to attain learning outcomes, the course has been designed considering the basic principles of entrepreneurship, the state of global environmental forces, and the dynamics of the Bangladeshi entrepreneurial environment. It covers knowledge and skills about identifying new business ideas, assessing the feasibility of those ideas, preparing a business plan, starting new ventures, conducting managerial operations, maintaining growth, and facing emerging business challenges effectively and efficiently.

Course Contents:

  1. Introduction to Entrepreneurship: What is entrepreneurship? Why become an entrepreneur? Characteristics of successful entrepreneurs; Importance of entrepreneurship; The entrepreneurial process; Approaches to entrepreneurship development; What is corporate entrepreneurship? The nature of corporate entrepreneurship; The need for corporate entrepreneuring; Corporate venturing obstacles; Conceptualizing corporate entrepreneurship strategy. (Case Study: 1.1 - Basic Text: Bruce R. Barringer, 4th Edition)
  2. Recognizing Opportunities and Generating Ideas: Identifying and recognizing opportunities; Finding gaps in the marketplace; Techniques for generating ideas; Encouraging and protecting new ideas. (Case Study: 2.2 - Basic Text: Bruce R. Barringer)
  3. Feasibility Analysis: Product/service feasibility analysis; Industry/target market feasibility analysis; Organizational feasibility analysis; Financial feasibility analysis. (Case Study: 3.2 - Basic Text: Bruce R. Barringer)
  4. Writing a Business Plan: Reasons for writing a business plan; Who reads the business plan? Guidelines for writing a business plan; Outline of the business plan; Presenting the business plan to investors. (Case Study: 4.1 - Basic Text: Bruce R. Barringer)
  5. Developing an Effective Business Model: The importance and diversity of business models; How business models emerge; Potential fatal flaws of business models; Components of an effective business model. (Case Study: 5.1 - Basic Text: Bruce R. Barringer)
  6. Entrepreneurship Development through Small Business: Modern small business enterprises; Setting up a small business enterprise; Strategic management in small business; Family business. (Case Study: 6.1 - Basic Text: Bruce R. Barringer)
  7. Ethical, Legal and Financing Issues: Initial ethical and legal issues facing a new firm; Obtaining business licenses and permits; The importance of intellectual property; Patents; Trademarks; Copyrights; Trade secrets; Financial objectives of a firm; The process of financial management. (Case Study: 7.2 & 12.2 - Basic Text: Bruce R. Barringer)
  8. Building a New Venture Team and Getting Funding: Creating a new venture team; The role of professional advisers; The importance of getting funding for a new venture; Sources of funding. (Case Study: 9.1 & 10.1 - Basic Text: Bruce R. Barringer)
  9. Managing and Growing an Entrepreneurial Firm: Preparing for growth; Reasons for growth; Managing growth; Challenges of growth; Internal growth strategies; External growth strategies. (Case Study: 14.1 - Basic Text: Bruce R. Barringer)
  10. Contemporary Issues in Entrepreneurship: Women entrepreneurship; Social entrepreneurship; Challenges of entrepreneurship development in Bangladesh; Role of government and non-government institutions in entrepreneurship development in Bangladesh; Role of Information and Communication Technology (ICT) in entrepreneurship development.

Assignments: Two (on any of the contents)

Basic Texts:

  1. Bruce R. Barringer and R. Duane Ireland – Entrepreneurship: Successfully Launching New Ventures (4th Edition)

Reference Books:

  1. Donald F. Kuratko – Introduction to Entrepreneurship (8th Edition)
  2. Poornima M. Charantimath – Entrepreneurship Development and Small Business Enterprises (1st Edition)
  3. Tahlil Azim – Entrepreneurship Development

Course Code: BCC 209

Course Title: Organizational Behavior

Marks: 100 Credit: 3 hours (Written: 75, Internal Evaluation: 20, Viva: 05)

Course Description:

This course focuses on the foundation of individual and group behavior at work. Special emphasis is given on the psychological roots, causes, and consequences of human behavior in organizations. Major topics include dynamics of individual and group behavior, motivation, leadership, power and politics, conflict management, and organizational change and stress management.

Course Objectives:

Upon successful completion of the course, students will be able to:

  1. Apply organizational behavior concepts to individual work experiences.
  2. Recognize how different personalities and other issues with different experiences may perceive and react to situations in different ways.
  3. Examine own behavior and beliefs about managerial behavior and to contrast and integrate these ideas with the theories and observations of others.
  4. Work more effectively in a group and team environment.
  5. Learn how organizational power and politics have been exercised.
  6. Understand how organizational conflicts and negotiation have to be managed.
  7. Learn how organizational change and stress have to be managed.

Course Contents:

  1. Introduction: Meaning and Concepts of Organizational Behavior (OB); Contemporary Issues in OB; Disciplines that Contribute to the OB Field; Challenges and Opportunities for OB; OB Model; Global Implications.
  2. Diversity in Organizations: Diversity; Discrimination; Biographical Characteristics; Ability; Implementing Diversity Management Strategies; Global Implications.
  3. Attitudes and Job Satisfaction: Attitudes; Main components of Attitudes; Major Job Attitudes; Job Satisfaction; Measuring Job Satisfaction; Global Implications.
  4. Emotions and Moods: What are Emotions and Moods; Emotional Labor; Affective Events Theory; Emotional Intelligence; OB Applications of Emotions and Moods; Global Implications.
  5. Personality and Values: What is Personality; The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator; The Big Five Personality Model; Values; The Importance of Values; Linking an Individual's Personality and Values to the workplace; Global implications.
  6. Perception and Individual Decision Making: What is Perception? Person Perception: Making Judgments About Others; The link Between Perception and Individual Decision Making; Decision Making in Organizations; Influences on Decision Making: Individual Differences and Organizational Constraints; Global Implications.
  7. Motivation: From Concepts to Application: Motivating by Job Design; Employee Involvement; Other Concurrent Methods to Motivate Employees.
  8. Foundations of Group Behavior and Understanding Work Teams: Defining and Classifying Groups, Stages of Group Development, Group Properties, Group Decision Making; Differences Between Groups and Teams; Types of Teams; Creating Effective Teams; Turning Individuals into Team Players, Global implications; Relevant Case Analysis.
  9. Leadership: Why Leadership; Charismatic Leadership and Transformational Leadership; Authentic Leadership; Mentoring; Challenges to the Leadership Construct; Creating Effective Leaders; Relevant Case Analysis.
  10. Power and Politics: Definition of Power; Contrasting Leadership and Power; Bases of Power; Dependency the key to Power; Power Tactics; Politics, Causes and Consequences of Political Behavior; Global Implications; Relevant Case Analysis.
  11. Conflict and Negotiations: Definition of Conflict; Transitions in Conflict Thought; Conflict Process; Negotiation; Negotiation Process; Global Implications; Relevant Case Analysis.
  12. Organizational Change and Stress Management: Change; Resistance to Change; Approaches to Managing Organizational Change; Creating a Culture for Change; What is Stress; Source of Stress; Consequences of Stress; Managing Stress; Relevant Case Analysis.

Basic Texts:

  1. S.P. Robbins & Others – Organizational Behavior (Latest Indian Edition)

Reference Books:

  1. Keith Davis and John Newstrom – Human Behavior at Work: Organizational Behavior
  2. Fred Luthans – Organizational Behavior
  3. J.M. Ivancevich, R. Konopaske, and M.T. Matteson – Organizational Behavior and Management (McGraw Hill, Current Edition)
  4. A.A. Arif and M.A. Taher – Organizational Behavior
  5. Joseph Reitz – Behavior in Organizations

Course Code: BCC 210

Course Title: Bangladesh Studies and Economic Development

Marks: 100 Credit: 3 hours (Written: 75, Internal Evaluation: 20, Viva: 05)

Course Objectives:

The general objective of this course is to understand and evaluate Bangladesh as an emerging country along with its economic growth and development. There are a number of specific objectives of starting this course in the BBA program, such as:

  1. To have a clear idea about the history of Bangladesh as an emerging country.
  2. To investigate the diverse constituents of the business environment of Bangladesh.
  3. To critically evaluate fundamental economic development theories and models.
  4. To understand the development strategies and policies for enhancing economic progress.

Course Contents:

PART A: Bangladesh Studies

  1. History of Bangladesh: People of Bangladesh, origin and Anthropological identity; Territories of Bangladesh in Ancient Time; Muslim Rule and British Colonial Rule in Indian Sub-Continent; The Pakistan Movement and the Birth of Pakistan; Language Movement, 1948-52; Juktafront Elections, 1954 and Six Point Program, 1966; Mass Upsurge of 1969 and Elections, 1970; and Birth of Bangladesh.
  2. Physical and Natural Resources of Bangladesh: Physical Features of Bangladesh: Location, Climate and major Physiographic Units; Surface Water Inflow and River Systems of Bangladesh; Pattern of Agriculture and types of Forest in Bangladesh; Mineral Resources, Energy, Transportation and Communication, Industrial Setup in Bangladesh; Distribution of Population in Bangladesh.
  3. Political, Administrative and Legal Structure of Bangladesh: Forms of Government and State Mechanism (Executive, Legislatures, Judiciary) of Bangladesh; Constitutions of Bangladesh and its Amendments; Administrative Structure of Bangladesh; Political Parties of Bangladesh; Different Political Regimes of Bangladesh.
  4. Socio-culture of Bangladesh: Social Structure and Social Stratification in Bangladesh; Population Migration from Rural to Urban areas in Bangladesh; Ethnic Minorities in Bangladesh; Social and Cultural Problems of Bangladesh.

PART B: Economic Development

  1. Concepts and Approaches of Economics of Development: Meaning of Economic Development; Economic Development vs Economic Growth; Measurement of Economic Development; Alternative Measures of Economic Development, PQLI and HDI; Determinants of Development; Challenges to economic Development; Factors of Economic Growth.
  2. Development Theories and Growth Models: An overview of the General Theories of Development; Theories of Economic Development: the Classical Theory, the Schumpeterian Theory, Rostow's Stages of Economic Growth, the Marxian Theory, Big-push theory, Lewis' Theory; Growth Models: The Solow Model, The Harrod-Domar Model, The golden rule of accumulation.
  3. Fundamental Issues and Strategies of Economic Development of Bangladesh: Fundamental Issues: Poverty, Balance of Payment, NGO Activities, Micro Credit Programs, Income Inequality, Unemployment, Inflation, Blue economy, Financial Administration; Strategies: Five year plans, SDG, Technology Transfer, and Women Empowerment.
  4. Local and International Measures for Economic Development: Local Measures: Capital formation, Role of agriculture and industry, Fiscal policy and monetary policy, Deficit financing as an investment, population growth, Entrepreneurship Development; International Measures: Role of foreign trade, Debt problem of LDCs, NIEO, Foreign capital and aid, Foreign Direct Investment (FDI), Role of MNCs.
  5. Comparative Analysis of Developed countries and Lessons for Bangladesh: Comparative analysis of USA, CANADA, UK, GERMANY, JAPAN, FRANCE and CHINA; lessons for the developing and less developing countries and Bangladesh.

Basic Texts:

  1. Prof. Sirajul Islam (Ed.) – Banglapedia: National Encyclopedia of Bangladesh (Latest Edition, 2006)
  2. Sirajul Islam – History of Bangladesh: Social and Cultural History, Political History, Economical History
  3. A.F. Salauddin Ahmed (Ed.) – Bangladesh National Cultural and Heritage: An Introductory Reader
  4. M. L. Jhingan – The Economics of Development and Planning (Revised edition)
  5. Debraj Ray – Development Economics (Princeton University Press, 1998)

Reference Books:

  1. Michael P. Todaro – Economic Development in the Third World (7th Edition, Longman)
  2. G.M. Meier – Leading Issues in Economic Development (7th Edition)
  3. M. Gillis et al. – Economics of Development (2nd Edition, W.W. Norton, 1985)
  4. S. Ghatak – An Introduction to Development Economics (2nd Edition, Allen and Unwin, 1986)
  5. R. L. Meier – Development Planning (McGraw Hill, 1995)
  6. M. P. Todaro – Development planning: Models and Methods (Oxford University Press, 1971)
  7. Akhtar Hossain – The Economics of Development and Planning (30th Edition, Konark, New Delhi)
  8. CPD (Centre for Policy Dialogue) – Various Publications
