To help you prepare quickly, here is a concise composite of the core concepts from Part B, Chapter 1: Concepts and Approaches of Economics of Development, based on past exam questions.

Economic Growth vs. Economic Development

  • Economic Growth: A narrow, quantitative concept referring to a sustained increase in a country’s real national output or per capita income (GDP/GNI). It focuses on production capacity and macroeconomic indicators.
  • Economic Development: A broad, multi-dimensional process involving quantitative growth alongside qualitative changes in socio-economic structures. It requires structural shifts (e.g., from agriculture to industry), reduction in poverty, inequality, and unemployment, and improvements in overall human well-being.

Measuring Economic Development

Development is measured using composite indices that go beyond monetary metrics:

  • Human Development Index (HDI): The primary metric measuring health, knowledge, and standard of living.
  • Other Metrics: Physical Quality of Life Index (PQLI), Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI), and the Gini Coefficient (to measure income inequality).

The Human Development Index (HDI) Calculation

The HDI evaluates three dimensions using specific indicators:

  1. Health: Measured by Life Expectancy at Birth.
  2. Knowledge: Measured by Mean Years of Schooling (for adults 25+) and Expected Years of Schooling (for children).
  3. Standard of Living: Measured by GNI per capita adjusted for Purchasing Power Parity (PPP).

Methodology: Raw values are normalized into indices between 0 and 1. The final HDI is the Geometric Mean of these three indices ().

Modern Economic Growth (MEG) and Determinants

  • MEG (Simon Kuznets): Characterised by high rates of per capita product and population growth, rapid structural transformation (shifting from agriculture to industry/services), and social/ideological modernization.
  • Determinants of Growth:
    • Economic Factors: Capital formation, natural resources, technological progress, and human capital (labor supply/skills).
    • Non-Economic Factors: Political stability, rule of law, administrative competence, and favorable social values.

Obstacles to Development in Bangladesh

Major constraints include the Vicious Circle of Poverty (low income low savings low investment), infrastructure and energy bottlenecks, a narrow export basket (over-reliance on RMG), financial sector instability, skill mismatches in the labor force, and high vulnerability to climate change.

Ready to move on to Part B, Chapter 2: Development Theories and Growth Models?

Here is the concise composite of the theories and models from Part B, Chapter 2: Development Theories and Growth Models, based on the examination bank.

1. Schumpeter’s Theory of Innovation

  • Core Driver: Development is a spontaneous, discontinuous change driven by Innovation.
  • The Entrepreneur: A visionary who introduces "new combinations" (new products, methods, markets, sources, or organizations).
  • Mechanism: Financed by bank credit rather than savings.
  • Creative Destruction: A cyclical process where successful innovation triggers a boom, followed by a recession where obsolete firms are liquidated to establish a higher equilibrium.

2. The Big-Push Theory (Paul Rosenstein-Rodan)

  • Main Thesis: Piecemeal investment fails; a "minimum quantum" of synchronized investment is required to launch an economy out of stagnation.
  • Three Indivisibilities:
    1. Production (SOC): Infrastructure like power/transport requires massive, lump-sum initial capital.
    2. Demand: Complementary industries must start together so workers in one industry can buy goods from another.
    3. Savings: Low incomes result in low savings; a massive push is needed to lift incomes rapidly.
  • Bangladesh Context: Applied through Mega-Projects like the Padma Bridge and Nuclear Power plants to lower transaction costs.

3. Balanced vs. Unbalanced Growth

  • Balanced Growth: Simultaneous, synchronized investment across all sectors to create mutual demand.
  • Unbalanced Growth (Hirschman): Strategic concentration in "leading sectors" with high Linkage Effects.
    • Backward Linkages: Demand for inputs (e.g., garments requiring textiles).
    • Forward Linkages: Output utilized by others (e.g., steel for construction).
  • Application: Bangladesh follows the Unbalanced Model, prioritizing the RMG sector and Social Overhead Capital (SOC) due to resource scarcity.

4. Dependency Theory

  • Core-Periphery: The world is divided into the developed Core and the underdeveloped Periphery.
  • Unequal Exchange: Periphery nations export low-value raw materials and cheap labor, while the Core exports high-value goods, leading to a net transfer of value to the Core.
  • Bangladesh: Primarily performs low-value "cut-make-trim" operations in RMG while Core nations capture the high-value design and branding profits.

5. Rostow’s Stages of Economic Growth

All societies pass through five stages:

  1. Traditional Society: Subsistence agriculture.
  2. Preconditions for Take-off: Infrastructure and entrepreneurship emerge.
  3. Take-off: Rapid growth in leading manufacturing sectors.
  4. Drive to Maturity: Modern technology spreads across the economy.
  5. High Mass Consumption: Durable goods and services dominate.
  • Bangladesh Status: Passing through Late Take-off and transitioning to Drive to Maturity.

6. The Solow Growth Model

  • Focus: Explains long-run growth via capital accumulation, labor growth, and technological progress.
  • Steady State: The equilibrium where actual investment () equals "break-even" investment (), stopping changes in capital per worker.
  • Key Findings: Higher savings increases the level of income but only Technological Progress generates sustained long-run per-capita growth.

7. Other Critical Models

  • Harrod-Domar: Growth () is determined by the savings rate () divided by the capital-output ratio (). It highlights the "savings gap" in LDCs.
  • Nelson’s Low-Level Equilibrium Trap: Stagnation occurs because small income gains are cancelled out by rapid population growth.
  • Marxian Theory: Class struggle between the Bourgeoisie (owners) and Proletariat (workers). Capitalists accumulate via Surplus Value (unpaid labor), but the falling rate of profit eventually leads to the downfall of capitalism.

Ready to move on to Part B, Chapter 3: Fundamental Issues and Strategies?

Here is the composite of the core topics from Part B, Chapter 3: Fundamental Issues and Strategies, focusing on the strategies Bangladesh uses to address structural development challenges.

1. Sustainable Development and the SDGs

  • Sustainable Development: Development that meets present needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs. It rests on the "Triple Bottom Line": economic, social, and environmental sustainability.
  • SDGs (2030 Agenda): A set of 17 interconnected global goals (e.g., No Poverty, Quality Education, Climate Action) designed to end poverty and protect the planet. Bangladesh has localized these into national plans like the Delta Plan 2100.

2. The Kuznets Hypothesis (Inequality vs. Development)

  • The Inverted-U Curve: Inequality initially rises during early industrialization as labor shifts to high-wage urban sectors and capital concentrates.
  • The Turning Point: Eventually, inequality falls due to the universalization of education, labor market integration, progressive taxation, and social safety nets.

3. Poverty Alleviation Strategies

  • Dimensions: Absolute poverty (lack of basic survival needs) vs. Relative poverty (income inequality relative to the average).
  • Government SSNPs: Targeted food security (VGD/VGF), employment generation (EGPP), and cash transfers for the elderly or destitute.
  • NGO Initiatives: Models like BRAC's Ultra-Poor Graduation (asset transfer + training) and Grameen Bank's Microcredit (collateral-free loans for self-employment) are globally recognized for empowering the poor and women.

4. Strategic Planning: Vision 2021 & 8th Five-Year Plan

  • Vision 2021: Aimed to transform Bangladesh into a middle-income, "Digital Bangladesh" by its 50th anniversary through growth and ICT modernization.
  • 8th Five-Year Plan (FY2021–2025): Focuses on "Promoting Prosperity and Fostering Inclusiveness". It targets 8%–8.5% GDP growth, poverty reduction to 15.6%, and preparedness for LDC graduation by diversifying exports.

5. Breaking the Vicious Circle of Poverty

  • The Trap: Low income Low savings/demand Low investment Low productivity Low income.
  • The Solution: Requires a "Big Push" of coordinated, large-scale investment in Social Overhead Capital (SOC) to jumpstart demand and production.

6. Technology Transfer & the "Appropriate Package"

  • Challenges: LDCs face high costs, technological dependency, and "inappropriate" capital-intensive bias from developed nations that can worsen unemployment.
  • Appropriate Package: For Bangladesh, this must be labor-intensive, adaptable "intermediate" technology that is "unbundled" (avoiding turnkey contracts) to ensure local skill-building and maintenance capacity.

7. The Blue Economy

  • Definition: Sustainable use of ocean resources for growth and jobs while preserving ecosystem health.
  • Potential: Following the resolution of maritime disputes, Bangladesh can leverage its 118,813 sq km of sea for deep-sea fishing, seaweed culture, shipbuilding, and eco-tourism to enhance food security and poverty alleviation.

8. Economic Planning and Balance of Payments (BOP)

  • Planning Systems: Bangladesh uses a Mixed Economy (composite) model, combining imperative planning for the public sector with indicative policies for the private sector.
  • BOP Structure: Tracks transactions via the Current Account (Trade, Services, and Remittances), Capital Account, and Financial Account (FDI and Loans).

Ready to move on to Part B, Chapter 4: Local and International Measures?

Here is the concise composite for Part B, Chapter 4: Local and International Measures for Economic Development, focusing on strategies and external factors.

1. Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs)

  • Role: Acts as the economy's backbone, contributing 20–25% of GDP and nearly 80–89% of industrial employment.
  • Benefits: High labor intensity, low capital requirements, poverty alleviation, and utilization of local raw materials like jute, cane, and leather.
  • Main Industries: Handloom (Jamdani), pottery, bamboo/cane crafts, and food processing.

2. Foreign Aid and Capital Formation

  • Foreign Aid Drawbacks: While it fills the savings gap, it creates a "Dependency Syndrome," leading to a "Debt Trap" (loans over grants) and "Tied Aid" (mandating purchases from donor nations at inflated prices).
  • Capital Formation: The process of adding to physical capital (machinery, infrastructure).
    • Domestic Sources: Voluntary savings, taxation, and public enterprise profits.
    • Foreign Sources: FDI, remittances, and concessional loans.

3. Deficit Financing

  • Role: A deliberate strategy where spending exceeds revenue to fund mega-projects (e.g., Padma Bridge) and mobilize idle resources.
  • Risks: Can cause "Demand-Pull Inflation" if the money supply outpaces real output, and a "Crowding-Out Effect" where government borrowing depletes credit for the private sector.
  • Safe Limit: Generally maintained at or below 5% of GDP to preserve macroeconomic stability.

4. FDI and Multinational Corporations (MNCs)

  • Assistance Debate:
    • Positive: MNCs bring FDI, "Technology Spillovers" (modern know-how), and integrate the host country into global value chains.
    • Negative: "Profit Repatriation" (sending earnings back to home countries), "Crowding-Out" local infant industries, and potential exploitation of cheap labor or lax environmental laws.

5. International Trade and Economic Integration

  • Free Trade: Rooted in "Comparative Advantage" (specializing in low-opportunity-cost goods).
  • Critique: The "Prebisch-Singer Hypothesis" warns that the terms of trade for primary/low-value exports (like RMG) deteriorate over time against manufactured goods.
  • Integration: Involves reducing trade barriers (e.g., SAFTA). Benefits include "Economies of Scale" and "Trade Creation," but risks include "Trade Diversion" (shifting trade to a higher-cost member nation).

6. Fiscal and Monetary Policy

  • Fiscal Policy: Uses the Annual Development Program (ADP) for infrastructure and Social Safety Net Programs (SSNPs) for equity.
  • Monetary Policy: Managed by Bangladesh Bank to control inflation and exchange rates using tools like the Repo Rate, Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR), and Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR).

7. Theory of Demographic Transition

  • Stages: Moves from high birth/death rates to low birth/death rates.
  • Bangladesh's Status: Firmly in Stage 3 (Late Expanding), characterized by falling birth rates and low death rates.
  • Demographic Dividend: A window where the working-age population (15–64) outnumbers dependents, offering a prime opportunity for growth.

Ready to conclude with Part B, Chapter 5: Comparative Analysis and Lessons for Bangladesh?

To wrap up your preparation, here is the concise composite for Part B, Chapter 5: Comparative Analysis and Lessons for Bangladesh, which synthesises the structural differences between nations and the roadmap for Bangladesh's graduation.

1. Characteristics of a "Developed" Country

A country is classified as developed when it exhibits high economic performance and an advanced quality of life. Key determinants include:

  • Income & Wealth: A sustained High Gross National Income (GNI) per capita that meets high-income thresholds.
  • Human Development: A High Human Development Index (HDI) score (typically >0.800), driven by near-universal literacy and premium healthcare access.
  • Economic Structure: An economy dominated by tertiary (services) and quaternary (knowledge/research) sectors with high levels of automation.
  • Institutional Maturity: Robust rule of law, political stability, and minimal corruption.

2. Comparative Analysis: Developed vs. Developing

Feature Developed Countries Developing Countries
Economic Base Services, high-tech manufacturing, and innovation. Primary agriculture or low-value manufacturing (e.g., RMG).
Capital High rates of domestic savings and internal capital formation. Severe savings-investment gap; high reliance on foreign capital and aid.
Technology Leading producers of technology with massive R&D investment. Consumers and importers of technology; limited native innovation.
Employment Primarily formal sector; low underemployment. Large informal economy; high disguised unemployment.

3. Strategic Lessons for Bangladesh

To transition successfully past the middle-income threshold, Bangladesh must adopt several core strategies used by advanced economies:

  • Prioritise Human Capital: Shift the national focus from providing "cheap labor" to developing a highly skilled, technical, and STEM-focused workforce.
  • Industrial Diversification: Reduce the "concentration risk" of over-reliance on the RMG sector by aggressively incentivizing pharmaceuticals, precision electronics, and IT services.
  • Institutional Strengthening: Improve the "Ease of Doing Business" by ensuring judicial efficiency in contract enforcement and streamlining bureaucratic processes.
  • Foster Native Innovation: Build structured links between academic research and the private sector to promote localized industrial patents rather than just adopting foreign hardware.
  • Infrastructure Modernization: Scale up Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) to build smart grids and deep-sea ports to lower transaction costs.

Here is the concise composite of core concepts from Part A, Chapter 2: Physical and Natural Resources of Bangladesh, based on past exam questions.

1. Key Mineral Resources

Bangladesh possesses significant non-renewable resources essential for its industrial and energy sectors:

  • Natural Gas: The primary commercial fuel, powering over half of national electricity and serving as the vital raw material for urea fertilizer.
  • Coal: High-grade bituminous coal used for thermal power (e.g., Barapukuria) and brick manufacturing.
  • Hard Rock (Granite): Extracted from Maddhapara for mega-infrastructure, railway ballast, and flood-control embankments.
  • Limestone: A foundational input for the domestic cement and lime industries.
  • Others: Silica sand (glass), white clay (ceramics), and mineral sands (heavy minerals like zircon).

2. Strategic Importance of Resources

Optimal utilization of these resources acts as a developmental catalyst by:

  • Energy Security: Reducing reliance on expensive imported LNG and petroleum.
  • Foreign Exchange Savings: Replacing imports with domestic inputs to stabilize the Balance of Payments.
  • Agricultural Productivity: Providing gas for domestic fertilizer production, which ensures national food security.

3. Population Dynamics

  • Features: Extremely high density (>1,200 people/sq km), rapid urbanization, and a "Demographic Dividend" where the working-age population (15–64) outnumbers dependents.
  • Economic Impact of Overpopulation: High density puts severe pressure on arable land (conversion to housing), stretches public utilities (health/education) beyond capacity, and leads to structural unemployment.

4. Environmental Costs of Development

Rapid growth has created significant ecological trade-offs:

  • Pollution: Discharge of untreated industrial effluents has turned rivers like the Buriganga into "dead zones".
  • Resource Depletion: Intensive irrigation and industrial washing (textiles) have caused alarming drops in groundwater tables.
  • Degradation: Unregulated brick kilns strip fertile topsoil and worsen urban air quality.

5. The Blue Economy

Following the resolution of maritime disputes with India and Myanmar, Bangladesh acquired 118,813 sq km of maritime territory. This "Blue Economy" offers massive potential for deep-sea fishing, maritime transport, shipbuilding, and eco-tourism.

Ready to move on to Part A, Chapter 3: Political, Administrative and Legal Structure?

Here is the composite of the core concepts regarding the Political, Administrative and Legal Structure of Bangladesh, focusing on the 1972 Constitution and the mechanics of the state.

1. The 1972 Constitution of Bangladesh

The Constitution is the supreme law of the land, adopted on November 4, 1972. The original document is highly progressive and characterized by:

  • Four Fundamental Principles: Article 8 anchors the state on Nationalism (Bengali culture/identity), Socialism (economic justice), Democracy (human rights/participation), and Secularism (elimination of communalism and religious discrimination).
  • Structure of Government: It established a Parliamentary (Westminster-style) system where the Prime Minister holds real executive power and is accountable to the legislature.
  • Unitary State: Unlike a federal system, all administrative and legislative power flows from a single central government in Dhaka.
  • Key Legal Protections: It provides for an Independent Judiciary (Supreme Court) and guarantees Enforceable Fundamental Rights, allowing citizens to move the court if their rights are violated.

2. The Legislative Branch (Jatiya Sangsad)

The Jatiya Sangsad is the unicameral (single-chamber) supreme lawmaking organ of the state.

  • Composition: It consists of 350 seats.
    • 300 members are directly elected from territorial constituencies.
    • 50 seats are exclusively reserved for women, allocated proportionally to political parties based on their share of elected seats.
  • Powers: Its primary functions include enacting laws, exercising "power of the purse" (no tax or expenditure can occur without its approval), and holding the executive cabinet accountable through standing committees.

3. Improving Public Administration

The current administrative system is often critiqued for its colonial-bureaucratic roots. Key proposed reforms for efficiency include:

  • Digitalization (Smart Governance): Transitioning to paperless, AI-assisted workflows to reduce "red tape" and institutional corruption.
  • Merit-Based System: Shifting from seniority-based promotions to a strict Performance Appraisal System (PAS) to ensure competent leadership.
  • Decentralization: Transferring fiscal and planning autonomy from the central secretariats to local government tiers (Upazila and Zila Parishads) to better address grassroots needs.
  • Accountability: Strengthening oversight bodies like the Anti-Corruption Commission (ACC) and implementing the Office of the Ombudsman as mandated by Article 77 of the Constitution.

Ready to move on to Part A, Chapter 4: Socio-culture of Bangladesh?

Here is the concise composite for Part A, Chapter 4: Socio-culture of Bangladesh, focusing on social structures, cultural challenges, and migration patterns.

1. Social Problems in Bangladesh

The socio-economic landscape is challenged by several systemic issues:

  • Overpopulation and Density: Extreme pressure on limited land, public healthcare, and educational infrastructure.
  • Poverty and Inequality: Despite progress, many remain vulnerable to economic shocks, and a widening wealth gap threatens social cohesion.
  • Gender Vulnerabilities: Systemic barriers include wage disparity, restricted leadership access, and persistent issues like child marriage and dowry.
  • Institutional Quality: Public education often fails to meet market demands, leading to "educated unemployment" due to skill mismatches.
  • Healthcare Access: High out-of-pocket expenses frequently drive low-income families back into poverty during medical emergencies.

2. Cultural Problems and Transitions

  • Superstition: Entrenched dogmatic beliefs in marginalized pockets sometimes impede scientific and medical progression.
  • Cultural Aggression: Rapid influx of global digital media can create identity conflicts or generational gaps among youth.
  • Erosion of Value Systems: Urbanization is breaking down traditional rural joint family structures, leaving the elderly without communal safety nets.
  • Moral Decay: Increased commercialization is linked to rising drug dependency and systemic corruption, eroding civic trust.

3. Rural-Urban Migration

This demographic shift is driven by structural imbalances between villages and cities:

  • The "Push" (Rural Realities): Natural disasters (riverbank erosion, salinity), chronic landlessness, and a lack of quality social infrastructure expel people from villages.
  • The "Pull" (Urban Attractions): Concentration of industries (RMG), higher and more reliable daily wages, and better access to civic amenities attract migrants.
  • Consequences:
    • Positive: Provides a steady supply of low-cost labor for industry and generates internal remittance flows that stabilize rural consumption.
    • Negative: Leads to overcrowded urban slums (bastees), severe strain on municipal transport and drainage, and an agricultural "brain drain" as active workers leave villages.
