Principal Industries and Causes of Industrial Backwardness in Bangladesh

Principal Industries in Bangladesh

Bangladesh has transitioned from an agrarian economy to an increasingly industrialized one. The principal industries driving the country’s manufacturing sector include:

  • Ready-Made Garments (RMG) & Textiles: The backbone of Bangladesh's export sector, accounting for over 80% of total export earnings and employing millions of workers, predominantly women.
  • Pharmaceuticals: A highly sophisticated sector meeting nearly 98% of domestic demand and exporting finished formulations to over 150 countries.
  • Jute and Jute Goods: Traditionally known as the "Golden Fiber," this sector produces raw jute, jute yarn, twine, sacks, and diversified eco-friendly lifestyle products.
  • Leather and Leather Goods: A major export-oriented industry leveraging the country’s abundant livestock supply to produce leather footwear, bags, and finished leather.
  • Shipbuilding: An emerging heavy industry focusing on small to medium-sized ocean-going vessels, cargo ships, and ferries for both domestic and international markets.
  • Agro-processing: Focuses on processing food grains, fruits, vegetables, tea, and dairy products to serve both local urban centers and expatriate markets.
  • Light Engineering: Produces spare parts, electronic accessories, agricultural machinery, and bicycles.
  • Heavy Infrastructure Industries: Includes crucial domestic production setups such as Cement, Fertilizer (Urea), and Steel Re-rolling mills.
Causes of Industrial Backwardness in Bangladesh

Despite significant growth in specific sectors like RMG, Bangladesh experiences industrial backwardness and a lack of manufacturing diversification due to several structural challenges:

  1. Infrastructural Deficiencies:
    • Power and Energy Crisis: Chronic shortages or erratic supply of electricity and natural gas disrupt production schedules and increase operating costs.
    • Transportation Bottlenecks: Inadequate port facilities (e.g., congestion at Chittagong Port), poorly maintained highways, and lack of deep-sea connectivity delay the transit of raw materials and finished goods.
  2. Lack of Adequate Capital and Credit Access:
    • High interest rates on commercial loans and stringent collateral requirements discourage long-term industrial investment.
    • Capital markets (stock exchanges) remain volatile and underdeveloped, failing to act as reliable channels for raising long-term industrial equity.
  3. Technological Backwardness:
    • Most small and medium enterprises (SMEs) continue to rely on obsolete or low-productivity technologies due to a lack of research and development (R&D) investments.
    • Slow adoption of Automation, Industry 4.0 standards, and modern machinery keeps production yields lower than regional competitors.
  4. Shortage of Skilled Labor and Entrepreneurial Skills:
    • While raw labor is abundant, there is a severe deficit of specialized technical labor, engineers, and high-level managerial professionals.
    • The education system is poorly aligned with market-driven vocational skills, forcing heavy industries to depend on foreign experts.
  5. Bureaucratic Red Tape and Corruption:
    • Complex regulatory frameworks, delays in obtaining utility connections, environmental clearances, and trade licenses create a high cost of doing business.
    • Inconsistent tax regimes and customs harassment discourage both domestic entrepreneurs and Foreign Direct Investment (FDI).
  6. Narrow Export Basket and Market Concentration:
    • Over-reliance on the RMG sector creates structural vulnerability. Other potential sectors fail to scale up due to a lack of targeted policy support, fiscal incentives, and trade diplomacy.

(b)

Mineral Resources of Bangladesh and Environmental Impacts of Economic Development

Mineral Resources of Bangladesh

Bangladesh possesses modest but economically significant reserves of mineral resources concentrated primarily in its eastern and northwestern regions:

Mineral Resource Major Reserve Locations Primary Economic Use
Natural Gas Titas, Bibiyana, Kailashtila, Jalalabad Power generation, industrial fuel, fertilizer feedstock, household cooking.
Coal Barapukuria (Dinajpur), Khalashpeer, Phulbari Thermal power generation, brick manufacturing.
Limestone Jaipurhat, Takerghat (Sunamganj), Sitakunda Raw material for cement production and lime processing.
Hard Rock / Granite Maddhapara (Dinajpur) Construction of highways, bridges, railway ballast, and flood control dams.
Silica Sand Sylhet, Moulvibazar, Habiganj Raw material for glass manufacturing and ceramics.
White Clay (China Clay) Bijoypur (Netrokona), Gaibandha Ceramics, sanitary ware, and electrical insulators.
Mineral Sands Cox's Bazar beaches, Kutubdia Contains heavy minerals like Ilmenite, Rutile, and Zircon (potential industrial value).
Environmental Problems Arising with Economic Development

Rapid economic growth, industrialization, and urbanization in Bangladesh have significantly degraded natural ecosystems, presenting a major trade-off between growth and sustainability:

  1. Industrial Pollution and Water Contamination:
    • Effluents from textile dyers, tanneries, and chemical plants are frequently discharged untreated into major river systems (e.g., Buriganga, Shitalakshya, and Karnaphuli).
    • This has destroyed aquatic biodiversity, created "dead zones" in rivers, and contaminated the food chain via heavy metals.
  2. Air Quality Degradation:
    • Unregulated emissions from traditional brick kilns, fitness-lacking commercial vehicles, and construction dust have turned urban centers like Dhaka into some of the most polluted cities globally.
    • This drives severe respiratory illnesses, lowering human capital productivity.
  3. Deforestation and Land Degradation:
    • Industrial expansion, commercial aquaculture (shrimp farming), and infrastructural projects have led to massive deforestation in the Chittagong Hill Tracts and the encroachment of the Sundarbans mangrove forest.
    • Soil fertility is depleted by excessive use of chemical fertilizers to maintain food security for a growing population.
  4. Groundwater Depletion:
    • Massive extraction of groundwater for intensive agricultural irrigation (such as Boro rice cultivation) and heavy industrial use (RMG washing and dyeing units) has caused the water table to drop critically in urban and semi-arid zones (e.g., the Barind Tract).
  5. Solid and Electronic Waste Management Crisis:
    • Urban consumption generates vast amounts of municipal solid waste and non-biodegradable plastic pollution that clogs urban drainage systems, worsening artificial flooding.
    • The digital transformation has introduced a massive influx of hazardous electronic waste (e-waste) without standard recycling protocols.
  6. Ecosystem Disruption due to Mining:
    • Open-cast and underground extraction of sand, stone, and coal destroys local topsoil, induces artificial landslides, and leads to the leaching of acidic compounds into surrounding agricultural plains.
