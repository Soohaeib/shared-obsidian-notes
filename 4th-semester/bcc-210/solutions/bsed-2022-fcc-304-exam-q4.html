(a)

Vicious Circle of Poverty and Its Influence on Underdevelopment

The concept of the "Vicious Circle of Poverty," widely popularized by economist Ragnar Nurkse, describes a situation where a combination of circular forces tend to act and react upon one another in such a way as to keep a poor country in a persistent state of poverty. As Nurkse concisely stated: "A country is poor because it is poor."

The vicious circle operates on two distinct fronts: the supply side and the demand side.

Influence on the Economy and the Aggression of Underdevelopment

This interlocking network of economic forces heavily influences a developing economy like Bangladesh, preventing it from breaking free from the shackles of underdevelopment through several structural channels:

  • The Trap of Low Capital Formation: Because the vast majority of the population lives near subsistence levels, the marginal propensity to consume is exceptionally high. This leaves virtually no room for personal or institutional savings. Without savings, the domestic capital formation rate remains too depressed to finance modern infrastructure or technology-driven industries.
  • Market Size Constraints (Demand Deficiency): Low per capita income translates into low purchasing power. When aggregate demand for manufactured goods is small, local and foreign entrepreneurs lack the inducement to invest in large-scale modern plants, as they cannot achieve economies of scale.
  • Low Productivity and Hidden Unemployment: Capital deficiency forces the labor force to rely on rudimentary tools and outdated techniques, particularly in agriculture. This causes low marginal productivity of labor, giving rise to widespread disguised unemployment and keeping wages at subsistence levels.
  • The Burden of Population Pressure: In an underdeveloped state, any minor increase in national income is quickly absorbed by population growth and immediate consumption needs, rather than being reinvested. Consequently, the economy remains stuck in a low-level structural equilibrium, unable to generate the self-sustaining momentum required for economic takeoff.

Nelson's Theory of the Low-Level Equilibrium Trap

Richard R. Nelson’s theory of the Low-Level Equilibrium Trap (1956) explains why underdeveloped economies experience persistent stagnation in per capita income at or near subsistence levels.

Endogenous Population Growth: At low levels of per capita income (), the population growth rate () is an increasing function of per capita income because higher income reduces mortality rates due to better nutrition and healthcare. However, beyond a certain threshold of per capita income, population growth reaches a biological maximum (around 3%) and eventually begins to decline as birth rates drop.
  3. Capital Accumulation and Savings: The rate of net capital formation is limited by the rate of savings, which is endogenously tied to per capita income. Below a critical subsistence level of per capita income, net savings and net investment are zero or negative.
Mechanism of the Population-Income Relationship

The core of the trap lies in the race between population growth and total income growth when an economy attempts to advance from a low base:

The Trap Dynamics
  • The Subsistence Equilibrium: At the stable trap point, per capita income is just enough to sustain the population. Capital formation is stagnant, and population growth is stable.
  • The Regressive Force: If a small injection of capital or a minor development project increases total national income, per capita income rises slightly above the subsistence level.
  • The Correction: This slight increase triggers a sharp decline in death rates, causing a rapid expansion in population growth. If the rate of population growth () rises faster than the rate of total income growth (), per capita income is inevitably dragged back down to the original stable subsistence level.

Thus, small, piecemeal developmental efforts are completely neutralized by the resulting demographic expansion, locking the country back into the low-level equilibrium trap.

Overcoming the Low-Level Equilibrium Trap

To break free from this demographic and economic trap, an economy must implement coordinated, large-scale strategies rather than small, isolated investments:

  • A "Critical Minimum Effort" or "Big Push": The economy requires a massive, lump-sum injection of investment that abruptly boosts per capita income far beyond the point where the population growth rate hits its biological maximum. Once per capita income exceeds this critical threshold, the rate of growth of total income permanently outpaces population growth, leading to self-sustained expansion.
  • Aggressive Population Control Measures: Simultaneously dampening the population growth curve through family planning, public health campaigns, and female literacy programs lowers the biological maximum growth line, making the trap easier to bypass.
  • Technological Discontinuity: Introducing advanced, labor-augmenting technology shifts the total production function upward, allowing output to grow much faster than capital and labor inputs alone.
  • Mobilization of Foreign Capital: Utilizing foreign direct investment (FDI), concessionary foreign aid, and external loans helps close the domestic savings-investment gap without forcing a reduction in the consumption levels of the low-income population.
