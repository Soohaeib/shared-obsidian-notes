BSED 2021 Exam Q1

(a)

Distinguish Between Economic Growth and Economic Development and Methods of Measurement

Economic growth and economic development are two foundational concepts in development economics, often used interchangeably in casual discourse but strictly differentiated in academic and policy frameworks.

Basis of Comparison Economic Growth Economic Development
Definition The quantitative, sustained increase in a country's real per capita output or national income over a specified period. A multidimensional process involving major changes in social structures, popular attitudes, national institutions, inequality reduction, and poverty eradication alongside economic growth.
Nature of Concept Narrow, unidimensional, and purely quantitative. Broad, multidimensional, both quantitative and qualitative.
Scope Focuses strictly on market production and output expansion (e.g., GDP, GNI). Focuses on structural changes, structural transformation of the economy, and institutional improvements.
Measurement Measured by percentage changes in Real Gross Domestic Product (GDP) or Real Gross National Income (GNI) per capita. Measured by complex indices such as the Human Development Index (HDI), Physical Quality of Life Index (PQLI), and Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI).
Structural Change Does not necessarily require progressive structural shifts; an economy can grow purely through commodity booms. Inherently requires structural shifts (e.g., transition from agriculture to industry/services, urbanization, progressive tax systems).
Target Audience Concerned primarily with aggregate macroeconomic performance. Concerned with distribution of wealth, human well-being, and capacity building.
Measurement of Economic Development

Economic development cannot be completely captured by a single monetary metric. Economists utilize several sophisticated tools to measure it accurately:

  • Human Development Index (HDI): Formulated by the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP), the HDI provides a composite metric based on three critical dimensions of human welfare:
    • Longevity: Measured by life expectancy at birth.
    • Knowledge: Measured by a combination of mean years of schooling for adults and expected years of schooling for children.
    • Standard of Living: Measured by Real GNI per capita adjusted for Purchasing Power Parity (PPP).
  • Physical Quality of Life Index (PQLI): Developed by Morris David Morris, this index measures development based on non-income components:
    • Infant mortality rate.
    • Life expectancy at age one.
    • Basic literacy rate.
  • Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI): Identifies overlapping deprivations at the household level across health, education, and living standards, offering a more vivid assessment of poverty than income-based lines.
  • Real GNI per Capita (PPP adjusted): While an income measure, adjusting it for purchasing power parity provides a baseline measure of the real economic choices available to an average citizen.

(b)

The Kuznets Hypothesis of Economic Growth and Income Distribution

The Kuznets Hypothesis, advanced by economist Simon Kuznets in 1955, postulates an Inverted-U relationship between the level of economic development (measured by per capita income) and income inequality.

According to this hypothesis:

  1. Early Stage of Development: As a low-income agricultural economy begins to industrialize and grow, income distribution initially worsens, causing inequality to rise.
  2. Inflection Point: Inequality reaches a peak at an intermediate level of development.
  3. Advanced Stage of Development: As the country reaches higher levels of industrialization and maturity, income distribution automatically becomes more equitable, causing inequality to fall.
Causes of the Initial Increase in Income Inequality During Development

Kuznets and subsequent development economists identified several structural mechanisms that explain why inequality increases during the initial phases of economic progress:

  • Inter-Sectoral Structural Shifts (The Dual Economy Effect): In the early stages of development, the economy undergoes a shift from a low-wage, traditional agricultural sector to a high-wage, modern industrial sector (similar to the dynamics outlined in the Lewis Model). Initially, only a small portion of the workforce transitions to urban industrial jobs, creating a vast income gap between modern sector workers and traditional agrarian laborers.

  • Concentration of Savings and Capital Accumulation:

    In early industrialization, capital is highly concentrated in the hands of a small elite class of industrial entrepreneurs. Because rich individuals have a higher marginal propensity to save compared to the poor, they accumulate capital at a faster rate. This concentration of asset ownership compounds their income through profits and returns on capital (), widening the gap with wage laborers.

  • Skill-Biased Technological Change:

    Modern industrial processes require specialized technical skills. In an early-stage developing economy, skilled labor is scarce, command over technology is limited, and the premium for skilled work is disproportionately high. This widens the wage differential between a small pool of educated/skilled workers and a massive pool of unskilled agricultural or manual laborers.

  • Weak Institutional Redistribution and Safety Nets:

    In the initial periods of growth, public institutions are often underdeveloped, tax systems are regressive (relying heavily on indirect taxes), and social safety nets are virtually non-existent. Governments typically prioritize capital formation over wealth redistribution, leaving the lower-income brackets without compensatory welfare buffers.

  • Urbanization and Urban Dualism:

    Rapid migration outpaces urban capacity, creating a dual economy within urban centers. A highly paid formal sector coexists with an unorganized, low-productivity informal sector (slums, day laborers), which drives up intra-urban income disparities.
