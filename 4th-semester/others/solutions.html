Insurance and Risk Management (BCC-206)

BCC-206 Q1(a)

Definition of Risk and the Boundary with Uncertainty

  • Risk: Risk is the uncertainty concerning the occurrence of a loss. It can also be defined in a specific sense as the relative variation of actual loss from expected loss.
  • Boundary: The conceptual boundary lies in the measurability of probability. Risk applies to situations where the probabilities of possible outcomes are known or can be estimated with accuracy (e.g., mortality rates). Uncertainty applies to situations where such probabilities cannot be calculated or estimated (e.g., the probability of a home being hit by a meteorite).

BCC-206 Q1(b)

Necessity of Risk Management and Effective Framework

  • Necessity: It is essential to ensure the survival of the firm, maintain stability of earnings, and meet legal obligations. It reduces the "cost of risk," which maximizes business value by lowering expected losses and reducing the fear associated with catastrophic events.
  • Common Organisational Risks: (1) Price Risk (fluctuations in commodity/currency prices); (2) Credit Risk (customer default); (3) Liability Risk (lawsuits); and (4) Property Risk (damage to assets).
  • Framework Stages:
    1. Identify all significant loss exposures.
    2. Measure and Analyze the frequency and severity of these exposures.
    3. Select Techniques: Choose a combination of Risk Control (avoidance, prevention) and Risk Financing (retention, transfer/insurance).
    4. Implement and Monitor the program for ongoing suitability.

BCC-206 Q1(c)

Pure Risk vs. Speculative Risk

  • Pure Risk: Only the possibilities of loss or no loss exist (e.g., a dwelling fire). The element of loss is absolute; society is generally harmed because wealth is destroyed without offsetting gains.
  • Speculative Risk: Either profit or loss is possible (e.g., investing in the stock market). Here, the element of loss can be socially beneficial; for instance, a firm failing due to a new competitor’s cheaper technology still results in lower costs for society.

BCC-206 Q2(a)

Rahim (Sedentary lifestyle/Family history): Loss Prevention combined with Life Insurance.

  • Defense: Prevention (lifestyle changes/medical check-ups) reduces the high-frequency/high-severity health hazard, while Life Insurance transfers the catastrophic financial impact of premature death to an insurer.

BCC-206 Q2(b)

Investment Fund (Market volatility): Hedging with Financial Derivatives.

  • Defense: Volatility is a speculative price risk; hedging (e.g., put options) provides an offsetting profit when market values decline, protecting the portfolio's net value.

BCC-206 Q2(c)

Software Firm (Security loopholes): Loss Prevention and Cyber Liability Insurance.

  • Defense: Prevention ("hardening" networks) lowers the probability of a breach, while specialized insurance handles the potentially ruinous legal liability and restoration costs associated with data theft.

BCC-206 Q2(d)

Small Business Owner (Bankruptcy fear): Incorporation.

  • Defense: Incorporation is a non-insurance transfer that limits the owner’s legal liability, shielding personal assets from business creditors.

BCC-206 Q2(e)

Garment Company (Coastal factories): Separation and Specialized Property Insurance.

  • Defense: Separation (multiple locations) ensures that a single event does not destroy all capacity. Specialized catastrophe insurance provides recovery funds for the unavoidable high-severity losses caused by cyclones.

BCC-206 Q3(a)

Loss Frequency vs. Loss Severity

  • Relationship: They generally share an inverse relationship. Frequency is the number of losses in a period; Severity is the size of each loss. High-frequency losses are usually low-severity (e.g., shoplifting), while low-frequency events often have high severity (e.g., plane crashes). A change in frequency (via loss prevention) can reduce total annual losses even if the severity of a single hit remains high.

BCC-206 Q3(b)

Quantitative Assessment Methods

  • Methods: Probability distributions (like the normal distribution) use historical data to find the mean (expected loss) and standard deviation (risk/variability).
  • Example: An actuary analyzing 10,000 homes knows that if 1% burn annually, the expected loss is 100 homes. Using the Law of Large Numbers, as the sample size increases, objective risk (variation) decreases, making predictions more accurate.

BCC-206 Q3(c)

Economic Conditions (Inflation and Interest Rates)

  • Inflation: Increases the demand for higher policy limits as the cost of repairing property or paying medical claims rises.
  • Interest Rates: Rising rates can lower premiums through cash flow underwriting (where investment income offsets underwriting losses). However, they also create interest rate risk for insurers' bond portfolios, which may eventually lead to a "hard" market with higher premiums.

BCC-206 Q4(a)

Insurance vs. Gambling

  • Difference: Insurance handles already existing pure risk; gambling creates a new speculative risk.
  • Social Benefit: Insurance is socially beneficial because it provides financial stability, enhances credit, and serves as a source of investment funds. Gambling is unproductive as the winner's gain comes solely at the loser's expense without restoring the loser's position.

BCC-206 Q4(b)

Principle of Utmost Good Faith

  • Definition: It requires all parties to a contract to act with total honesty and disclose all material facts.
  • Importance: It is essential for trust because insurers cannot personally verify every detail of an applicant’s life or property; it prevents adverse selection and ensures fairness in premium pricing.

BCC-206 Q4(c)

Principle of Insurable Interest

  • Significance: The insured must be in a position to lose financially if the event occurs; this prevents insurance from becoming a wagering contract.
  • Examples: A person has an insurable interest in their own home; a bank in a mortgaged property; or a business partner in the life of another partner.

BCC-206 Q4(d)

Reinsurance

  • Purpose: Reinsurance is the transfer of insurance from a primary insurer (ceding company) to another insurer (reinsurer).
  • Importance: It allows companies to increase underwriting capacity, stabilize profits, and protect against catastrophic losses (clash losses) that exceed their retention limits.

BCC-206 Q5(a)

Life Insurance and Intergenerational Security

  • Definition: A contract where the insurer, in exchange for a premium, pays a fixed sum upon the death of the insured or at the end of a specified period.
  • Mission: To provide protection against the financial consequences of premature death and to provide income for old age.
  • Intergenerational Tool: It provides intergenerational security by ensuring that a family head’s unfulfilled financial obligations (e.g., child education, mortgage) are met, allowing wealth and opportunity to persist despite the breadwinner's death.

BCC-206 Q5(b)

Term vs. Whole Life vs. Universal Life

  • Term: Level or decreasing death benefit; no cash value; lowest premium; suitable for young families with limited income or temporary needs.
  • Whole Life: Lifetime protection; guaranteed cash values; fixed level premiums; suitable for lifelong estate planning.
  • Universal Life: Separates protection and savings; flexible premiums; cash value earns interest (market-linked with a guaranteed minimum); suitable for those needing adjustable benefits.

BCC-206 Q5(c)

Challenges and Trends

  • Challenges: Volatile interest rates, market saturation in developed regions, and rising management expenses.
  • Trends: Insurtech (digital marketing/underwriting), Unit-Linked Insurance Plans (ULIPs) which shift investment risk to the policyholder, and a shift toward micro-life insurance for low-income segments.

BCC-206 Q6(a)

Marine Insurance in International Trade

  • Concept: Covers losses incident to a marine adventure, including damage to ships (Hull), goods (Cargo), and loss of earnings (Freight).
  • Significance: It is the backbone of global trade, providing indemnity against "perils of the sea" (collision, sinking, piracy). Example: Insuring a shipment of computers from one port to another.

BCC-206 Q6(b)

Types of Marine Insurance Policies

  • i. Time Policy: Covers the risk for a specific period of time rather than a specific voyage.
  • ii. Unvalued Policy: The value of the property is not fixed at inception; the claim is based on the market value at the time of loss.
  • iii. Floating Policy: Covers multiple shipments under one sum; details of each shipment are declared later.
  • iv. Blanket Policy: Covers varied property in a particular time and place for a fixed premium, often with a year-end adjustment based on actual risk.
  • v. Block Policy: Provides very broad "all-risk" coverage for specific trade inventories (e.g., jewelers' block) whether on premises or in transit.

BCC-206 Q7(a)

Jiban Bima Corporation (JBC) Product Categories

  • JBC operates diverse schemes including Whole Life, Endowment (the most popular), Double Endowment, Marriage Endowment, Educational Annuity, Limited Payment Endowment, and Money Back Policies.

BCC-206 Q7(b)

Note on IDRA Bangladesh

  • Establishment: Created by the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority Act, 1999.
  • Mandate: To regulate the insurance industry to ensure solvency, fair play, and the protection of policyholders' interests.
  • Key Functions: Licensing new insurers, approving product pricing/wording, monitoring solvency margins, and regulating market conduct to prevent fraud.

BCC-206 Q7(c)

Proposed Government Policy Measures

  • Strategic Initiatives:
    • Implement mandatory insurance for high-risk public sectors like commercial transport and industrial construction.
    • Promote Microinsurance by supporting NGOs and Self-Help Groups to reach the low-income population.
    • Develop Public-Private Partnerships for catastrophe and agricultural crop insurance to manage national-level disaster risks.
    • Enforce stricter transparency norms and corporate governance to restore public trust and ensure prompt claim settlements.
    • Launch nationwide insurance awareness campaigns focusing on the "Risk and Safety" (Yogakshema) benefits of coverage.

Business Ethics (BCC-207)

BCC-207 Q1(a)

Definition of Ethics & Relationship with Law

  • Definition of Ethics: Ethics is the study of business situations, activities, and decisions where issues of right and wrong are addressed.
  • Relationship between Ethics and Law: Ethics begins where the law ends, covering "grey areas" where legal rules are silent or values are in conflict.

BCC-207 Q1(b)

Importance of Business Ethics

  • It is crucial for mitigating potential harm to stakeholders, restoring public trust, and addressing the increasing power and influence of business in society.

BCC-207 Q1(c)

Relevance of Globalisation

  • Globalisation deterritorializes economic activities, allowing them to escape the control of national governments and necessitating global ethical standards.

BCC-207 Q2(a)

Ethical Decision Components and Individual Factors

  • Key Components of an Ethical Decision: An ethical decision is characterized by choice, significant effects on others, and the perception of the issue as ethically relevant by at least one party.
  • Influence of Individual Factors: Factors such as cognitive moral development determine an individual's reasoning process, while personal values and cognitive biases shape what they perceive as "preferable" conduct.

BCC-207 Q2(b)

Sustainability and TBL

  • Sustainability: Meeting present needs without compromising future generations.
  • The Triple Bottom Line (TBL): Shifts corporate goals from purely economic value to a balanced focus on People, Planet, and Profit.

BCC-207 Q2(c)

Tea Estate Case

  • Under the TBL framework, the owner should not continue using the pesticide; while it offers a 30% economic gain, it fails the environmental pillar (water contamination) and the social pillar (harm to villager health).

BCC-207 Q3(a)

Firm-Employee Relationship & Globalisation

  • Firm-Employee Relationship: Ethical issues include discrimination, privacy breaches, and the struggle for fair wages.
  • Globalisation: Complicates this by creating a "race to the bottom" where multinationals exploit lower standards in developing countries.

BCC-207 Q3(b)

Employee Rights and Duties

  • Core Rights and Duties: Core rights include freedom from discrimination, privacy, and fair wages, while duties include complying with contracts and respecting employer property.
  • Workplace Discrimination: Violates these rights by denying equal opportunity based on irrelevant characteristics.

BCC-207 Q3(c)

FoodGiant Case:

  • i. Ethical Issues: Targeted marketing to vulnerable children (consumers) and exploitative working hours without overtime pay or union rights (employees).
  • ii. Double Standard: The company promotes "healthy choices" in developed markets while exploiting lack of regulation to sell sugary products to children in developing ones.
  • iii. Social Responsibility: These actions are not socially responsible because they fail to respect the long-term well-being of stakeholders for short-term gain.

BCC-207 Q4(a)

Business Ethics Management

  • Definition: This is the formal or informal attempt to manage ethical issues through specific policies and programmes.
  • Components: Include codes of ethics, reporting channels, and risk analysis.

BCC-207 Q4(b)

Types of Ethical Codes

  • These include corporate codes of conduct, professional codes, and industry-wide codes.

BCC-207 Q4(c)

Reasons for Social Accounting

  • Organizations engage in this to respond to stakeholder pressure, enhance transparency, and improve internal accountability.

BCC-207 Q5(a)

Government as Stakeholder

  • Function: Government defines the "licence to operate" for businesses.
  • Ethical Issues: In developing economies, issues like "state capture"—where private firms influence regulation through illicit payments—are common.

BCC-207 Q5(b)

Corporate Citizenship and Engagement

  • Corporate Citizenship: This views the firm as a political actor governing citizens' rights.
  • Roles: A "key player" actively shapes the regulatory game to its advantage, while a "passive rule-follower" merely complies with existing laws.

BCC-207 Q5(c)

MegaPort Ltd. Case:

  • Analysis: Bribery to accelerate approvals is ethically invalid; it violates the principles of fair competition and corporate integrity.
  • Systemic Changes: Stricter transparency, independent auditing, and international enforcement are required to prevent such ethical violations.

BCC-207 Q6(a)

Agency Relationship

  • Shareholders are principals who hire managers as agents. Conflict arises from diverging interests (e.g., profit vs. high pay), while informational asymmetry leaves shareholders with less knowledge than managers.

BCC-207 Q6(b)

Hostile Takeovers

  • These can devastate employee welfare through layoffs and erode local community identity by relocating traditional brands.

BCC-207 Q6(c)

Pharma CEO Share Sale:

  • Analysis: This is a case of insider trading, violating shareholder rights to fairness and information.
  • Governance Reforms: Should include mandatory "blackout" periods for executive trading and stricter internal controls.

BCC-207 Q7(a)

Ethical Theories

  • Consequentialist theories (e.g., Utilitarianism) judge actions by their outcomes.
  • Non-consequentialist theories (e.g., Ethics of Duties) focus on underlying principles.

BCC-207 Q7(b)

Egoism vs. Utilitarianism vs. Duties

  • Egoism: Focuses on self-interest.
  • Utilitarianism: Seeks the "greatest good for the greatest number". Utilitarianism is most common in business due to its alignment with cost-benefit analysis.
  • Ethics of Duties: Focuses on universal moral laws.

BCC-207 Q7(c)

Nadia’s Factory Choice:

  • Distributing a bonus is defensible via Feminist Ethics (prioritizing relationships/care), while investing in safety equipment is most defensible via Ethics of Duty (treating workers as ends in themselves by ensuring long-term safety).

Entrepreneurship Development (BCC-208)

BCC-208 Q1(a)

Definitions and Passion:

  • An entrepreneur is a person who creates a new venture and undertakes the risk of starting an enterprise by putting an idea into practice.
  • Entrepreneurship is the process of pursuing opportunities and turning an idea into a business without regard to resources currently controlled.
  • Passion is the number one characteristic of successful entrepreneurs, acting as the driving force that sustains the business through tough times.

BCC-208 Q1(b)

Execution Intelligence:

  • It is the ability to fashion a solid idea into a viable business by developing a business model, assembling a team, and managing finances.
  • It relates to an entrepreneur as the factor that often determines start-up success through translating creativity and imagination into measurable results.

BCC-208 Q1(c)

Attributes of Corporate Entrepreneurship:

  • Include being proactive, innovative, and risk-taking.

BCC-208 Q1(d)

Aarong Case:

  • i) Vision was most important, as she identified an unmet market need by connecting rural artisans with reliable markets.
  • ii) Leadership allowed for organizing thousands of artisans into a productive network.
  • iii) Aarong created social value by ensuring fair prices and employment opportunities for disadvantaged rural women.

BCC-208 Q2(a)

Marketplace Gap:

  • Gaps in the marketplace are business opportunities created when products or services that consumers need aren't available at all or in a specific location. Entrepreneurs create value by identifying these underserved niches within larger markets.

BCC-208 Q2(b)

Creativity in Opportunity Recognition:

  • Creativity is the process of generating novel or useful ideas. It allows entrepreneurs to recognize opportunity gaps—the difference between what's available and what's possible—and turn them into business concepts.

BCC-208 Q2(c)

Traits for Opportunity Recognition:

  • Prior industry experience, cognitive factors such as entrepreneurial alertness, social networks, and creativity.

BCC-208 Q2(d)

Drinkwell Case:

  • i) Drinkwell identified severe shortages of safe drinking water caused by salinity in groundwater in coastal districts.
  • ii) This is an entrepreneurial opportunity because it addressed a major social and health problem while creating a sustainable business model where local operators earn income.
  • iii) The company introduced community-based water purification systems that convert contaminated water into affordable, safe drinking water through plants operated by local entrepreneurs.

BCC-208 Q3(a)

Best Time for Business Model:

  • The best time for a firm to develop its business model is following the initial validation of the business idea but prior to fleshing out the operational details of the company.

BCC-208 Q3(b)

Pathao Case:

  • i) Pathao's business model was disruptive because it used a gig-based rider network and real-time matching via smartphone connectivity to lower costs and scale rapidly without owning vehicles.
  • ii) Affected industries included taxis, rickshaws, buses, restaurant deliveries, parcel logistics, and cash-based transactions.
  • iii) Local factors included Dhaka's extreme traffic congestion, widespread affordable smartphone use, and the local acceptance of mobile payments.

BCC-208 Q3(c)

Main Contents of a Business Plan:

  • Executive Summary, Industry Analysis, Company Description, Market Analysis, The Economics of the Business, Marketing Plan, Design and Development Plan, Operations Plan, Management Team and Company Structure, Overall Schedule, and Financial Projections.

BCC-208 Q3(d)

Aamra Fresh Agro Case:

  • i) Revenue sources were commissions on each sale and subscription packages for regular customers.
  • ii) Identified risks included transportation delays, seasonal fluctuations in production, and competition from established online grocery platforms.

BCC-208 Q4(a)

Ethical Culture Prerequisites:

  • Prerequisites for a strong ethical culture include leaders leading by example, establishing a formal code of conduct, and implementing ethics training programs.

BCC-208 Q4(b)

Liability of Newness:

  • Liability of newness refers to the fact that new companies often falter because the people involved can't adjust quickly enough to their new roles and the firm lacks a track record of success.
  • A new venture can overcome this by assembling a talented management team and recruiting high-quality advisors to gain legitimacy.

BCC-208 Q4(c)

Industry Experience:

  • Relevant industry experience provides founders with better-established professional networks, more applicable marketing and management skills, and a deeper understanding of industry subtleties.

BCC-208 Q4(d)

FreshBrew Case:

  • i. Liability of newness explains their struggles, as they lacked the experience and reputation needed with suppliers and customers.
  • ii. Industry advisors help FreshBrew with specialized tasks like training and negotiating supplier contracts while providing legitimacy.
  • iii. A benefit of multiple founders is that a team provides greater resources, a broader diversity of viewpoints, and psychological support compared to a sole individual.

BCC-208 Q5(a)

Acquisition:

  • Acquisition is the outright purchase of one firm by another, such as Walmart buying Moosejaw.
  • They fail sometimes due to a clash of corporate cultures, incompatible business processes, and increased business complexity.

BCC-208 Q5(b)

Second-Generation Challenges:

  • Challenges for second-generation entrepreneurs include non-participating family members, family emotions, family versus business conflicts, defining authority, and the "fair to all" approach to profit-sharing.

BCC-208 Q5(c)

Amena Akter Case:

  • i) Amena faced the financial challenge of limited access to finance due to bank requirements for collateral and lengthy documentation.
  • ii) The social barrier was the belief that women should prioritize household responsibilities over business.
  • iii) Family responsibilities required her to balance household duties and childcare with supplier negotiations and marketing.
  • iv) Strategies included increasing online marketing, offering home delivery services, and participating in entrepreneurship training programs.

BCC-208 Q6(a)

New Product Development:

  • Keys to effective NPD include finding an unfilled need, developing products that add value, getting quality and pricing right, and focusing on a specific target market.
  • Common reasons for failure are overestimating market potential, poor design, inadequate feasibility analysis, bad timing, and poor service.

BCC-208 Q6(b)

Internal vs. External Growth:

  • Internal growth strategies rely on efforts generated within the firm itself, such as new product development or geographic expansion.
  • External growth strategies rely on third-party relationships, including mergers, acquisitions, strategic alliances, joint ventures, and licensing.

BCC-208 Q6(c)

Licensing as a Growth Strategy:

  • Licensing is granting permission for another firm to use proprietary rights to a product or process in exchange for royalties.
  • As a growth strategy, it enables firms to expand their reach and earn income with low capital investment by leveraging others' manufacturing or distribution capabilities.

BCC-208 Q7(a)

Need for Capital:

  • New ventures need to raise money to manage cash flow challenges (lag between spending and sales), fund capital investments (equipment/facilities), and support lengthy product development cycles.

BCC-208 Q7(b)

Steps to Raise Financing:

  • Three steps for raising debt or equity: 1. Determine precisely how much money is needed; 2. Determine the most appropriate type of financing; 3. Develop a strategy for engaging potential investors or bankers.

BCC-208 Q7(c)

IPO as a Milestone:

  • An IPO is a milestone because it raises equity capital, increases public profile, provides a liquidity event for investors, and creates currency (stock) for future acquisitions.

BCC-208 Q7(d)

Advantages of Leasing:

  • Advantages of leasing include the ability to acquire assets with little or no down payment, which preserves cash for other operational needs.

Organizational Behaviour (BCC-209)

BCC-209 Q1(a)

Introduction to OB and Interpersonal Skills:

  • Definition of OB: A field of study investigating the impact that individuals, groups, and structure have on behavior within organisations to apply such knowledge toward improving effectiveness.
  • Importance of Interpersonal Skills: Determines managerial effectiveness and career advancement. Helps organisations attract and retain high-performing employees. Strongly associated with employee job satisfaction and reduced stress/turnover.

BCC-209 Q1(b)

Major Contributing Disciplines:

  • Psychology: Focuses on individual behavior (learning, personality, emotions, perception).
  • Social Psychology: Blends psychology and sociology to focus on influence and change.
  • Sociology: Studies people in relation to their social environment or culture.
  • Anthropology: Studies societies to learn about fundamental values and national cultures.

BCC-209 Q1(c)

Concept Success vs. Failure (Analysis):

  • Challenge 1: Continuing Globalization: Concepts must adapt to differing cultural norms (e.g., what motivates in one culture may not in another).
  • Challenge 2: Workforce Diversity: Managers must adapt to heterogeneous characteristics (age, gender, race) that shape distinct preferences for rewards and communication.
  • Opportunity 1: Positive Work Environments: Using OB to develop human strengths and foster vitality.
  • Opportunity 2: Big Data/AI: Leveraging data to define objectives and test theories for more effective decision-making.

BCC-209 Q2(a)

Software vs. Construction Ability Analysis:

  • Programming (Software): Requires Intellectual Ability—mental capacities for thinking, reasoning, and problem-solving (e.g., number aptitude, verbal comprehension).
  • Heavy Equipment Operator (Construction): Requires Physical Ability—capacity for tasks demanding stamina, dexterity, and strength.

BCC-209 Q2(b)

Biographical Characteristics:

  • Objective personal characteristics—such as age, gender, race, and ethnicity—easily obtained from personnel records.
  • Effect on OB: They represent "surface-level diversity" that may activate stereotypes or influence turnover, absenteeism, and perceived fairness.

BCC-209 Q2(c)

Causes of Job Satisfaction:

  • Intrinsic Factors: Interesting work, training, variety, autonomy, and social support.
  • Pay vs. Work Itself: Pay correlates with satisfaction only up to a point of financial comfort. The intrinsic nature of the work itself is a much stronger long-term predictor of satisfaction.

BCC-209 Q3(a)

Components of Attitude and Predicting Behaviour:

  • Three Components of Attitude: Cognitive (The belief or opinion segment), Affective (The emotional or feeling segment), Behavioral (The intention to behave in a certain way).
  • Predicting Behaviour: Managers use attitudes (like job satisfaction) as the best single predictors of outcomes like performance, absenteeism, and turnover.

BCC-209 Q3(b)

Emotional Labour:

  • An employee’s expression of organizationally desired emotions during interpersonal transactions.
  • Surface Acting: Hiding inner feelings and forgoing emotional expressions (e.g., a cashier smiling despite being berated by a rude customer).
  • Deep Acting: Trying to modify inner true feelings based on display rules (e.g., a customer service rep empathizing with a caller’s frustration to feel genuine care).

BCC-209 Q3(c)

GreenMart Case Analysis:

  • i. Affected Attitudes: Job Satisfaction (dissatisfied/unappreciated) and Employee Engagement (checked out/emotional exhaustion).
  • ii. Role of Emotional Intelligence (EI): EI helps supervisors detect and manage emotional cues. High EI can improve morale and reduce burnout by facilitating better teamwork and reducing counterproductive behaviors.

BCC-209 Q4(a)

Big Five Personality Model:

  • Traits: Conscientiousness (Responsible, dependable), Emotional Stability (Calm, secure), Extroversion (Sociable, assertive), Openness to Experience (Creative, curious), Agreeableness (Cooperative, trusting).
  • Manager in Bangladesh: Conscientiousness is the best overall predictor of job performance. However, in a collectivist culture like Bangladesh, Agreeableness may be more important for fostering organizational commitment and team harmony.

BCC-209 Q4(b)

Perception and Shortcuts:

  • Perception is how individuals organize and interpret sensory impressions to give meaning to their environment.
  • Halo/Horns Effect: Drawing a general impression based on a single characteristic.
  • Contrast Effect: Evaluation affected by comparisons with recently encountered people.
  • Stereotyping: Judging someone based on the perception of the group they belong to.

BCC-209 Q4(c)

Perception/Decision-Making Link:

  • Decisions are choices among alternatives; perception determines which data are screened and interpreted as "relevant".
  • Organizational Constraints: Performance evaluation systems, reward systems, formal regulations, and time constraints all limit choices and bias decisions.

BCC-209 Q5(a)

Groups and Stages of Development:

  • Group Definition: Two or more individuals, interacting and interdependent, who come together to achieve particular objectives.
  • Stages: Forming (Uncertainty about purpose and leadership), Storming (Intragroup conflict over control), Norming (Close relationships and cohesiveness develop), Performing (Structure is fully functional and accepted), Adjourning (Wrapping up activities for temporary groups).

BCC-209 Q5(b)

Work Group vs. Work Team:

  • Work Group: Interacts primarily to share information; individual effort without positive synergy.
  • Work Team: Individual efforts result in performance greater than the sum of inputs (synergy).
  • Effective Team Characteristics: Adequate resources, effective leadership/structure, trust, member ability/personality, and a common purpose.

BCC-209 Q5(c)

BDCom Networks Case Analysis:

  • i. Current Stage: Storming. Evidence: Open conflicts over leadership, arguments about priorities, and project delays.
  • ii. Team Leader Actions: 1. Leadership and Structure: Clarify who does what and ensure members share the workload. 2. Common Purpose: Re-establish a clear mission and specific goals for the project.

BCC-209 Q6(a)

Leadership and Styles:

  • Leadership Importance: It is the ability to influence a group toward achieving a vision or goals.
  • Charismatic: Relies on followers attributing heroic abilities based on values-driven, symbolic, or unconventional behavior.
  • Transformational: Inspires followers to transcend self-interest via individualized consideration, intellectual stimulation, and idealized influence.

BCC-209 Q6(b)

Power and Its Bases:

  • Power is the capacity to enforce one’s will over others.
  • Bases: Formal (Coercive, Reward, Legitimate). Personal (Expert, Referent).
  • Most Sustainable: Personal Power (Expert and Referent). It is most strongly related to employee performance and commitment.

BCC-209 Q6(c)

DynaFab Ltd. Case Analysis:

  • i. Power Identification: Mr. X is relying on Legitimate Power (formal hierarchy). The previous manager possessed Expert Power (20 years of experience) and Referent Power (respect and trust).
  • ii. Advice to Mr. X: Cultivate Expert Power by demonstrating competence and Referent Power by treating workers with respect/dignity to build trust.

BCC-209 Q7(a)

Conflict Process:

  • A process starting when one party perceives that another has negatively affected something the first party cares about.
  • Five Stages: 1. Potential Opposition or Incompatibility. 2. Cognition and Personalization. 3. Intentions (Competing, Collaborating, etc.). 4. Behavior (Overt actions). 5. Outcomes (Functional or Dysfunctional).

BCC-209 Q7(b)

Organisational Change and Resistance:

  • When things become different than they were.
  • Reasons for Resistance: Habit, security needs, fear of unknown, and structural inertia.
  • Overcoming Resistance: Communication (providing rationale) and Participation (involving employees in the process).

BCC-209 Q7(c)

MegaBank Case Analysis:

  • i. Stress Sources: Organizational (role ambiguity, increased workload, poor communication) and Personal (fear of job loss/security). Consequences visible: Physiological (headaches, insomnia), Psychological (reduced concentration), and Behavioral (absenteeism, resistance to software).
  • ii. Strategies: Individual Strategy: Mindfulness/Relaxation Techniques to adopt a compassionate state and reduce psychological distress. Organizational Strategy: Job Redesign to clarify roles and provide autonomy, reducing the strain of ambiguity.

Bangladesh Studies and Economic Development (BCC-210)

BCC-210 Q1(a)

History of Bangladesh:

  • The Pakistan Movement sought a separate state for Muslims in British India, leading to Pakistan's creation in 1947.
  • The Language Movement (1948–1952) emerged to establish Bengali as a state language, culminating in the historic events of February 21, 1952.
  • In 1954, the Juktafront (United Front) won the provincial elections.
  • Sheikh Mujibur Rahman proposed the Six Point Programme in 1966, demanding regional autonomy for East Pakistan.
  • This led to the Mass Upsurge of 1969, forcing the fall of Ayub Khan.
  • The 1970 Elections gave the Awami League a clear majority, but refusal to hand over power led to the Bangladesh Liberation War in 1971. Bangladesh emerged as an independent country on December 16, 1971.

BCC-210 Q1(b)

Constitution of Bangladesh (1972):

  • Adopted on November 4, 1972, serving as the highest law of the land.
  • Establishes a parliamentary democracy with a Prime Minister as the head of government.
  • Key features include universal adult franchise for citizens aged 18 and over, a unicameral legislature (Jatiyo Sangshad) with 300 directly elected members, and the recognition of fundamental rights. It provides the legal basis for the executive, legislature, and judiciary.

BCC-210 Q1(c)

Judiciary System of Bangladesh:

  • Modelled after the British system and is governed by the 1972 Constitution along with codes of civil and criminal laws.
  • Comprises four main institutions: the Supreme Court, Subordinate Courts, the Magistracy, and Administrative Tribunals.
  • Incorporates certain religious principles for personal matters such as marriage and inheritance.

BCC-210 Q2(a)

Characteristics and Underdevelopment of Bangladesh Economy:

  • Characterised by an undeveloped agricultural system, inadequate capital, a low standard of living, and high population pressure.
  • Faces an unfavourable balance of payments and dependence on foreign aid.
  • Economic reasons for underdevelopment include low income, low savings, and difficulties in capital formation. Political instability and social factors like "brain drain" and undeveloped infrastructure also hinder progress.

BCC-210 Q2(b)

Cottage Industries and Their Role:

  • Main cottage industries include hand-loom, silk, pottery, bamboo/cane, leather, and weaving.
  • They play a vital role by generating employment, particularly for women, reducing the pressure on agriculture, and requiring small amounts of capital.
  • They help reduce inequality, save foreign exchange, and safeguard national heritage.

BCC-210 Q2(c)

Population of Bangladesh:

  • Bangladesh has a population of approximately 169.83 million with a high density of 1,153 persons per square kilometre.
  • Overpopulation leads to food deficits, housing problems, and health hazards.
  • It creates social and political hazards while hindering capital formation and increasing the number of unskilled labourers.

BCC-210 Q3(a)

Economic Growth, Development, and HDI:

  • Economic growth refers to a sustained increase in national income (GDP), whereas economic development includes growth plus changes in technical and institutional arrangements.
  • The Human Development Index (HDI) is measured using three indicators: longevity (life expectancy), educational attainment (adult literacy and years of schooling), and a decent standard of living (adjusted GDP per capita).
  • Factors determining economic growth include natural resources, capital accumulation, technological progress, and non-economic factors like social and political stability.

BCC-210 Q3(b)

Kuznets Hypothesis:

  • States that in early stages of growth, income inequality increases, stabilises, and then declines in later stages, forming an "inverted-U" curve.
  • Inequality increases during development due to the transition to capital-intensive techniques and rural-urban migration.
  • It eventually decreases as per capita income rises for the lowest groups through government intervention in services like health and education.

BCC-210 Q3(c)

Sustainable Development and SDGs:

  • Sustainable development means meeting the needs of the present generation without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.
  • The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) include 17 global goals such as ending poverty and hunger, ensuring quality education, achieving gender equality, and taking urgent action on climate change.

BCC-210 Q4(a)

Poverty and Alleviation in Bangladesh:

  • Poverty is measured by economic condition, calorie intake (requirement: 2122 gram), and the inability to meet basic needs.
  • Alleviation programmes include social safety nets, old age allowances, and microcredit support.
  • Organisations like BRAC, ASA, Proshika, and Grameen Bank work extensively on rural development and poverty reduction.

BCC-210 Q4(b)

8th Five-Year Plan of Bangladesh:

  • The 8th Five-Year Plan (2020–2025) targets a GDP growth of 8.51% by 2025 and aims to reach a GNI per capita of $3,106.
  • Major targets include employing 11.33 million people, reducing the poverty rate to 15.6%, and achieving 100% electrification.

BCC-210 Q4(c)

Microcredit and NGOs:

  • Microcredit provides small loans to the poor without collateral, mainly through institutions like Grameen Bank and various NGOs.
  • It is widely considered an effective tool for reducing poverty in Bangladesh by supporting self-employment.
  • NGO activities include providing health services, education, and promoting rural development.

BCC-210 Q5(a)

Schumpeter Model of Development:

  • Schumpeter argues that development is driven by "innovations," which are spontaneous changes that displace previous equilibrium states.
  • These innovations are carried out by entrepreneurs and are often financed through bank credit.
  • The process is characterised by "circular flow" and cyclical movements.

BCC-210 Q5(b)

Big Push Theory:

  • States that a high minimum amount of investment is required to overcome development obstacles in an underdeveloped economy.
  • It is needed in less developed countries to achieve external economies through the simultaneous establishment of technically interdependent industries.

BCC-210 Q5(c)

Balanced and Unbalanced Growth:

  • Balanced growth involves simultaneous investment in all sectors to ensure harmony.
  • Unbalanced growth suggests strategic investment in selected sectors to create new opportunities.
  • For a developing country like Bangladesh, unbalanced growth is often supported as it recognises resource scarcities and utilises "linkages" to propel development.

BCC-210 Q6(a)

Capital Formation:

  • Capital formation increases the stock of physical and human resources, enabling expanded future output. It raises national income and promotes technological progress.
  • Domestic sources include savings drives and fiscal measures. External sources include foreign aid and investment.

BCC-210 Q6(b)

Demographic Transition Theory:

  • Describes stages of population growth: Stage I has high birth/death rates; Stage II (Population Explosion) has high birth but falling death rates; Stage III has low rates.
  • Criticism includes its failure to account for specific social conditions and the role of state policy in reducing fertility.
  • Bangladesh is currently in a transitional phase (Stage II) with a declining death rate but relatively stable birth rate.

BCC-210 Q6(c)

Agricultural Economy and Social Progress:

  • A strong agricultural economy provides food for an expanding population, earns foreign exchange, and increases rural incomes, which stimulates industrial demand.
  • It is a prerequisite for economic development and social transformation, especially in rural areas, making the statement agreeable.

BCC-210 Q7(a)

Developed vs. Developing Countries and Lessons:

  • Developed countries have high standards of living, low poverty, and high industrialization, while developing countries face rapid population growth, capital deficiency, and low productivity.
  • Lessons for Bangladesh include focusing on fiscal and market institutions before spending needs arise and using government spending to advance development even at low levels.

BCC-210 Q7(b)

Traditional Theory of Free Trade:

  • Free trade allows countries to specialise based on comparative advantage, leading to direct benefits like larger manufactured imports and indirect benefits like checking inefficient monopolies.
  • It serves as an "engine of growth" by enlarging consumption capacities.

BCC-210 Q7(c)

Economic Integration:

  • Economic integration is encouraged through trade liberalisation and regional institutions.
  • Benefits include economies of scale, expansion of markets, and trade creation.
  • Problems include political differences, administrative hurdles, and uneven distribution of benefits.
