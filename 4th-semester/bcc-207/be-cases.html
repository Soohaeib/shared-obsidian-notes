How to Use This Handout This handout contains 20 original case studies covering all 10 chapters of BCC 207. Each case is followed by three discussion questions designed to help you practice applying theoretical frameworks to real-world business ethics dilemmas — exactly the skill required in Section (c) of the final examination. Recommended approach: (1) Read the case without looking at the questions. (2) Identify the ethical issues independently. (3) Decide which theory or concept best applies. (4) Then read the questions and draft your response. Cases 1–19 are aligned to specific chapters. Case 20 is an integrative case drawing on all chapters — similar in scope to a comprehensive final examination question.
01 The Milk Vendor's Choice Topic: Chapter 1: Introduction to Business Ethics Case 1 of 20
Case Scenario
Karim sells fresh milk in a small rural market in Sylhet. During a prolonged drought, supply falls sharply, and costs rise. A dealer offers Karim adulterated milk—diluted with water and containing artificial whiteners—at half the usual price. No local regulation explicitly prohibits this practice. Karim's competitors have already started selling the adulterated product and are earning higher margins. Many of his customers are low-income families with young children who depend on the milk as a primary protein source. Karim must decide whether to switch to the cheaper, adulterated supply to stay competitive.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Using the relationship between law and ethics as discussed in class, explain whether Karim's potential decision would be legal but unethical, or both legal and ethical. Justify your reasoning. Q2. How does individual morality differ from professional ethics in this scenario? Which do you think should guide Karim's decision, and why? Q3. What stakeholders are affected by Karim's decision? List them and briefly explain the impact on each.

Key Concepts: [Law vs. Ethics] [Individual Morality] [Ethical Behaviour]

02 The Sustainability Trade-Off at GreenPack Ltd. Topic: Chapter 1: Sustainability & Globalisation Case 2 of 20
Case Scenario
GreenPack Ltd. is a Bangladeshi packaging company supplying materials to garment exporters. To reduce costs, management proposes switching from biodegradable packaging to cheaper single-use plastic, which is not yet banned under existing regulations. The CFO calculates this will save BDT 3 crore per year and protect jobs during a slow market. However, the company's own sustainability report pledges commitment to a 'zero-plastic' target by 2027. An environmental CSO has already begun monitoring GreenPack's discharge into a nearby river.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Evaluate GreenPack's proposed decision using the Triple Bottom Line (TBL) framework—considering environmental, economic, and social dimensions. Q2. Does the absence of a legal prohibition make the switch to single-use plastic ethically acceptable? Why or why not? Q3. How should GreenPack balance its profitability needs with its stated sustainability commitments? Suggest a practical ethical course of action.

Key Concepts: [Triple Bottom Line] [Sustainability] [Stakeholder Impact]

03 The Consequentialist Manager Topic: Chapter 2: Theories of Business Ethics Case 3 of 20
Case Scenario
Farhan is the general manager of a textile factory in Gazipur employing 800 workers. Due to a global slowdown in orders, the factory is running at 40% capacity. Farhan has two options: (A) Retain all 800 workers at reduced hours and half-pay, keeping families afloat but risking the company's cash reserves; or (B) Immediately lay off 400 workers, paying the legally required severance, which stabilises finances and protects the remaining 400 jobs long-term. A local NGO is watching the factory closely for any worker rights violations.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Apply utilitarian reasoning to evaluate both options. Which option produces the 'greatest good for the greatest number'? Show your analysis clearly. Q2. A strict utilitarian might justify Option B. Do you think this is ethically satisfactory? What does this reveal about the limitations of Consequentialist theory? Q3. How might a Kantian (duty-based) ethicist evaluate Farhan's decision differently from a utilitarian?

Key Concepts: [Consequentialism] [Utilitarianism] [Stakeholder Welfare]

04 The Gift or the Bribe? Topic: Chapter 2: Theories of Business Ethics Case 4 of 20
Case Scenario
Tania is a procurement officer at a private university in Dhaka. A stationery supplier sends her a personal gift—a gold bracelet worth BDT 25,000—at Eid, along with a note expressing hope for continued business. The supplier's pricing is competitive, but not the lowest available. The university has no formal gift policy. Tania is tempted to keep the gift as it is given during a festive season, telling herself it is a cultural norm and does not obligate her to favour the supplier in future tenders. Her colleague, however, immediately disclosed a similar gift and returned it.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Apply Kant's Categorical Imperative to Tania's situation: could the act of keeping the gift be 'universalised' without contradiction? What would Kant conclude? Q2. How does this scenario illustrate the difference between egoism and duty-based ethics? Which framework best explains Tania's rationalisation? Q3. If Tania keeps the gift but genuinely awards the contract on merit, has she acted unethically? Explain with reference to at least one ethical theory.

Key Concepts: [Kantian Ethics] [Moral Duties] [Egoism vs. Universalisability]

05 Virtue at the Top — or Just Optics? Topic: Chapter 2: Theories of Business Ethics Case 5 of 20
Case Scenario
Rina has recently been appointed CEO of MediCare Bangladesh, a pharmaceutical distributor. She launches a high-profile 'Ethics First' campaign, publishing a code of conduct and hosting town halls about integrity. However, employees privately observe that Rina routinely fast-tracks contracts for suppliers linked to her personal network, dismisses junior staff concerns without due process, and responds to whistleblowers with reassignments. Publicly, Rina is praised as a progressive leader. Internally, morale is declining, and key staff are considering resignation.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Using Virtue Ethics, evaluate whether Rina's behaviour demonstrates genuine virtue or merely the appearance of ethical leadership. What virtues are missing in her conduct? Q2. Feminist ethics emphasises relationships, care, and voice. How does Rina's treatment of junior employees and whistleblowers violate these principles? Q3. What is the difference between compliance-oriented ethics management and values-oriented ethics management? Which does Rina's organisation exemplify, and what should change?

Key Concepts: [Virtue Ethics] [Feminist Ethics] [Character vs. Compliance]

06 The Promotion Denied Topic: Chapter 3 / Chapter 6: Ethical Decision-Making & Employee Ethics Case 6 of 20
Case Scenario
Dilnoza has worked for five years as a senior analyst at an investment firm in Dhaka. She is consistently rated as a high performer. When a Head of Department role opens, she is overlooked in favour of a less experienced male colleague. Her manager privately tells her that 'clients prefer dealing with a man in senior roles' and that her recent marriage 'might affect her availability.' Dilnoza discovers that two other women have been passed over in similar circumstances in the past three years. She is considering filing a formal complaint but fears retaliation.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Identify the ethical issues in this scenario, classifying them under individual, organisational, and societal dimensions of ethical decision-making. Q2. How do situational factors (manager's attitude, organisational culture, client expectations) influence whether discrimination becomes normalised in an organisation? Q3. What rights of Dilnoza as an employee are being violated? What should the firm do to address systemic discrimination? Reference the course material on affirmative action.

Key Concepts: [Ethical Decision-Making] [Workplace Discrimination] [Situational Influence]

07 The Whistleblower's Dilemma Topic: Chapter 3: Making Decisions in Business Ethics Case 7 of 20
Case Scenario
Shaheen works as an accountant at a large construction company in Chittagong. During a routine audit, he discovers that senior management has been systematically inflating project costs to skim funds—approximately BDT 1.5 crore over two years—which he suspects are being siphoned through a shell company. Shaheen's direct supervisor is implicated. He has three choices: (1) report internally through HR, knowing his supervisor controls his performance review; (2) report to the Anti-Corruption Commission (ACC); or (3) remain silent to protect his job and family income.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Walk through an ethical decision-making model step by step as Shaheen would apply it to his situation. What does the model suggest he should do? Q2. How do individual factors (personal ethics, financial pressure, loyalty) and situational factors (power dynamics, lack of whistleblower protection) interact to complicate Shaheen's decision? Q3. Evaluate each of Shaheen's three options from the perspectives of at least two ethical theories. Which option is most defensible, and what risks does it carry?

Key Concepts: [Ethical Decision-Making Models] [Individual Moral Development] [Whistleblowing]

08 Managing Ethics in a Garment Factory Topic: Chapter 4: Managing Business Ethics Case 8 of 20
Case Scenario
SafeWear Ltd. is a mid-sized garment factory in Narayanganj that exports to European brands. Following a fire safety incident two years ago, it introduced a formal code of conduct, compliance training, and a grievance hotline. However, factory floor supervisors continue to pressurise workers not to report overtime violations, senior managers override safety protocols to meet shipment deadlines, and the grievance hotline has never received a single call—despite documented complaints reaching the CSO that oversees the factory. An audit is due next month from a European client.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Distinguish between compliance-oriented, values-oriented, and external-orientation approaches to ethics management. Which best describes SafeWear's current approach, and what are its weaknesses? Q2. What does the silence of the grievance hotline tell us about the gap between formal ethics programs and informal organisational culture? How should management bridge this gap? Q3. What role can ethical leadership play in transforming SafeWear's ethical culture before and after the audit? What specific actions would you recommend to the MD?

Key Concepts: [Codes of Ethics] [Formal vs. Informal Ethics Management] [Ethical Culture]

09 The Hostile Takeover of Sunrise Foods Topic: Chapter 5: Shareholders and Business Ethics Case 9 of 20
Case Scenario
Sunrise Foods is a listed Bangladeshi food company with a loyal workforce of 3,000 and strong community ties in Rajshahi, where its main plant is located. A large conglomerate, MegaCorp Holdings, has launched a hostile takeover bid, offering shareholders a 40% premium above the current share price. Sunrise's board has rejected the offer, arguing it undervalues the firm's long-term potential and will lead to layoffs and factory relocation. MegaCorp is now approaching individual shareholders directly. Several institutional investors are inclined to accept the premium immediately.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Explain the agency relationship in this case. Are the Sunrise board members acting in the best interests of shareholders, or are they protecting their own positions? How can we tell? Q2. Critically assess the ethical issues raised by hostile takeovers, drawing on at least three dimensions: employee welfare, shareholder rights, and community impact. Q3. If you were an institutional shareholder, how would you evaluate the ethical dimensions of accepting or rejecting the takeover offer? What additional information would you need?

Key Concepts: [Corporate Governance] [Hostile Takeover] [Shareholder Rights] [Agency Problem]

10 The Insider at Dhaka Exchange Topic: Chapter 5: Shareholders and Business Ethics Case 10 of 20
Case Scenario
Selim is a senior executive at a publicly listed bank in Bangladesh. Three weeks before the bank officially announces a major non-performing loan (NPL) crisis that will significantly reduce quarterly earnings, Selim sells all his personal shares in the bank, as well as advising his brother-in-law to do the same. After the announcement, the share price falls by 28%. The Bangladesh Securities and Exchange Commission (BSEC) begins an investigation. Selim claims his share sale was coincidental—'part of a pre-planned portfolio rebalancing'—and denies discussing any bank business with his brother-in-law.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Using the four ethical dimensions of insider trading covered in class (fairness, misappropriation of property, harm to investors, undermining relationships), evaluate Selim's conduct. Q2. How does insider trading violate the principles of corporate governance and the duties owed by executives to shareholders and the market? Q3. If Selim's claim of 'pre-planned rebalancing' were true, would that change the ethical assessment? Discuss the role of intent versus outcome in evaluating this type of financial misconduct.

Key Concepts: [Insider Trading] [Fairness] [Financial Market Ethics]

11 The Social Media Monitor Topic: Chapter 6: Employees and Business Ethics Case 11 of 20
Case Scenario
TechStart Bangladesh, a growing IT startup in Dhaka, has implemented software that monitors all employee computer activity during and after office hours on company devices—including personal social media usage, messaging apps, and browser history. The policy is mentioned in a 47-page employment contract that employees signed at onboarding but was never verbally explained. When a developer, Rimi, privately posted on Facebook criticising the company's unpaid overtime culture (without naming the employer), she was called into HR and warned that her post had been flagged. Rimi was not dismissed but felt deeply uncomfortable continuing to work there.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Using the four dimensions of employee privacy (physical, social, informational, and psychological), analyse the extent to which TechStart's monitoring policy is ethically justified or problematic. Q2. Under what conditions can an employer legitimately monitor employee digital activity? Where should the ethical boundary be drawn between company security and employee privacy? Q3. Does the fact that Rimi signed a contract that mentioned the policy make TechStart's conduct ethically acceptable? What principles of informed consent are relevant here?

Key Concepts: [Employee Privacy] [Electronic Surveillance] [Psychological Privacy]

12 Fair Wages at the Bottom of the Chain Topic: Chapter 6: Employees and Business Ethics Case 12 of 20
Case Scenario
A European sportswear brand sources products from a supplier in Bangladesh. The supplier's workers earn the legal minimum wage—currently BDT 12,500 per month—but a labour rights study calculates that a 'living wage' in Dhaka, accounting for food, rent, transport, and education costs, is approximately BDT 20,000. The supplier argues it cannot unilaterally raise wages without raising prices to the European buyer, who is under pressure from its own shareholders to control costs. Workers, mostly women, regularly work unpaid overtime to meet targets. The brand publicly markets itself as 'committed to ethical labour standards.'
Discussion Questions
Q1. Distinguish between the legal minimum wage and a living wage. What ethical obligations, if any, do multinational buyers have toward workers in their supply chains? Q2. Evaluate the claim that 'paying the legal minimum is sufficient' from the perspectives of Utilitarianism, Kantian Ethics, and the rights-based approach to employee ethics. Q3. How does globalisation create an ethical race to the bottom in labour standards? What mechanisms—regulatory, market-based, or civil society—could address this problem?

Key Concepts: [Fair Wages] [Employee Rights] [Globalisation and Labour Ethics]

13 Misleading Marketing at NutriPlus Topic: Chapter 7: Consumers and Business Ethics Case 13 of 20
Case Scenario
NutriPlus is a Bangladeshi food company that markets a powdered drink under the tagline 'Clinically Proven to Boost Children's Immunity.' The product's actual ingredient list shows it contains mainly sugar and artificial flavouring, with trace amounts of vitamin C. Independent testing by a consumer association found no credible clinical trial supporting the marketing claim. The product targets lower-middle-income parents who spend a significant portion of their food budget on it, believing it to be medicinal. The Directorate General of Drug Administration has not yet acted on the consumer association's complaint.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Identify the ethical issues in NutriPlus's marketing strategy from the perspective of consumer rights and marketing ethics as discussed in the course. Q2. How does this case challenge the concept of 'consumer sovereignty'—the idea that informed consumers can protect themselves through their purchasing choices? Q3. What responsibilities do corporations, regulators, and civil society organisations each bear in preventing deceptive marketing targeted at vulnerable consumer groups?

Key Concepts: [Consumer Rights] [Marketing Ethics] [Consumer Sovereignty]

14 The Robi–Airtel Merger Revisited Topic: Chapter 7 / Chapter 5: Competitors, Mergers & Business Ethics Case 14 of 20
Case Scenario
In 2016, Robi Axiata and Airtel Bangladesh merged to form a single entity retaining the Robi brand. The combined company became the second-largest mobile operator in Bangladesh. Following the merger, approximately 800 staff redundancies were announced. The new entity also renegotiated supplier contracts downward, citing economies of scale, which caused financial distress for several small tech vendors. On the other hand, network coverage expanded to rural areas, data prices fell, and the combined entity invested in 4G infrastructure. Consumer groups offered mixed reactions.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Using the ethical framework on mergers and acquisitions from the course, analyse whether the Robi–Airtel merger can be considered ethically justified despite the job losses and supplier impacts. Q2. How do the interests of shareholders, employees, suppliers, consumers, and the broader public conflict in this case? Which stakeholder group's interests should take priority, and on what ethical grounds? Q3. What conditions or safeguards should regulators impose on corporate mergers to ensure they remain ethically acceptable and do not simply serve the interests of acquiring shareholders?

Key Concepts: [Mergers & Acquisitions] [Competition Ethics] [Stakeholder Impact]

15 The Price-Fixing Cartel Topic: Chapter 8: Suppliers, Competitors and Business Ethics Case 15 of 20
Case Scenario
Three major pharmaceutical distributors in Bangladesh—informally referred to as the 'Big Three'—have been meeting quarterly for the past four years. During these meetings, they agree to divide regional markets, fix minimum prices for certain essential medicines, and boycott any hospital that negotiates directly with overseas manufacturers. An investigative journalist uncovers email evidence of these arrangements. The distributors argue their cooperation 'stabilises the market' and prevents 'destructive price wars.' Patients in affected areas pay 15–20% more than the market rate for essential medications.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Explain the concept of a cartel and why it raises ethical concerns beyond its illegality. How does this case illustrate the problems of insufficient competition? Q2. Who bears the cost of this price-fixing arrangement, and how does it violate the ethical rights of consumers, hospitals, and smaller competitors? Q3. What would an ethically defensible industry coordination arrangement look like, as opposed to a cartel? Where is the line between legitimate business cooperation and unethical collusion?

Key Concepts: [Competition Ethics] [Insufficient/Aggressive Competition] [Conflict of Interests]

16 Grameen Bank and the CSO Partnership Topic: Chapter 9: Civil Society and Business Ethics Case 16 of 20
Case Scenario
A leading Bangladeshi microfinance institution (MFI) enters into a partnership with an international NGO focused on women's empowerment. The NGO provides legitimacy and global funding access, while the MFI provides branch networks and credit delivery. After two years, internal documents reveal that loan officers are pressuring rural women to take multiple loans to meet portfolio growth targets, resulting in over-indebtedness in some communities. The international NGO is aware of the complaints but delays raising them publicly, fearing it will jeopardise the partnership and its own donor funding.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Identify the ethical issues arising in this business–CSO collaboration from the perspective of both the MFI and the NGO. Q2. What does this case reveal about the risks of co-opting CSO partners and the challenge of maintaining CSO independence and accountability? Q3. Referring to the Accountability Charter for International NGOs discussed in class, which principles is the NGO violating in this case, and what should it do?

Key Concepts: [CSO as Stakeholders] [Business–CSO Collaboration] [Civil Society Accountability]

17 The Environmental Activist vs. the Brick Kiln Topic: Chapter 9: Civil Society and Business Ethics Case 17 of 20
Case Scenario
A community-based environmental group in Bogura is campaigning against a cluster of brick kilns that operate near agricultural land and a river, emitting black smoke and discharging waste water. The group has submitted formal complaints to the Department of Environment (DoE), organised a petition signed by 5,000 residents, and staged peaceful demonstrations. The kilns are technically operating within outdated permissible emission limits set in 1997. The kiln owners argue they provide employment for 1,200 seasonal workers and pay taxes. A more aggressive faction of the group is threatening to block kiln deliveries.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Classify the civil society tactics used by the environmental group into direct, indirect, violent, and non-violent categories as discussed in the course. Which tactics are ethically defensible? Q2. The kilns are legal. Does legal compliance remove the kiln owners' ethical obligation to address community concerns? What ethical responsibilities do businesses have toward civil society? Q3. How should the kiln owners, local government, and the CSO negotiate a resolution that respects both livelihood concerns and environmental sustainability?

Key Concepts: [CSO Tactics] [Business and Sustainability] [Civil Society Pressure]

18 Government Contracts and Political Connections Topic: Chapter 10: Government, Regulations and Business Ethics Case 18 of 20
Case Scenario
BuildRight Ltd. is a construction company in Bangladesh that wins a disproportionately high number of government infrastructure contracts. An investigative report reveals that BuildRight's chairman is the brother-in-law of a senior planning ministry official. Competitors argue the tender evaluation process is opaque and that BuildRight's bids are never the lowest. A World Bank-funded project has flagged BuildRight for review after a road it built deteriorated within 18 months of construction. BuildRight's management maintains that it 'follows all procurement rules' and attributes its success to 'superior relationships and experience.'
Discussion Questions
Q1. How does this case illustrate the ethical issues in business–government relations discussed in the course? Distinguish between legal compliance and ethical behaviour in this context. Q2. Explain the concept of 'regulatory capture.' To what extent does this case suggest that the regulatory environment has been captured by business interests? Q3. What reforms in public procurement ethics and corporate citizenship standards would reduce the likelihood of such arrangements? Draw on concepts from the course.

Key Concepts: [Business–Government Relations] [Corporate Citizenship] [Regulatory Capture]

19 Tax Ethics and the Phantom Profit Topic: Chapter 10: Government, Regulations and Business Ethics Case 19 of 20
Case Scenario
DigiGlobe Bangladesh Ltd. is the local arm of a Singapore-based e-commerce group. It generates over BDT 800 crore in annual revenue from Bangladeshi consumers but reports a local taxable profit of only BDT 12 crore, thanks to a system of royalty payments to its Singapore parent for 'use of brand and technology.' The effective tax paid in Bangladesh is BDT 3.6 crore, an effective rate of under 0.5% of revenue. The National Board of Revenue (NBR) has no specific legislation addressing this type of transfer pricing. Locally, DigiGlobe employs 2,000 people and is a major digital economy player.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Is DigiGlobe's tax strategy ethical? Use the distinction between 'letter of the law' and 'spirit of the law' to analyse its conduct. Q2. From the perspective of corporate citizenship, what obligations does DigiGlobe have toward Bangladesh as a society and government stakeholder—beyond legal tax compliance? Q3. What regulatory mechanisms should the Bangladeshi government introduce to address this type of base erosion? What ethical arguments could DigiGlobe use to resist or accept such regulations?

Key Concepts: [Corporate Citizenship] [Tax Ethics] [Business vs. Public Interest]

20 The Ethical Supply Chain: Integrative Case Topic: Integrative — All Chapters Case 20 of 20
Case Scenario
ThreadRight International is a UK-based fashion retailer sourcing 70% of its garments from Bangladesh. A joint investigation by an NGO and a journalist reveals: (1) its main supplier pays workers BDT 11,500/month—below the living wage; (2) a subsidiary supplier uses child labour for embroidery sub-contracting; (3) ThreadRight's own packaging supplier in Bangladesh dumps chemical dye waste into a local canal; (4) ThreadRight's Bangladesh Country Manager accepted gifts from suppliers in exchange for preferred vendor status; and (5) the company's published Sustainability Report claims it has 'zero tolerance for labour violations and environmental harm.' ThreadRight's share price drops 18% in one week following media coverage. The Bangladesh government threatens to revoke ThreadRight's preferential trade status.
Discussion Questions
Q1. Identify at least five distinct ethical failures in this case, linking each to a specific topic from the BCC 207 course syllabus (e.g., employee rights, corporate governance, CSO accountability, marketing ethics, government relations). Q2. As ThreadRight's newly appointed Head of Ethics and Compliance, prepare a prioritised action plan addressing the most urgent ethical issues. Justify your priorities using ethical theory. Q3. This case involves shareholders, employees, consumers, suppliers, CSOs, and government as stakeholders. Using a stakeholder ethics framework, explain whose interests should take precedence and how ThreadRight should balance competing claims to restore trust.
