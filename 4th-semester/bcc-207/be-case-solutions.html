Case 1: The Milk Vendor's Choice

Q1. Relationship between Law and Ethics

  • Legal but Unethical Choice: Karim's potential decision to switch to an adulterated milk supply would be legal under local market parameters but deeply unethical.
  • Law vs. Ethics Divide: Law represents the minimum standard of behavior enforced by a state through explicit rules, whereas ethics represents broader normative principles of right and wrong. Because there is no local regulation explicitly prohibiting this specific adulteration practice, the act does not violate statutory law.
  • Ethical Failure: The action is unethical because it relies on active deception (using whiteners to mask dilution) and inflicts direct physical harm on vulnerable consumers. Legality is not a reliable guide for ethical acceptability.
  • Conclusion: Karim's potential action falls into the "legal but unethical" quadrant of business activities, demonstrating that complying with the law alone is insufficient for responsible commerce.

Q2. Individual Morality vs. Professional Ethics

  • Individual Morality: This encompasses a person’s private conscience, religious convictions, and personal value systems regarding right and wrong. Individually, Karim likely feels personal guilt about selling low-quality, manipulated products to his neighbors.
  • Professional Ethics: This defines the specific moral obligations, standards, and roles an economic actor assumes when serving the public. A professional vendor owes an absolute duty of care to provide safe, non-hazardous goods and maintain transparency with customers.
  • Guiding Framework: Professional ethics must guide Karim's decision. While individual morality alerts his conscience, professional business ethics codifies his structural responsibility as a market participant to protect consumer safety and reject deceptive practices, even when market competitors normalize them.
  • Conclusion: Professional ethics should be the primary driver, holding Karim to the standard of market integrity and product safety over short-term financial pressures.

Q3. Affected Stakeholders and Impacts

  • Low-Income Families and Children: They suffer the most severe negative impact. They face health hazards from chemical whiteners and suffer from malnutrition due to purchasing watered-down milk under false pretenses.
  • Karim (The Business Owner): Choosing the adulterated supply saves his business from short-term financial distress but destroys his long-term asset—customer trust, reputation, and personal integrity.
  • Competitors: If Karim maintains high standards, competitors retain an unfair cost advantage; if Karim capitulates, it drives a "race to the bottom" that ruins the entire local market’s integrity.
  • Local Market/Community: Systemic trust in the marketplace collapses, driving up healthcare risks and creating public skepticism toward essential food sellers.
  • Conclusion: Karim’s choice carries multi-stakeholder consequences where a purely economic decision transfers severe physical and financial damage onto the most vulnerable community members.

Case 2: The Sustainability Trade-Off at GreenPack Ltd.

Q1. Triple Bottom Line (TBL) Evaluation

The Triple Bottom Line framework requires evaluating organizational choices across Economic (Profit), Social (People), and Environmental (Planet) performance vectors.

  • Economic Dimension (Profit): The proposal yields a strong short-term positive impact, generating BDT 3 crore in annual savings and securing financial stability during a slow market.
  • Social Dimension (People): The social impact is conflicted. It protects immediate employee jobs through cost reduction. However, it inflicts long-term harm on the surrounding community by degrading public water systems and violates customer trust by discarding its stated mission.
  • Environmental Dimension (Planet): The environmental impact is entirely negative. Reverting to single-use plastics pollutes local rivers and completely contradicts the company's public commitment to a "zero-plastic" target by 2027.
  • Conclusion: Under a true TBL model, the irreversible environmental degradation and loss of stakeholder trust far outweigh the immediate economic benefit of BDT 3 crore, making the switch unsustainable.

Q2. Legality vs. Ethical Acceptability

  • Regulatory Insufficiency: The absence of a formal regulatory ban on single-use plastic does not render the decision ethically acceptable.
  • Breach of Voluntary Commitment: GreenPack publicly issued a Sustainability Report committing to a "zero-plastic" target by 2027. Reversing this choice constitutes corporate hypocrisy, violating principles of transparency, stakeholder trust, and integrity.
  • Negative Externalities: Exploiting weak local enforcement to dump environmental cleanup costs onto the public river system for private financial preservation is an unethical displacement of corporate responsibility.
  • Conclusion: A lack of legal constraints does not authorize unethical behavior; corporations are morally bound to hold to their published ethical targets even when laws lag behind.

Q3. Balancing Profitability and Sustainability

  • Phased Materials Strategy: GreenPack should reject the plastic pivot and instead adopt a phased cost-containment model, maintaining biodegradable packaging while streamlining other internal operating expenses.
  • Green Premium Restructuring: Management should leverage its sustainability commitments to negotiate long-term supply relationships or a modest "eco-premium" with its garment export clients who also face international sustainability scrutiny.
  • CSO Partnership: Engage the monitoring environmental CSO to co-audit waste processes, transforming an external monitoring threat into a collaborative transparency initiative that enhances brand equity.
  • Conclusion: GreenPack can protect its long-term market valuation by standing firm on its 2027 target and working with green exporters to co-fund eco-friendly operations.

Case 3: The Consequentialist Manager

Q1. Utilitarian Evaluation of Options

Utilitarianism requires choices that maximize overall systemic utility, producing the greatest net good for the greatest number of affected stakeholders.

  • Option A Analysis (Retain all 800 workers at half-pay):
    • Benefits: Prevents immediate unemployment shocks for all 800 workers, keeping families afloat with base sustenance earnings.
    • Harms: Risks the complete cash reserves of the firm, threatening total bankruptcy and 100% job losses if the market downturn persists.
  • Option B Analysis (Lay off 400 workers with severance):
    • Benefits: Immediately stabilizes corporate liquidity, guaranteeing long-term job security and full pay for the remaining 400 workers.
    • Harms: Drives 400 families into severe, immediate economic distress, only minimally offset by standard severance.
  • The Utilitarian Selection: Option B is mathematically preferred under strict utilitarianism. While Option A avoids immediate pain, it creates a high-probability risk of complete organizational collapse (losing 800 jobs). Option B ensures the certain survival of 50% of the system, minimizing total net systemic harm.
  • Conclusion: Option B generates the highest net systemic utility by choosing certain survival for half the enterprise over a high-risk gamble that could ruin the entire workforce.

Q2. Limitations of Consequentialist Theory

  • Incomplete Moral Satisfaction: Pure reliance on Option B is ethically problematic because it treats human beings as disposable corporate inputs and financial metrics rather than moral agents.
  • Quantification Flaw: Utilitarianism struggle to objectively measure and weigh qualitative human suffering, family disruption, and long-term poverty against liquid corporate cash reserves.
  • Tyranny of the Majority: This reveals a major flaw in consequentialism: it permits sacrificing the fundamental rights and welfare of a minority group (the 400 terminated workers) to preserve the comfort and utility of the majority.
  • Conclusion: The case demonstrates that pure utilitarianism can justify harsh, indifferent outcomes, showing its limitation in protecting individual human dignity against numerical cost-benefit analyses.

Q3. Kantian (Duty-Based) Evaluation vs. Utilitarianism

  • The Formula of Humanity: Kantian ethics completely rejects sacrificing individuals for a collective calculation. Deontology requires that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or others, always as an end in itself and never merely as a means to an end.
  • Kantian Assessment of Option B: Terminating 400 workers simply to balance financial statements treats those employees as disposable tools for corporate preservation, which is a direct violation of their inherent moral worth.
  • Kantian Assessment of Option A: Option A respects the organization's moral obligation to the entire workforce by sharing the economic burden equitably across all members rather than sacrificing a portion to protect the rest.
  • Conclusion: While a utilitarian prioritizes the economic survival outcome (Option B), a Kantian focuses on the moral purity of the action and duty to equity, firmly endorsing Option A or a non-sacrificial alternative.

Case 4: The Gift or the Bribe?

Q1. Kant's Categorical Imperative Analysis

Kant’s Categorical Imperative dictates that an action is morally permissible only if its underlying maxim can be universalized as a law for everyone without creating a contradiction.

  • Formulating the Maxim: Tania's choice operates on the maxim: "A procurement officer may accept expensive personal items from active suppliers during holiday seasons, provided there is no formal company policy against it and no explicit promise of favoritism."
  • Universalisation Test: If this maxim became universal law, every purchasing officer globally would accept high-value items from competitive bidders. This would systematically undermine institutional objectivity, fair competition, and vendor independence. The concept of unbiased procurement would collapse into systemic corruption, making the entire tender process a contradiction.
  • Kant's Conclusion: Because universalizing Tania’s maxim logically destroys the integrity of the procurement system itself, it fails the contradiction test. Kant would conclude that Tania has a strict duty to refuse the gold bracelet.

Q2. Egoism vs. Duty-Based Ethics

  • Ethical Egoism: This framework claims that an agent’s primary moral duty is to maximize their own long-term self-interest and personal gain.
  • Duty-Based Ethics: This framework asserts that actions are inherently right or wrong based on adherence to universal moral obligations, regardless of personal outcomes or rewards.
  • Tania's Alignment: Tania's behavior is rooted in Egoism. She rationalizes retaining a BDT 25,000 personal asset by hiding behind cultural holiday traditions and the absence of clear rules, prioritizing her own material gain over professional neutrality.
  • Conclusion: Tania's excuses reflect egoistic self-interest, whereas her colleague's immediate disclosure and return of the gift show duty-based ethics, prioritizing organizational integrity over personal profit.

Q3. Unethical Behavior Despite Merit-Based Award

  • Compromised Objectivity: Tania acts unethically even if she awards the contract strictly on merit.
  • Conflict of Interest Creation: Accepting an expensive luxury item from a supplier whose bid is not the cheapest creates an unmapped conflict of interest. It establishes a psychological expectation of reciprocity, compromising the professional independence required of her office.
  • Deontological Intent: In duty-based ethics, the moral value of an action is judged by the intent and adherence to duty, not the eventual outcome. Retaining the bribe-adjacent gift violates her core professional duty of absolute neutrality, even if the final contract choice coincidentally aligns with merit.
  • Conclusion: Retaining the gift is fundamentally unethical because it introduces bias and compromises institutional trust, independent of the final vendor selection.

Case 5: Virtue at the Top — or Just Optics?

Q1. Virtue Ethics Evaluation of Rina

Virtue Ethics evaluates actions based on the character and motivations of the individual, stating that true ethical leadership must stem from authentic internal virtues like honesty, justice, and integrity.

  • Superficial Leadership: Rina’s conduct proves that her "Ethics First" campaign is merely a public relations tool used to project a progressive corporate image, entirely separated from her true personal character.
  • Deficient Virtues:
    • Integrity (Consistency): She exhibits a total disconnect between public statements and private administrative choices.
    • Fairness and Justice: She nepotistically fast-tracks contracts for personal associates while denying junior employees standard due process.
    • Moral Courage: She punishes whistleblowers with reassignments to suppress criticism instead of directly addressing operational faults.
  • Conclusion: Rina lacks genuine virtue; her leadership relies on the deceptive appearance of ethics, which destroys internal organizational health for external praise.

Q2. Feminist Ethics Analysis

Feminist Ethics (or the Ethics of Care) rejects cold hierarchies and instead prioritizes empathy, the preservation of healthy relationships, protection of vulnerable stakeholders, and ensuring all members have an active voice.

  • Silencing the Vulnerable: Rina directly violates these tenets by dismissing junior staff concerns without proper consideration, effectively removing their voice within the organization.
  • Relational Retaliation: Reassigning whistleblowers replaces an environment of care and psychological safety with fear-based compliance and punitive power dynamics.
  • Systemic Disruption: The drop in internal morale and subsequent staff resignations prove that her lack of empathy and relational care causes severe, tangible damage to the human fabric of the company.
  • Conclusion: Rina’s top-down, punitive management methods violate feminist ethics by breaking organizational trust and aggressively silencing vulnerable employees.

Q3. Compliance-Oriented vs. Values-Oriented Management

  • Compliance-Oriented Approach: Focuses heavily on preventing, detecting, and punishing legal or procedural violations via strict rules and mechanisms. It is typically driven by fear of external penalties or bad publicity.
  • Values-Oriented Approach: Centers on defining core ethical aspirations and building a shared internal commitment to values like honesty and fairness.
  • MediCare Bangladesh's Current State: Under Rina, the firm uses a superficial compliance-oriented facade. The code of conduct is deployed purely for public relations protection while the actual corporate culture remains corrupt.
  • Recommended Action Plan:
    • Independent Reporting Channels: Move whistleblower hotlines completely outside Rina’s chain of command to an independent ombudsman to prevent executive retaliation.
    • External Procurement Audits: Eliminate contract fast-tracking by introducing mandatory independent audits for all major supplier relationships.
    • Cultural Metric Realignment: Restructure corporate governance so that executive bonuses and performance evaluations are tied directly to anonymous internal culture feedback and morale metrics.
  • Conclusion: MediCare Bangladesh must move away from public relations compliance, strip unilateral power from its executive layer, and establish independent safeguards to build an authentic, values-oriented culture.

Case 6: The Promotion Denied

Q1. Dimensions of Ethical Decision-Making

  • Individual Dimension: The direct manager holds personal biases, stereotypes, and assumptions regarding the commitment, focus, and availability of married women in high-pressure roles.
  • Organisational Dimension: The investment firm lacks transparent, standardized promotion criteria. A culture of unaccountable decision-making exists, evidenced by two other qualified women being systematically passed over within a three-year period.
  • Societal Dimension: Structural gender inequality and traditional patriarchal expectations persist in the broader professional environment, reinforcing the biased view that senior leadership roles belong to men.
  • Conclusion: Dilnoza’s case is not an isolated incident; it represents a multi-layered ethical failure where individual bias is enabled by organizational silence and societal stereotypes.

Q2. Normalisation of Discrimination via Situational Factors

  • Manager's Attitude: When leaders base professional growth on personal status (marriage) rather than merit, it signals to the workforce that bias is an acceptable tool for talent evaluation.
  • Organisational Culture: Passing over multiple women without internal reviews or consequences creates an informal precedent. This teaches staff that discrimination is standard practice and that challenging it will lead to career retaliation.
  • Client Expectations: Management uses "client preference" as a moral framing shield. By shifting the blame to external clients, the firm avoids taking moral responsibility for its own internal decisions, making discrimination seem like a commercial necessity.
  • Conclusion: Situational pressures work together to turn unethical biases into standard corporate operations, effectively silencing victims through fear of retaliation.

Q3. Employee Rights and Affirmative Action Solutions

  • Rights Violated: Dilnoza's right to equal employment opportunity, right to fair and objective evaluation, and right to protection from gender and status-based discrimination are entirely violated.
  • Affirmative Action Strategy: The firm must implement a formal affirmative action program to rectify its systemic imbalances across four key areas:
    • Fair Job Criteria: Establish objective, documented performance metrics for promotions to remove subjective managerial bias.
    • Promotion Goals: Set explicit targets for female representation in senior leadership positions to break structural barriers.
    • Anti-Bias Training: Mandate diversity and inclusion training for all department heads to challenge underlying stereotypes.
    • Safe Grievance Channels: Set up an independent reporting hotline with strict, legally binding anti-retaliation policies to protect whistleblowers.
  • Conclusion: To bridge the gap, the firm must shift from passive compliance to active affirmative action, ensuring fair career progression for all qualified employees.

Case 7: The Whistleblower's Dilemma

Q1. Step-by-Step Ethical Decision-Making Model

  1. Recognise the Moral Issue: Shaheen identifies that senior management is systematically inflating costs and stealing BDT 1.5 crore through a shell company. This is a clear case of corporate fraud, theft, and a breach of law.
  2. Make a Moral Judgement: He weighs his options. Staying silent protects his family's financial security but makes him complicit in crime. Reporting upholds justice but risks his job. He judges that theft must be exposed.
  3. Establish Moral Intent: Shaheen prioritizes his ethical duties and systemic professional integrity over his immediate fear of job loss or supervisor pushback.
  4. Engage in Moral Behaviour: He executes his choice by reporting the financial fraud directly to an external, independent authority like the Anti-Corruption Commission (ACC), bypasssing the compromised internal channels.
  • Conclusion: Following this model step-by-step guides Shaheen to choose external whistleblowing as the only path that successfully moves from ethical awareness to real moral action.

Q2. Interaction of Individual and Situational Factors

  • Individual Pressures: Shaheen’s personal ethical values push him toward honesty. However, his immediate financial need to sustain his family income and a conventional sense of corporate loyalty pull him toward silence.
  • Situational Roadblocks: Severe power imbalances exist because his direct supervisor is involved in the fraud and controls his career reviews. This, combined with the lack of structural whistleblower protection laws, makes reporting highly dangerous.
  • The Interaction: The high situational risk can force an individual to change how they frame the issue. It pressures them to drop down from high-level moral reasoning to basic survival instincts, making an ethical choice much harder to execute.
  • Conclusion: The combination of financial pressure and fear of career retaliation complicates Shaheen's choice, turning a clear-cut case of fraud into a risky personal dilemma.

Q3. Theoretical Evaluation of Options

Option 1: Report Internally to HR
  • Utilitarian Perspective: Yields low utility. Because the supervisor holds significant organizational power, the report will likely leak, leading to Shaheen being fired while the fraud continues unchecked.
  • Kantian Deontology: Fails to respect Shaheen’s self-worth. Sending a report into a compromised internal trap risks his livelihood without upholding a universal law.
Option 2: Report Externally to the ACC
  • Utilitarian Perspective: Yields high net utility. Although Shaheen faces major personal risks, it protects the public, cleanses market corruption, and saves BDT 1.5 crore in systemic funds.
  • Kantian Deontology: Highly defensible. It treats the rule "do not steal" as a universal law and fulfills his strict duty to report crime, refusing to treat himself or his office as a tool for fraud.
Option 3: Remain Silent
  • Utilitarian Perspective: Protects one family's short-term income but causes massive long-term harm by draining company cash reserves and damaging public market trust.
  • Kantian Deontology: Completely unacceptable. It universalizes a maxim that allows employees to hide grand theft out of personal fear, which destroys the foundation of accounting ethics.
  • Conclusion: Option 2 (External report to the ACC) is the most ethically defensible choice under both theories, even though it carries the high risk of immediate job termination and professional blacklisting.

Case 8: Managing Ethics in a Garment Factory

Q1. Ethics Management Approaches and SafeWear’s State

  • Compliance-Oriented Approach: Focuses purely on preventing, detecting, and punishing violations of law through strict rules. It is driven by a fear of legal penalties.
  • Values-Oriented Approach: Focuses on defining core organizational principles and encouraging a shared employee commitment to high ethical standards.
  • External-Orientation Approach: Focuses heavily on satisfying external stakeholders (such as buyers and auditors). Right actions are defined simply by what is acceptable to outside observers.
  • SafeWear’s Current Approach: SafeWear runs a superficial External-Orientation. They introduced a code of conduct and a hotline solely to appease European buyers after a major fire incident, not to build an ethical culture.
  • Weaknesses: This creates an ethics gap. Because the program lacks internal values, floor supervisors easily override safety protocols and suppress overtime violations to meet shipment deadlines.
  • Conclusion: SafeWear’s purely external focus leaves it vulnerable; its ethics program exists only on paper, creating a high risk of failure during genuine audits.

Q2. The Hotline Silence and Bridging the Culture Gap

  • The Meaning of Silence: The completely silent hotline proves that a massive gap exists between the formal ethics program and the informal factory culture. It indicates zero psychological safety and an active fear of retaliation among workers.
  • Informal Culture Reality: The informal rules tell workers that using the hotline will lead to harassment or termination by floor supervisors, completely overriding the formal policy.
  • Bridging the Gap: Management must take immediate steps to make the system trustworthy:
    • Outsource the grievance hotline to an independent, external Civil Society Organisation (CSO) to ensure anonymous reporting.
    • Introduce severe penalties, including termination, for any supervisor who pressures workers or overrides safety protocols.
  • Conclusion: A hotline is useless without psychological safety; management must actively protect workers to make its formal ethics policies credible.

Q3. Ethical Leadership Actions for the Managing Director (MD)

  • Role of Leadership: Ethical culture cannot be established by rules alone; it requires leaders to model the behavior. The MD must demonstrate that safety and workers' rights take precedence over fast shipment deadlines.
  • Recommended Pre-Audit Actions:
    • Order an immediate, transparent internal audit of all overtime hours and safety systems before the European client arrives.
    • Temporarily slow production lines to safely fix all documented floor hazards.
  • Recommended Post-Audit Actions:
    • Restructure management incentives so that supervisor bonuses are tied directly to zero-safety-violation metrics rather than output speed alone.
    • Hold regular, direct town-hall meetings on the factory floor to guarantee anti-retaliation protection for all workers.
  • Conclusion: The MD must shift from defensive compliance to active ethical leadership, proving that the factory values human safety over short-term production bonuses.

Case 9: The Hostile Takeover of Sunrise Foods

Q1. The Agency Relationship and Board Motivations

  • The Agency Framework: Shareholders are the principals (the owners) who delegate operational authority to the board of directors and managers, who act as their agents. Agents are legally and morally bound to protect the long-term value of the principals' investments.
  • Evaluating Board Intentions:
    • Acting in Good Faith: The board is acting in the shareholders' best interests if its rejection is backed by objective financial valuations showing that MegaCorp’s 40% premium undervalues the company’s future long-term growth.
    • Self-Preservation: The board is suffering from an agency problem if it rejects the bid simply to protect executive jobs, power, and personal perks from being eliminated by the takeover.
  • How to Tell: Look for clear evidence. If the board refuses to negotiate for a higher price or fails to present an independent financial plan for alternative growth, they are likely protecting their own positions.
  • Conclusion: Hostile takeovers highlight the conflict of interest in agency relationships, requiring independent financial audits to determine if management is protecting the owners or themselves.

Q2. Ethical Assessment Across Takeover Dimensions

  • Employee Welfare: Hostile takeovers frequently result in aggressive corporate restructuring, cost-cutting, and mass layoffs. In this case, 3,000 workers face immediate job insecurity and potential factory relocation.
  • Shareholder Rights: Individual shareholders hold a clear property right to sell their stock to maximize financial gains. Blocking them from accepting a 40% market premium restricts their financial autonomy.
  • Community Impact: Sunrise Foods holds deep ties to Rajshahi. Moving the main plant would devastate the local economy, collapse regional employment, and harm local suppliers who rely on the business.
  • Conclusion: Hostile takeovers pit short-term property rights against long-term human welfare, showing that a purely financial decision can cause widespread damage to a community.

Q3. Institutional Shareholder Evaluation Framework

  • Fiduciary Duty vs. Corporate Citizenship: An institutional investor must balance their fiduciary duty (generating strong returns for their pension or fund beneficiaries) against sustainable corporate citizenship (avoiding actions that destroy communities and jobs).
  • Required Information to Make a Decision:
    • An independent, objective long-term asset valuation report of Sunrise Foods to check if the 40% premium is truly a fair price.
    • MegaCorp’s explicit, post-takeover operational blueprint detailing whether they plan to shut down or retain the Rajshahi factory.
    • The current board’s alternative business strategy to see if staying independent will beat the immediate cash offer.
  • Conclusion: Responsible institutional investors must look beyond immediate cash payouts, analyzing long-term operational plans to prevent short-term financial gains from destroying sustainable businesses.

Case 10: The Insider at Dhaka Exchange

Q1. Evaluation via the Four Ethical Dimensions

  • Fairness: Selim acts unfairly. A healthy stock market requires that all investors have equal access to public information. Trading on secret data gives him an unearned advantage, turning the trade into a form of cheating.
  • Misappropriation of Property: The upcoming non-performing loan (NPL) crisis data is confidential corporate property. Selim stole this proprietary information for private financial gain, violating his executive duty of confidentiality.
  • Harm to Investors: Selim’s trade caused direct financial harm. The public investors who bought his shares unknowingly absorbed an immediate 28% financial loss that rightfully belonged to Selim.
  • Undermining Relationships: His conduct destroys the fiduciary relationship of trust between corporate executives, institutional shareholders, and the public market, discouraging widespread investment.
  • Conclusion: Selim’s trade fails every dimension of financial ethics, proving that insider trading is a serious ethical violation rather than a victimless crime.

Q2. Corporate Governance Violations and Executive Duties

  • Breach of Transparency and Integrity: Corporate governance requires executives to provide accurate, timely financial data to the public market. Trading ahead of a crisis completely violates this principle.
  • Violation of the Duty of Loyalty: Executives owe a strict duty of loyalty to put the welfare of the corporation and its shareholders ahead of personal profit. Selim abandoned this duty to save his personal portfolio.
  • Violation of the Duty of Care: This duty requires executives to protect the market reputation and stability of their firm. Selim’s insider trade dragged the bank into a regulatory investigation, destroying public market trust.
  • Conclusion: Insider trading shatters the core pillars of corporate governance, turning an executive from a trusted custodian into an insider threat to the financial system.

Q3. Role of Intent vs. Outcome in Financial Misconduct

  • Deontological Evaluation (Intent-Based): If Selim's trade was truly an automated, unalterable portfolio rebalancing set up months in advance, his personal moral blame is reduced. It shows he did not intentionally set out to cheat the market. However, failing to halt the trade once he gained insider knowledge still shows a failure of professional duty.
  • Utilitarian Evaluation (Outcome-Based): The ultimate outcome remains identical and damaging. The public market was disrupted, innocent investors lost 28% of their capital, and market trust was damaged.
  • Systemic Regulatory Perspective: In financial market ethics and BSEC regulations, executing a trade while holding material, non-public information is treated as a severe violation regardless of intent. The structural damage done to market fairness remains high.
  • Conclusion: While a true pre-planned defense may alter judgments of personal intent, it does not change the negative outcome. Financial misconduct must be restricted based on its damaging systemic effects to keep markets fair and trusted.

Case 11: The Social Media Monitor

Q1. Analysis via the Four Dimensions of Employee Privacy

  • Physical Privacy: While physical surveillance is minimal, TechStart’s policy encroaches on physical privacy by monitoring company-owned devices used outside office hours. This breaches the boundary of the employee’s home space during personal time.
  • Social Privacy: Highly problematic. By tracking personal messaging apps and social media usage, the company monitors who employees talk to and how they maintain relationships outside of their work roles.
  • Informational Privacy: Violated. The company collects, stores, and flags personal browsing histories and private conversations without clear boundaries regarding what constitutes work-related data security versus purely personal data.
  • Psychological Privacy: Severely violated. Rimi experienced deep discomfort after her private thoughts regarding the firm's unpaid overtime culture were flagged. Constant surveillance forces psychological compliance, preventing employees from mentally disconnecting from work.
  • Conclusion: TechStart's monitoring policy is ethically flawed across all four dimensions because it extends corporate control deep into the personal lives, relationships, and private thoughts of its employees.

Q2. Legitimate Monitoring Conditions and Ethical Boundaries

  • Business Necessity: Monitoring is legitimate only when directly tied to protecting trade secrets, ensuring cyber security, or preventing explicit illegal activities on company networks.
  • Time and Device Boundaries: Surveillance must be strictly restricted to core working hours and professional tasks, leaving personal browsing and out-of-office hours completely unmonitored.
  • Proportionality: The least intrusive method must be used. Flagging specific security threats is acceptable; reading private chat histories or personal social media feeds is an overreach.
  • Conclusion: The ethical boundary must be drawn where business risk ends and personal identity begins. Companies have a right to secure their assets, but they have no moral claim over an employee's private expressions or after-hours digital footprint.

Q3. Contract Validity and Informed Consent Principles

  • The Contract Illusion: The mere presence of a clause in a 47-page onboarding document does not make the practice ethically acceptable. Legal signatures do not automatically equal genuine ethical consent.
  • Principles of Informed Consent:
    • Disclosure: The company must clearly disclose the full scope of its electronic tracking. Burying it in a massive text document lacks transparency.
    • Comprehension: The policy was never verbally explained. True consent requires that employees fully understand how their data is flagged and analyzed.
    • Voluntariness: Onboarding sign-offs are often coercive; employees cannot easily negotiate individual terms without risking their job offer.
  • Conclusion: TechStart failed to secure authentic informed consent, using a lengthy contract to hide an invasive surveillance program that exploits employee economic vulnerability.

Case 12: Fair Wages at the Bottom of the Chain

Q1. Legal Minimum Wage vs. Living Wage and Buyer Obligations

  • Legal Minimum Wage: A statutory base rate fixed by regional governments (currently BDT 12,500 per month in this scenario). It represents a political compromise and is frequently insufficient to meet basic costs of living due to inflation.
  • Living Wage: A benchmark calculated to cover essential human needs—including food, housing, healthcare, transportation, and education—enabling a lifecycle of basic dignity (calculated here at BDT 20,000).
  • Multinational Buyer Obligations: Global brands have a strict duty of care across their supply chains. They cannot ethically use public "ethical standards" branding while demanding pricing models that force suppliers to underpay workers and demand unpaid overtime.
  • Conclusion: The legal minimum wage is a regulatory floor, whereas a living wage is an ethical baseline. Multinational buyers are morally obligated to close this BDT 7,500 gap by adjusting their sourcing prices to ensure fair wages.

Q2. Theoretical Evaluation of "Paying the Legal Minimum is Sufficient"

  • Utilitarianism: This claim fails. Keeping wages at BDT 12,500 while requiring unpaid overtime inflicts chronic poverty and physical exhaustion on a large pool of female workers. The marginal profit gains for western buyers and shareholders do not outweigh this widespread human suffering.
  • Kantian Ethics: This claim is completely rejected. Treating vulnerable workers as cheap tools to meet export targets without fair pay uses them purely as a means to an end. Kant demands treating workers as ends in themselves, which requires paying a wage that respects their human worth.
  • Rights-Based Approach: This claim violates core employee rights. Workers have a fundamental right to fair compensation for their labor and a safe work-life balance, making forced, unpaid overtime an explicit rights violation.
  • Conclusion: Relying strictly on inadequate local laws is indefensible under all three moral frameworks; it permits severe economic exploitation for corporate profit.

Q3. Globalization's Race to the Bottom and Counter-Mechanisms

  • The Race to the Bottom: Globalization enables multinational brands to shift production to countries with the lowest wages and weakest labor enforcement. This pressures local suppliers to cut corners on safety and squeeze wages to win production contracts.
  • Corrective Mechanisms:
    • Regulatory: Governments should pass laws linking national minimum wage adjustments directly to independent cost-of-living indexes, while strictly penalizing unpaid overtime.
    • Market-Based: Buyers should adopt certified "Fair Trade" models, where consumers pay a small premium that goes directly toward funding the living wage gap for factory workers.
    • Civil Society: Civil Society Organisations (CSOs) should run global advocacy campaigns to expose corporate hypocrisy, using independent factory floor audits to hold brands accountable to their public statements.
  • Conclusion: Reversing globalization's downward wage spiral requires co-ordinated pressure from independent CSO monitoring, shifting buyer purchasing models from low-cost exploitation to verified fair living wages.

Case 13: Misleading Marketing at NutriPlus

Q1. Consumer Rights and Marketing Ethics Violations

  • Right to Information: NutriPlus directly violates this right by using the misleading tagline "Clinically Proven to Boost Children's Immunity" for a product that is mostly sugar and artificial flavoring. Consumers are denied the objective truth needed to make safe choices.
  • Right to Safety: While sugar is not an immediate poison, misrepresenting a sugary drink as medicinal can lead to nutritional neglect and health risks for children, violating the core duty of consumer safety.
  • Deceptive Advertising: This is an explicit breach of marketing ethics. NutriPlus fabricated chemical and medical value where none exists, using trace amounts of vitamin C to mask an unhealthy product.
  • Conclusion: NutriPlus’s marketing strategy relies on active deception, violating core consumer rights by replacing accurate ingredient transparency with false health claims.

Q2. The Challenge to Consumer Sovereignty

  • The Concept of Consumer Sovereignty: This theory argues that in a free market, consumers act as rational, fully informed economic actors who use their buying power to reward quality products and eliminate deceptive businesses.
  • Why it Fails Here:
    • Information Asymmetry: Lower-middle-income parents lack the laboratory tools needed to test the product's chemical makeup. They must trust the packaging labels.
    • Exploitation of Vulnerability: The "Clinically Proven" framing deliberately targets parents' emotional desire to protect their children's health, bypassing critical evaluation.
    • Budget Displacement: False health claims trick parents into spending scarce food budgets on sugary drinks instead of actual nutrition, turning the free-market choice into an exploitative trap.
  • Conclusion: Consumer sovereignty is an unrealistic assumption when corporations deploy sophisticated, unverified medical claims against vulnerable, non-expert consumer groups.

Q3. Institutional Responsibilities in Mitigating Deceptive Marketing

  • Corporations: Must hold to strict honesty in advertising, ensuring all marketing claims match verified clinical trials before products hit the shelves.
  • Regulators (DGDA): Must proactively police the market. Regulators should impose immediate fines, force deceptive products off the market, and respond quickly to consumer complaints rather than remaining passive.
  • Civil Society Organisations (CSOs): Must continue running independent product testing, launching public awareness campaigns to expose corporate lies, and pursuing legal action to force regulatory enforcement.
  • Conclusion: Protecting vulnerable consumers requires a combination of strong regulatory penalties from the DGDA and independent product testing by vigilant consumer advocacy groups.

Case 14: The Robi–Airtel Merger Revisited

Q1. Ethical Justification of the Merger

  • Utilitarian Justification (The Positive Side): From a consequentialist view, the merger produced significant long-term public benefits: it expanded rural network coverage, lowered mobile data prices, and drove major investments in 4G infrastructure for millions of citizens.
  • The Negative Externalities: The merger also caused immediate harm by eliminating 800 staff positions and cutting supplier contracts, which caused financial distress for small local tech vendors.
  • The Ethical Verdict: The merger can be considered ethically justified under a utilitarian framework because the long-term infrastructure benefits for millions of consumers outweigh the short-term disruptions. However, this justification holds true only if management takes active steps to support the displaced workers and suppliers.
  • Conclusion: The corporate restructuring is ethically permissible due to its broad public utility, but it requires fair transition policies to protect displaced stakeholders from absorbing all the costs.

Q2. Stakeholder Conflicts and Ethical Prioritisation

  • The Conflict Grid:
    • Shareholders demand cost reductions, market consolidation, and higher dividends.
    • Employees and Suppliers require job security, fair transition timelines, and stable contracting prices.
    • Consumers and the Public demand affordable data, wider coverage, and modern digital infrastructure.
  • The Priority Choice: The Consumers and Broader Public should take priority under a utilitarian framework, as telecommunications serve as a baseline utility for modern economic life. However, Kantian deontology notes that the 800 laid-off workers cannot simply be treated as disposable costs to boost shareholder profits.
  • Conclusion: While public infrastructure goals take priority, the company must provide robust severance packages and fair supplier transition periods to respect the rights of its internal stakeholders.

Q3. Recommended Regulatory Safeguards for Mergers

  • Mandatory Redundancy Protections: Regulators should require merging entities to provide extended severance packages, retraining grants, and job placement support for all displaced staff (such as the 800 redundant employees).
  • Supplier Protection Guidelines: Introduce mandatory transition periods for existing vendor contracts to prevent large, merged entities from using their dominant purchasing power to bankrupt small local suppliers.
  • Enforceable Infrastructure Targets: Bind merger approvals to clear, legally binding commitments regarding rural service expansion and price caps to ensure cost savings benefit consumers rather than just corporate profits.
  • Conclusion: Regulators must move beyond basic financial reviews, imposing social and labor safeguards to ensure corporate mergers serve the public good rather than just shareholder interests.

Case 15: The Price-Fixing Cartel

Q1. Cartel Dynamics and the Ethics of Insufficient Competition

  • The Cartel Concept: A cartel is an agreement among independent market competitors to fix prices, restrict supply, or divide territories to maximize corporate profits.
  • Ethical Failure Beyond Illegality: Cartels are unethical because they destroy the free market system. They use economic coercion to eliminate consumer choice, creating artificial scarcity for maximum corporate gain.
  • The Problem of Insufficient Competition: When the "Big Three" distributors remove competition, the market mechanism breaks down. Instead of keeping prices low through open competition, distributors can force a 15–20% price hike on essential medicines, exploiting patients who have no alternative options.
  • Conclusion: Cartels represent a severe market failure; they remove fair competition to extract high profits from vulnerable consumers who depend on essential healthcare products.

Q2. Impact and Rights Violations of Price-Fixing

  • Patients (Consumers): Bear the highest burden, paying an unfair 15–20% premium for essential medications. This violates their right to fair pricing and exploits their medical vulnerabilities.
  • Hospitals: Their right to open, fair commercial negotiation is completely blocked by the distributors' organized boycotts, forcing them into unfair purchasing contracts.
  • Smaller Competitors: Are systematically forced out of the market by the cartel's territory-splitting arrangements, violating their right to fair market entry.
  • Conclusion: The "Big Three" cartel inflicts financial harm across the healthcare network, violating consumer rights and using anti-competitive boycotts to control the market.

Q3. Legitimate Coordination vs. Unethical Collusion

  • Legitimate Business Cooperation: Focuses on improving industry-wide efficiency and safety without altering prices. Examples include setting shared safety standards, running joint logistics networks to remote areas, or co-funding independent scientific research.
  • The Line of Collusion: Cooperation becomes unethical collusion the moment competitors discuss and align on core market variables—such as setting minimum prices, dividing geographic regions, or organizing boycotts against buyers.
  • The Ethical Metric: Legitimate coordination aims to lower production costs and improve value for the public; unethical collusion aims to restrict competition to guarantee high corporate profits.
  • Conclusion: Industry groups must draw a clear line between operational efficiency partnerships and price-fixing arrangements, ensuring market cooperation never compromises fair pricing for consumers.

Case 16: Grameen Bank and the CSO Partnership

Q1. Ethical Issues in Business–CSO Collaboration

  • For the Microfinance Institution (MFI):
    • Mission Drift: The MFI has shifted its core focus from grassroots women's economic empowerment to aggressive corporate portfolio growth targets.
    • Predatory Lending Practices: Loan officers are systemically pressuring vulnerable rural women to take on multiple overlapping debts, intentionally causing community over-indebtedness.
  • For the International NGO:
    • Breach of Fiduciary and Moral Duty: The NGO is prioritizing its organizational survival, public reputation, and access to donor funding over its explicit mission to protect vulnerable women.
    • Complicity through Silence: By intentionally delaying public disclosure of documented exploitation, the NGO is actively shielding the MFI from accountability.
  • Conclusion: This partnership demonstrates an ethical breakdown where both entities sacrifice human empowerment to secure institutional metrics and financing.

Q2. Risks of Co-option and the Challenge of CSO Independence

  • The Risk of Co-option: This scenario demonstrates corporate co-option, where a corporate or financial entity uses partnership resources to neutralize a CSO's critical oversight capacity. The NGO is transformed from an independent watchdog into a marketing and legitimacy shield for the MFI.
  • The Challenge of Independence: When CSOs become structurally dependent on corporate networks for infrastructure access or institutional funding, they develop a corporate mindset. This creates internal conflicts of interest.
  • Accountability Asymmetry: CSOs face an imbalance where their accountability shifts upward toward international financial donors and elite business partners, completely cutting off downward accountability to the beneficiaries they serve.
  • Conclusion: To maintain independence, CSOs must build diversified funding bases and clear exit clauses to avoid becoming defensive marketing extensions of corporations.

Q3. Violations of the International NGO Accountability Charter and Actions

  • Principles Violated:
    • Respect for Human Rights and Empowerment: The NGO fails this principle by ignoring the financial exploitation and mounting debt distress of its target beneficiaries.
    • Transparency and Public Disclosure: The NGO intentionally conceals internal reports to protect its public image, directly violating its commitment to open communication.
    • Responsible Advocacy and Integrity: By remaining silent out of fear of losing funding, the NGO compromises its professional integrity.
  • Recommended Actions:
    • Issue an immediate, transparent public report detailing the community debt levels.
    • Force the MFI to halt all aggressive portfolio targets and establish an independent debt-restructuring fund for the affected women.
    • Formally separate from the MFI if it refuses to adjust its lending metrics, realigning the NGO's funding strategies with its foundational ethics.
  • Conclusion: The NGO must embrace downward accountability, correcting its path by putting the rights of vulnerable communities ahead of its donor funding.

Case 17: The Environmental Activist vs. the Brick Kiln

Q1. Classification and Ethical Evaluation of Civil Society Tactics

  • Indirect Non-Violent Tactics: Submitting formal complaints to the Department of Environment (DoE) and organizing a 5,000-resident petition. These legal approaches use regulatory channels to build democratic pressure.
  • Direct Non-Violent Tactics: Staging peaceful public demonstrations near the community. This method increases visibility and challenges corporate behavior in the public square.
  • Direct Violent / Coercive Tactics: Threatening to block kiln deliveries to disrupt operations. While intended as economic disruption, physical blockades border on aggressive coercion.
  • Ethical Assessment: The indirect and direct non-violent tactics are fully defensible as they respect open democratic engagement. Coercive physical blockades are harder to defend unless all legal and peaceful paths are completely blocked and immediate, irreversible health damage is taking place.
  • Conclusion: The environmental group should rely on its strong public petitions and non-violent protests, as coercive blockades risk losing public support and legal standing.

Q2. Legal Compliance vs. Ethical Responsibility and Duties to CSOs

  • The Inadequacy of Legalism: Operating within outdated 1997 emission limits does not remove the kiln owners' moral obligations. Law is a lagging indicator of societal consensus; a business can remain legally compliant while destroying surrounding agricultural lands and public health.
  • Sustainable Corporate Citizenship: True sustainability requires businesses to manage their negative externalities—such as black smoke and industrial wastewater. Providing 1,200 seasonal jobs and paying taxes does not give an enterprise a license to cause environmental damage.
  • Corporate Duties toward Civil Society: Kiln owners are morally obligated to treat regional community groups as legitimate stakeholders. This requires sharing accurate emission data, participating in community discussions, and actively working to reduce environmental impacts.
  • Conclusion: Simple legal compliance is an insufficient defense when operations cause real environmental damage. Businesses must earn their social license to operate by protecting their local ecosystems.

Q3. Framework for a Sustainable Multi-Stakeholder Resolution

  • Step 1: Environmental Assessment: The local government must step in to mandate an updated Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA), moving past the obsolete 1997 metrics to establish modern air and water quality benchmarks.
  • Step 2: Technological Transition: Kiln owners must commit to a binding schedule to upgrade their facilities to cleaner technology, such as Zig-Zag kilns or Hybrid Hoffman systems, to reduce emissions.
  • Step 3: Financial Collaboration: Local government can provide tax credits or green energy loans to fund these upgrades, protecting the livelihood of the 1,200 seasonal workers.
  • Step 4: CSO Oversight: The environmental group pauses its delivery blockades in exchange for a seat on an independent monitoring committee that verifies emission reductions.
  • Conclusion: This collaborative approach balances environmental health with job preservation, using structural upgrades and community oversight to resolve the conflict.

Case 18: Government Contracts and Political Connections

Q1. Ethical Issues in Business–Government Relations

  • The Nepotism Conflict: BuildRight's success is tied directly to family connections—the chairman being the brother-in-law of a senior planning ministry official—creating a severe conflict of interest.
  • Market Distortion and Unfair Competition: By bypassing competitive bidding where prices are transparently evaluated, the company undermines market fairness and disadvantages superior contractors.
  • Misappropriation of Public Funds: Defending a bid based on "relationships" while delivering a road that falls apart within 18 months represents a direct theft of public tax funds and World Bank capital.
  • Compliance vs. Ethical Behaviour: Ticking boxes on formal procurement documents does not make cronyism acceptable. Legal compliance concerns following the minimum written rule, while ethical behavior requires open competition, fair pricing, and public accountability.
  • Conclusion: BuildRight uses formal compliance as a cover for political favoritism, ignoring its broader duties of public safety and market fairness.

Q2. Analysis of Regulatory Capture

  • The Concept of Regulatory Capture: This occurs when executive agencies or regulatory bodies, which are legally mandated to protect the public interest, end up being systematically directed or influenced by the commercial interests of the industry elite they are supposed to oversee.
  • Evidence of Capture:
    • The planning ministry consistently ignores lower competitive bids to favor a single, politically connected player.
    • The evaluation process remains opaque, shielding connected insiders from public or professional scrutiny.
    • Internal oversight failed to flag the poor construction of a road that failed within 18 months, requiring an outside international body (the World Bank) to step in and initiate a review.
  • Conclusion: The regulatory framework has been captured. The state agency no longer serves as an independent guardian of public infrastructure, operating instead to enrich a politically connected construction firm.

Q3. Reforms in Public Procurement and Corporate Citizenship

  • Mandatory E-GP with Blind Evaluation: Introduce automated electronic government procurement (e-GP) systems where bidder identities are scrubbed during evaluation, forcing selections to be based purely on price and technical merit.
  • Strict Conflict-of-Interest Disclosures: Mandate that all bidding executives disclose any family relationships to ministry officials. Any failure to disclose or presence of a direct connection must trigger automatic disqualification.
  • Performance-Linked Blacklisting: Implement a corporate accountability standard where any contractor whose infrastructure fails standard durability reviews within a set period is automatically blacklisted from future public bidding.
  • Independent Auditing: Require all high-value public projects to undergo independent quality testing by external engineering firms before funds are disbursed.
  • Conclusion: Eliminating cronyism requires shifting from opaque, relationship-based contracting to automated, transparent procurement systems with enforceable performance penalties.

Case 19: Tax Ethics and the Phantom Profit

Q1. Letter of the Law vs. Spirit of the Law in Tax Strategy

  • The Letter of the Law Approach: DigiGlobe is technically compliant with the literal text of local laws. Because the National Board of Revenue (NBR) lacks specific transfer-pricing rules for the digital economy, shifting BDT 800 crore out via royalty payments is not legally classified as evasion.
  • The Spirit of the Law Violation: The clear intent of tax legislation is that corporations should pay a fair share of taxes on profits generated within the community that provides their consumers and infrastructure. Manufacturing artificial royalty loops to report a tiny local profit (paying less than 0.5% effectively) bypasses this civic duty.
  • Conclusion: DigiGlobe’s tax strategy is unethical; it exploits a temporary regulatory gap to strip revenue from the developing economy where its wealth is actually created.

Q2. Corporate Citizenship Obligations Beyond Compliance

  • Extended Corporate Citizenship: Under this framework, a corporation is a citizen within society and owes duties to help maintain social infrastructure. DigiGlobe uses Bangladesh's public roads, telecom systems, and legal frameworks to generate BDT 800 crore in revenue.
  • The Social Contract: While employing 2,000 local people is positive, it does not replace the company's duty to pay taxes. Tax revenues fund the schools, hospitals, and public services that sustain their own workforce and consumer base.
  • Conclusion: Corporate citizenship requires companies to contribute fairly to the public treasury, rather than treating local job creation as an excuse to avoid funding national development.

Q3. Regulatory Mechanisms and Corporate Ethical Arguments

  • Recommended Regulatory Mechanisms:
    • Arm's Length Principle: The NBR must introduce transfer pricing regulations requiring all intra-company transactions and royalties to match open-market prices.
    • Digital Services Tax (DST): Implement a flat tax on gross local revenues for multinational digital platforms, ensuring tax collection bypasses complex accounting loops.
  • Arguments for Corporate Resistance: DigiGlobe might claim that new taxes risk double taxation, reduce foreign direct investment (FDI), or limit funds needed to invest back into the country's digital economy.
  • Arguments for Corporate Acceptance: Embracing fair taxation builds long-term brand equity, ensures regulatory stability, and strengthens the public infrastructure required to scale their business sustainably.
  • Conclusion: The government must introduce clear transfer pricing protections, forcing multinational tech platforms to transition from exploitative tax planning to verifiable corporate citizenship.

Case 20: The Ethical Supply Chain: Integrative Case

Q1. Five Distinct Ethical Failures and Syllabus Links

  1. Violation of Employee Rights (Chapter 6): Main suppliers are paying workers BDT 11,500 per month, which falls well below a baseline living wage.
  2. Abuse of Human Rights / Supply Chain Exploitation (Chapter 6/8): A subsidiary supplier uses child labor for embroidery sub-contracting, violating core international human rights standards.
  3. Environmental Degradation / Failure of Sustainability (Chapter 1): The packaging vendor dumps toxic chemical dye waste directly into local canals, damaging the regional ecosystem.
  4. Internal Governance Failure and Corruption (Chapter 4/5): The Bangladesh Country Manager accepted bribes and gifts from suppliers for preferred status, creating conflicts of interest and undermining corporate governance.
  5. Marketing Deception and Greenwashing (Chapter 7): The company’s public Sustainability Report claims a "zero tolerance" policy, creating a direct conflict with their actual supply chain operations.
  • Conclusion: ThreadRight suffers from a systemic ethical failure across its entire operation, where formal ethics reporting is disconnected from actual supply chain practices.

Q2. Prioritised Action Plan for the Head of Ethics and Compliance

  • Priority 1: Halt Child Labor and Support Victims (Immediate)
    • Action: Terminate the relationship with the sub-contractor using child labor. Absorb the children into a fund that pays for their formal education while matching their lost family income.
    • Justification: Kantian Deontology. Using children as low-cost tools to meet production targets treats human beings purely as a means to an end, violating a fundamental moral duty.
  • Priority 2: Stop Environmental Pollution (Immediate)
    • Action: Suspend orders from the packaging supplier until they install functional effluent treatment plants (ETPs) to stop dumping chemical waste.
    • Justification: Utilitarianism. Dumping chemical dye into canals causes widespread health issues for local communities, creating a massive negative outcome that far outweighs short-term production savings.
  • Priority 3: Terminate Corrupt Executives and Clean Governance (Short-Term)
    • Action: Fire the Bangladesh Country Manager for accepting bribes and overhaul internal procurement tracking.
    • Justification: Virtue Ethics. Corporate integrity requires leaders to display honesty and professional care, making bribery a fireable offense.
  • Priority 4: Implement Living Wages Across Suppliers (Medium-Term)
    • Action: Adjust ThreadRight's purchasing contracts upward, guaranteeing the main supplier can transition workers from BDT 11,500 to a verified regional living wage.
    • Justification: Rights-Based Ethics. Workers have a fundamental right to fair pay that supports basic human dignity.
  • Priority 5: Reform Sustainability Reporting (Medium-Term)
    • Action: Retract the greenwashed marketing reports and replace them with verified social and environmental accounting metrics.
  • Conclusion: The compliance strategy must focus on immediate human rights protection before addressing structural wage gaps and long-term reporting reform.

Q3. Stakeholder Prioritisation and Restoring Trust

  • The Stakeholder Hierarchy: To survive this crisis, ThreadRight must reject the narrow idea of shareholder primacy. Vulnerable Stakeholders (the factory workers, exploited children, and polluted local communities) must take priority over immediate shareholder demands.
  • Why the Bottom of the Chain Comes First: Without factory employees and local communities, the company has no physical product or operational base. Protecting shareholders at the expense of human safety destroys the company's social license to operate, leading to government penalties and dropping stock values.

  • Balancing Competing Claims to Restore Trust:
    • Independent Social Accounting: Bring in trusted civil society organizations (CSOs) to run transparent, unannounced factory audits, publishing the raw findings to rebuild public credibility.
    • Restructure Buying Contracts: Shift pricing models from low-cost sourcing to guaranteed living wages, proving to consumers that the brand values basic human rights.
    • Government Partnerships: Collaborate openly with the Department of Environment to co-fund community water cleanup initiatives, protecting the country's preferential trade status.
  • Conclusion: Restoring trust requires ThreadRight to transition from superficial greenwashing to a verifiable stakeholder model that treats human safety and environmental health as core business requirements.
