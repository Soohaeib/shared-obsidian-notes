IRM 2020 Exam Q7

(a)

Subject Matters of Marine Insurance

Marine insurance provides financial protection against losses involving ships, cargo, and the environments through which property is transferred between points of origin and destination. The four primary subject matters of marine insurance are:

  • Hull Insurance: This covers the physical vessel itself, including its machinery, furniture, tackle, and equipment. It protects the shipowner against losses resulting from damage to the ship caused by marine perils (such as stranding, sinking, fire, or collision).
  • Cargo Insurance: This protects the owners of the goods or merchandise being transported by sea against loss or damage during transit. Coverage usually extends from the warehouse of origin to the final destination warehouse (warehouse-to-warehouse coverage).
  • Freight Insurance: Freight is the reward or payment received by the shipowner for safely carrying cargo to its destination. If the cargo is lost or damaged and cannot be delivered, the shipowner loses the freight earnings. Freight insurance indemnifies the shipowner against this loss of revenue.
  • Liability Insurance (Protection and Indemnity - P&I): This covers third-party liabilities incurred by the shipowner. It includes legal liabilities for injury or death of crew members or passengers, damage to other ships during collisions, damage to docks/harbors, and liabilities arising from environmental pollution (such as oil spills).

(b)

Application of Core Principles to Marine Insurance

The fundamental principles of insurance are uniquely applied within the context of marine insurance contracts due to the international and dynamic nature of maritime trade:

Insurable Interest

In standard insurance contracts, an insurable interest must exist at the inception of the policy. However, in marine insurance, an insurable interest must exist at the time of the loss, even if it did not exist when the insurance was originally effected. This exception is vital because ownership of goods frequently changes hands while cargo is in transit through the endorsement of bills of lading. The assured must stand in a legal or equitable relationship to the marine adventure, where they benefit from the safe arrival of the property or suffer financial prejudice from its loss or damage.

Utmost Good Faith (Uberrimae Fidei)

A contract of marine insurance is based on mutual trust. The shipowner and cargo owner generally possess absolute knowledge of the risks, while the underwriter relies entirely on the information provided. Therefore, the insured is legally obligated to disclose every material fact (e.g., the exact nature of the cargo, the age and condition of the vessel, the specific route) before the contract is concluded. Any misrepresentation or non-disclosure of a material fact makes the contract voidable at the option of the insurer.

Indemnity

Marine insurance is a contract of indemnity, meaning the insured should be restored to the financial position they occupied prior to the loss, without making a profit. However, marine policies are almost always valued policies. At the time the policy is issued, the insurer and insured agree on a fixed valuation for the subject matter (e.g., cargo value plus shipping costs plus an agreed percentage for estimated profit). In the event of a total loss, the agreed value is paid out regardless of fluctuations in market values at the time of the loss. This modified application simplifies international claim settlements.

(c)

Various Clauses of Marine Insurance Policies

Marine insurance policies utilize standard, internationally recognized clauses designed by the Institute of London Underwriters (ILU) to precisely define the scope of coverage and exclusions:

Institute Cargo Clauses (ICC)

These are standard clauses that determine the level of peril coverage for cargo transport:

  • Institute Cargo Clauses (A): This is the widest form of coverage, written on an "All Risks" basis. It covers all physical losses or damages except for standard exclusions such as inherent vice, wear and tear, or improper packaging.
  • Institute Cargo Clauses (B): This is a restrictive, named-perils clause. It covers losses arising from specified events like fire, explosion, capsizing, grounding, collision, earthquake, volcanic eruption, and washing overboard.
  • Institute Cargo Clauses (C): This offers the most limited named-perils coverage. It excludes risks like washing overboard or damage by water entry, covering only catastrophic events such as fire, explosion, grounding, stranding, or sinking.

Sue and Labour Clause

This clause requires the insured to take all reasonable, prudent steps to prevent or minimize a loss to the insured property when a peril strikes. The insurer, in turn, promises to reimburse the insured for any expenses reasonably incurred while attempting to save or protect the property, even if those attempts are ultimately unsuccessful.

Running Down Clause (Collision Liability Clause)

Found in hull insurance policies, this clause extends coverage to protect the shipowner against legal liabilities if the insured vessel collides with another ship and causes damage to that ship or its cargo.

Inchmaree Clause (Negligence Clause)

This clause covers losses caused by the bursting of boilers, breakage of shafts, or latent defects in the machinery or hull of the ship, as well as losses resulting from the negligence of the master, officers, crew, or pilots, provided there is no want of due diligence by the shipowners.

Free of Capture and Seizure (FC&S) Clause

This clause explicitly excludes all losses, damages, or expenses caused by war, capture, seizure, arrests, restraints, detonations of mines/torpedoes, or other hostile political actions. To secure coverage against these risks, a separate War Risk policy or endorsement must be purchased.

Strikes, Riots, and Civil Commotions (SR&CC) Clause

This clause excludes any losses or damage caused by the violent acts of strikers, locked-out workmen, or persons taking part in labor disturbances, riots, or civil commotions. Similar to war risks, protection against these perils must be specifically reinstated by paying an additional premium.
