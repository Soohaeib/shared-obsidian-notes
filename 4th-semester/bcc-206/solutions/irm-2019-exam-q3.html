(a)

Benefits of Insurance to Society

(a_1)

Indemnification for Loss

Indemnification permits individuals, families, and organizations to be restored to their original financial positions after a covered loss occurs. When a family breadwinner is injured, a home is destroyed, or a business facility is damaged, insurance benefits provide the necessary funds to cover medical bills, rebuild structures, or replace lost inventory. This leads to several key societal outcomes:

  • Maintenance of Economic Stability: Insured individuals and families can maintain their pre-loss standard of living instead of falling into poverty or becoming dependent on public welfare or charity systems.
  • Business Continuity: For enterprises, prompt indemnification ensures that operations can resume quickly following disasters. This minimizes business interruptions, helps companies avoid insolvency, preserves local employment opportunities, and maintains stable market supply chains.

(a_2)

Enhancement of Credit

Insurance drastically enhances a borrower’s creditworthiness, making them a significantly more attractive and lower-risk prospect for financial lenders. Lenders are highly hesitant to advance substantial sums of money without concrete guarantees that their financial interest is protected against unforeseen hazards.

  • Collateral Protection: When a house is purchased via a bank mortgage, or commercial real estate is funded through commercial loans, lenders almost universally require property insurance. This guarantees that if the underlying collateral is destroyed by fire or natural disaster, the loan will still be repaid through insurance proceeds.
  • Operational and Personal Guarantees: Similarly, businesses seeking lines of credit must often maintain comprehensive general liability and commercial property policies to prove operational resilience. In personal and partner-led businesses, life insurance policies can be utilized to guarantee outstanding debts, ensuring that a borrower’s sudden death does not lead to a default on the credit extended.

(a_3)

Source of Funds for Capital Investment and Accumulation

Because insurance premiums are collected in advance of actual losses, insurance companies accumulate vast sums of premium capital that must be held in reserve to pay for future claims. Rather than leaving these massive cash reserves idle, insurers actively invest them back into the financial markets.

  • Long-Term Capital Accumulation: Life insurance companies and property-casualty insurers are among the largest institutional investors in modern global and domestic financial systems. They channel billions into long-term financial instruments such as corporate bonds, government securities, public infrastructure bonds, commercial mortgages, and equity shares.
  • Economic Development Support: This continuous inflow of capital supports large-scale economic development. It lowers the overall cost of borrowing for both corporations and municipal governments, funds vital public works projects (such as highways, power plants, and hospitals), finances corporate expansions, and drives technological innovations that ultimately increase national productivity and full employment.

(b)

Major Differences Between Insurance and Gambling

Feature Insurance Gambling
Nature of Risk Handled Deals entirely with an existing pure risk (situations where there is only a possibility of loss or no loss, such as a fire, accident, or premature death). Creates a completely new speculative risk where none existed before (situations where there is a deliberate possibility of either a financial gain or a financial loss, such as a wager on a game).
Risk Creation No new risk is created by the transaction; the contract simply transfers and pools a risk that already threatens the insured. A brand-new risk is intentionally generated by the transaction solely for entertainment, excitement, or personal financial gain.
Social and Economic Outcome A socially productive mechanism that reduces societal risk, provides financial predictability, mitigates anxiety, and restores economic units to their pre-loss state. A socially non-productive transaction that merely shifts wealth in a zero-sum fashion; it increases net societal risk and can cause severe negative externalities like financial ruin or addiction.
Application of Indemnity Operates strictly under the principle of indemnity; the insured is legally prohibited from profiting from a loss or recovering more than their actual financial deficit. Does not observe the principle of indemnity; the winning party is fully permitted to make a substantial profit directly at the expense of the losing party.
Requirement of Insurable Interest Requires a valid, legally recognized insurable interest to exist in the subject matter for the policy to be enforceable. No insurable interest is present or required; participants hold no prior financial relationship with the outcome outside of the wager itself.

(c)

Meaning and Requirement of Insurable Interest

Meaning of Insurable Interest

An insurable interest means that the insured or applicant must stand in a legally acknowledged financial or emotional relationship to the subject matter of the insurance contract. This relationship implies that the insured will suffer a direct financial loss, legal liability, or recognizable harm if the insured property or life is damaged, destroyed, or lost, and conversely, will derive a tangible benefit or utility from its safe and continued existence.

Reasons Why Insurable Interest is Required

The doctrine of insurable interest is a foundational requirement in every insurance contract due to three crucial legal and operational reasons:

To Prevent Gambling and Wager Contracts

If an insurable interest were not legally required, individuals would be free to purchase insurance policies on arbitrary property or lives completely unrelated to them—such as insuring a random stranger’s house against fire or a stranger’s life against death. The transaction would degenerate into a simple gambling contract where the buyer is merely wagering that an unfortunate event will occur to collect a windfall payout. Requiring an insurable interest ensures that insurance remains a legitimate risk-transfer mechanism rather than an unauthorized vehicle for speculative betting.

To Reduce Moral Hazard

Moral hazard refers to the incentive or inclination of an individual to intentionally cause, fabricate, or exaggerate a loss in order to collect insurance cash benefits. If people could legally insure property or lives in which they possess no financial stake, a massive incentive would emerge to deliberately cause destruction (e.g., committing arson on someone else's building or orchestrating the demise of an individual) to receive the policy proceeds. Requiring an insurable interest ensures that the policyholder stands to lose financially from a disaster, aligning their incentives with loss prevention and preservation.

To Measure and Limit the True Financial Loss

In indemnity insurance contracts (such as property and liability policies), the extent of the insured’s insurable interest serves as the absolute maximum financial ceiling for their recovery. An insured cannot recover an amount greater than their actual ownership stake or financial interest at the time of the loss, regardless of the policy's face value. This precisely quantifies the maximum payout, reinforces the principal rules of indemnity, and prevents the policyholder from securing an unfair financial profit from a disaster.
