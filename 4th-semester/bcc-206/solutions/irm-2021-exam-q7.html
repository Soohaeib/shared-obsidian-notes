(a)

Definition and Uses of Fire Insurance

Definition of Fire Insurance

Fire insurance is a specific contract of indemnity where the insurer, in consideration of a premium paid by the insured, undertakes to compensate the insured for any financial loss or damage caused by fire or related perils to the specified property (such as buildings, machinery, or stock) during a designated period, up to an agreed maximum sum insured. For a loss to be claimable under fire insurance, the fire must be accidental, unintended by the insured, and must involve actual ignition or burning where it shouldn't be.

Uses of Fire Insurance

  • Asset Protection and Indemnity: It safeguards individuals and businesses from devastating financial losses by compensating them for the actual value of property destroyed or damaged by fire.
  • Business Continuity: It provides the necessary capital to repair, rebuild, or replace damaged infrastructure, ensuring that business operations can resume quickly after a catastrophic event.
  • Credit Enhancement: Financial institutions and commercial banks typically require fire insurance policies on properties or inventory used as collateral before approving mortgages or business loans.
  • Risk Transfer and Peace of Mind: It allows property owners to transfer the highly volatile risk of fire to a professional risk bearer, giving business owners and individuals peace of mind to focus on growth.
  • Coverage for Allied Perils: Modern fire policies can be extended via endorsements to cover additional allied perils like lightning, explosions, storms, floods, riots, and strikes, offering holistic asset protection.

(b)

Procedure of Settlement of Fire Insurance Claims

The standard procedure for settling a fire insurance claim generally involves the following sequential steps:

  1. Notice of Loss: Immediately upon the occurrence of a fire, the insured must give written notice to the insurance company. This notification must include basic information such as the policy number, date, time, location, and an initial assessment of the cause of the fire.
  2. Mitigation of Loss: The insured is legally obligated to take all reasonable steps to minimize the spread of the fire and protect the remaining property from further damage (e.g., calling the fire service, safely removing unaffected stock).
  3. Filing the Formal Claim: The insured must submit a formal, detailed claim form within the stipulated timeframe (usually 15 to 30 days from the date of loss). This form specifies itemized details of the damaged property, their values at the time of the fire, and the total amount claimed.
  4. Appointment of a Surveyor/Loss Assessor: Upon receiving the claim, the insurer appoints an independent licensed surveyor or loss assessor to inspect the site. The surveyor investigates the actual cause of the fire, determines whether it falls under covered or excluded perils, and evaluates the physical extent of the loss.
  5. Submission of Evidence and Documentation: The insured must assist the surveyor by providing documentary proof of ownership and valuation, such as:
    • Fire Brigade Report
    • Police First Information Report (FIR)
    • Audited books of accounts, stock registers, and purchase invoices
    • Repair estimates or replacement quotes
  6. Surveyor's Report: The surveyor compiles their findings into a final survey report, detailing the cause of the fire, the verified valuation of the property, the salvage value, and a recommendation for the final payout based on policy terms (applying clauses like depreciation or the average clause if underinsured).
  7. Final Adjustment and Settlement: The insurance company evaluates the surveyor's report against the policy terms and conditions. If satisfied, they issue a discharge voucher to the insured. Once signed by the insured, the insurer releases the settlement check. If the loss was caused by an excluded peril or fraud, the claim is formally repudiated.

(c)

Problems and Prospects of Insurance Business in Bangladesh

The insurance sector in Bangladesh holds significant economic potential but faces deep-rooted systemic issues.

Problems Facing the Insurance Sector

  • Low Penetration and Lack of Trust: Bangladesh has one of the lowest insurance penetration rates in the world. A pervasive lack of public awareness, combined with a historical image crisis due to delayed or unpaid claims, breeds distrust among the population.
  • Skill Deficiencies and Shortage of Actuaries: The industry suffers from a severe deficit of technically skilled human resources, professional underwriters, and qualified actuaries, which hinders scientific pricing and robust risk management.
  • Fierce and Unhealthy Competition: The market is over-saturated with a disproportionately high number of non-life insurance companies relative to the market size, leading to unethical practices such as undercutting premiums and paying illegal commissions.
  • Product Homogeneity: Insurance products remain highly traditional, focusing almost exclusively on standard fire, marine, and motor policies, with minimal innovation in emerging areas like health, crop, cyber, or liability insurance.
  • Cultural and Religious Misconceptions: A large segment of the population holds fatalistic beliefs or views conventional insurance as contradictory to religious principles, although the growth of Islamic insurance (Takaful) has partially addressed this.
  • Weak Regulatory Enforcement: While the Insurance Development and Regulatory Authority (IDRA) has introduced key initiatives, full operationalization and compliance enforcement across all companies remain challenging.

Prospects of the Insurance Sector

  • Sustained Economic Growth: As Bangladesh experiences expanding industrialization, rising per capita income, and a growing middle class, the demand for personal lines of insurance (life, health, property) is structurally poised to expand.
  • Unrivaled Infrastructure and Mega-Projects: The massive development of national infrastructure projects (special economic zones, deep-sea ports, power plants) requires heavy insurance and reinsurance protection, opening up large-scale commercial pipelines.
  • Digital Transformation and Insurtech: The rapid adoption of Mobile Financial Services (MFS) like bKash and Nagad provides a perfect delivery architecture for microinsurance, digital policy purchases, and instant automated claim settlements for rural and low-income populations.
  • Niche Product Diversification: There is a vast unserved market for specialized insurance designs, including agricultural/crop insurance to guard against climate vulnerability, expatriate worker life policies, and corporate group health programs.
  • Regulatory Reforms and Transparency: Strengthening IDRA's regulatory oversight—including mandatory automation, capital adequacy updates, and stringent compliance frameworks—will gradually eliminate weak market actors, restore consumer trust, and foster a stable, competitive environment.
