4th Semester Final 2025

Insurance and Risk Management (BCC-206)

Question 1

  • (a) Construct your own definition of 'risk' and justify the conceptual boundary between risk and uncertainty.

  • (b) Justify the necessity of risk management in contemporary business operations. Identify at least four common organisational risks and outline the sequential stages of an effective risk management framework.

  • (c) Contrast pure risk with speculative risk using real-life scenarios. Show how the element of loss operates differently in each category.

Question 2

For each of the following risk situations, design an appropriate risk management strategy (single or combination) and defend your choice:

  • (a) Rahim, a 45-year-old bank manager, has a sedentary lifestyle and his father died of a heart attack at age 55.

  • (b) A private investment fund notices unusual volatility in the stock market that threatens its portfolio value.

  • (c) A software development firm discovers critical security loopholes in its client database system.

  • (d) A small business owner fears bankruptcy because of mounting personal loan obligations.

  • (e) A garment export company has three factories located in coastal areas frequently hit by cyclones and tidal surges.

Question 3

  • (a) Explain the interrelationship between loss frequency and loss severity in risk assessment. Use examples to show how a change in one can impact the other.

  • (b) How can quantitative methods like probability distributions and statistical analysis be used to assess loss frequency and severity? Illustrate with an example.

  • (c) Discuss how changes in economic conditions such as inflation and interest rates affect the demand for insurance among individuals and organisations.

Question 4

  • (a) Distinguish between insurance and gambling. Illustrate your answer with suitable examples, and explain why insurance is generally regarded as a socially beneficial practice, whereas gambling is not.

  • (b) Explain the principle of utmost good faith in insurance contracts. Why is this principle essential for maintaining trust and fairness between the insurer and the insured?

  • (c) Discuss the significance of the principle of "insurable interest" in insurance contracts. Illustrate your answer with suitable examples.

  • (d) Discuss the purpose and importance of reinsurance in the insurance industry. Explain how reinsurance enables insurance companies to manage their risk exposure effectively.

Question 5

  • (a) Craft a precise definition of life insurance and highlight its primary mission. Show how life insurance serves as a tool for intergenerational financial security.

  • (b) Contrast term life, whole life, and universal life insurance on parameters such as premium structure, cash value, and suitability for different life stages.

  • (c) Identify the pressing challenges confronting the life insurance industry today and examine recent trends including insurtech innovations and evolving consumer expectations.

Question 6

  • (a) Explain the concept of marine insurance and discuss its significance in international trade. Illustrate your answer with suitable examples.

  • (b) Discuss the key elements of a marine insurance policy. Define the following types of marine insurance policies:

    • i. Time Policy
    • ii. Unvalued Policy
    • iii. Floating Policy
    • iv. Blanket Policy
    • v. Block Policy

Question 7

  • (a) Enumerate and briefly characterise the diverse life insurance product categories currently operated and administered by Jiban Bima Corporation.

  • (b) Compose a concise analytical note on the establishment, mandate, and key functions of the Insurance Development and Regulatory Authority (IDRA) of Bangladesh.

  • (c) Propose a set of actionable policy measures and strategic initiatives that the Government of Bangladesh ought to adopt in order to enhance the public appeal and penetration of the insurance business.

Business Ethics (BCC-207)

Question 1

  • (a) Define ethics. Identify the relationship between ethics and law.

  • (b) Briefly explain the importance of business ethics.

  • (c) Point out the relevance of globalisation for business ethics.

Question 2

  • (a) Explain the key components of an ethical decision in business. How do individual factors, such as moral development, personal values, and cognitive biases, influence ethical decision-making?

  • (b) Define Sustainability and explain how the Triple Bottom Line (TBL) framework shifts the goal of a corporation from purely economic value to an extended set of social and environmental goals.

  • (c) A Bangladeshi tea estate owner finds that using a specific pesticide increases yield by 30% but is contaminating into local water sources used by villagers. Using the TBL framework, argue why the owner should or should not continue using the pesticide. Consider the economic benefits, social impacts, and environmental consequences in your answer.

Question 3

  • (a) Discuss the ethical issues that arise in the firm-employee relationship. How does globalisation create additional ethical challenges for companies employing people in developing countries?

  • (b) Outline the core rights and duties of employees in an organisation. How does workplace discrimination violate these rights?

  • (c) FoodGiant is a large food and beverage company. In developing countries, it advertises sugary foods and drinks to children using cartoon characters, free toys, and school programmes. However, in developed countries, the company promotes its own products as healthy choices. At the same time, workers in FoodGiant's factories work 12 hours a day without extra pay for overtime. Employees who try to form a labour union often lose their jobs.

    Required:

    • i. Identify the ethical issues related to FoodGiant's treatment of consumers and employees.
    • ii. Explain why the company's marketing practices in developing countries can be considered a double standard.
    • iii. Discuss whether FoodGiant's actions are socially responsible. Support your answer with reasons.

Question 4

  • (a) Define business ethics management. Explain its components.

  • (b) Briefly explain the different types of ethical codes.

  • (c) Identify the reasons for social accounting by the organisations.

Question 5

  • (a) How does the government function as a stakeholder in business ethics? What ethical issues typically arise in the relationship between business and government, particularly in developing economies?

  • (b) What is meant by 'corporate citizenship'? Discuss the relationship between viewing a business as a "key player in the regulatory game" versus a "passive rule-follower". How should corporations engage with government in an ethical manner?

  • (c) MegaPort Ltd. is a large port infrastructure company seeking a major government contract in a South Asian country. Company representatives paid government officials a fee to accelerate the approval process, although illegal under international anti-bribery laws. This is a common local practice. The company argues that without such payments, it cannot compete with the local firms who routinely engage in such practices. Also, it claims that the project will bring thousands of jobs and local people.

    Required: Analyze the ethical dimensions of MegaPort Ltd.'s conduct using the concepts of government as stakeholder, corporate citizenship, and globalisation of business-government relations. Is the 'common local practice' argument ethically valid? What systemic changes in both corporate governance and regulatory frameworks would help prevent such ethical violations?

Question 6

  • (a) Explain the Agency Relationship between shareholders (principals) and managers (agents), identifying the roles of conflict of interest and informational asymmetry.

  • (b) A large multinational company seeks a hostile takeover of a traditional Bangladeshi food brand without the board's approval. Evaluate the potential impact of this takeover on employee welfare and local community identity.

  • (c) In a major Bangladeshi pharmaceutical firm, the CEO sold a significant volume of personal shares in the company one week before the public announcement of a failed drug trial that caused the stock price to fall by 35%. The information about the drug trial failure was known internally months before the announcement. Minority shareholders suffered major losses. Regulators launched an investigation, but the CEO argued the sale was 'planned in advance' for personal financial reasons.

    Required: Analyze the above case from the perspectives of (i) ethical issues in corporate governance, (ii) shareholder rights. What governance reforms should the company implement to prevent such a situation?

Question 7

  • (a) Distinguish between Consequentialist and Non-consequentialist ethical theories. Provide one business example for each to illustrate their application.

  • (b) Compare and contrast egoism, Utilitarianism, and ethics of duties. Which theory do you consider most applicable to modern business decision-making and why?

  • (c) Nadia is the operations director of a garment factory in Chittagong. Year-end profits are strong. She faces a choice: (1) distribute a substantial bonus to all 500 workers, improving their living standards but reducing investor dividends; or (2) pay higher dividends to shareholders to retain investment for planned expansion, which may create 200 new jobs next year but provides nothing to current workers now. Some senior managers suggest a third path: allocate funds to improve safety equipment, which satisfies no one immediately but reduces accident risk in future periods.

    Required: Apply at least two ethical theories to evaluate each of Nadia's three options. Which course of action is most ethically defensible? Justify your reasoning.

Entrepreneurship Development (BCC-208)

Question 1

  • (a) Passion works as a driving force for a successful entrepreneur. Elucidate the statement by defining Entrepreneur and Entrepreneurship.

  • (b) Define execution intelligence. How would you relate it with an entrepreneur?

  • (c) Mention some attributes of firms that pursue high levels of corporate entrepreneurship.

  • (d) In 1978, Ayesha Abed helped establish Aarong in Bangladesh to create sustainable income opportunities for rural artisans, especially women. At that time, many artisans struggled to find reliable markets for their handcrafted products and often faced delayed payments. Ayesha Abed recognized this problem and transformed it into an entrepreneurial opportunity by connecting rural producers with urban consumers through Aarong. Her success demonstrates several key entrepreneurial qualities. First, she showed vision by identifying an unmet market need. Second, she displayed leadership by organising thousands of artisans into a productive network. Third, she exhibited social responsibility, ensuring fair prices and employment opportunities for disadvantaged women. Finally, her perseverance and innovation helped Aarong grow from a small outlet into one of Bangladesh's leading lifestyle retail brands. Today, Aarong supports tens of thousands of artisans and serves as a successful example of how entrepreneurial qualities can create both business success and positive social impact in Bangladesh.

    Required:

    • i) Which entrepreneurial quality was most important in establishing Aarong? Why?
    • ii) How did leadership contribute to Aarong's success?
    • iii) In what way did Aarong create social value in Bangladesh?

Question 2

  • (a) How can finding a gap in the marketplace create a business opportunity for an entrepreneur to pursue?

  • (b) How does creativity contribute to the opportunity recognition process?

  • (c) Point out some traits that help an entrepreneur in successful opportunity recognition.

  • (d) In many areas of coastal Bangladesh, especially in districts such as Satkhira and Khulna, people face severe shortages of safe drinking water due to salinity in groundwater. During the dry season, families often travel long distances to collect fresh water, which consumes time and affects health and productivity. Recognising this problem, Drinkwell introduced community-based water purification systems that convert saline and contaminated water into safe drinking water. The company installs purification plants in villages and operates them through local entrepreneurs. Residents can purchase clean water at an affordable price while local operators earn income from managing the facilities. This solution addressed a significant social problem while creating a sustainable business opportunity. As a result, thousands of people gained access to safe drinking water, waterborne diseases were reduced, and local employment opportunities were created. The case demonstrates how identifying a community problem can lead to innovative entrepreneurial ventures that generate both social and economic value.

    Required:

    • i) What problem did Drinkwell identify in coastal Bangladesh?
    • ii) Why can this case be considered an entrepreneurial opportunity?
    • iii) What innovation did the company introduce?

Question 3

  • (a) What is the best time for a firm to develop its business model?

  • (b) Pathao, one of Bangladesh's leading digital platforms, emerged in 2015 as a motorcycle-based ride-sharing service at a time when Dhaka's traffic congestion was among the worst in the world. Traditional transportation—taxis, rickshaws, and buses—struggled to offer reliability or speed. Pathao introduced a disruptive business model built on smartphone connectivity, real-time matching, and a gig-based rider network. Instead of owning vehicles, Pathao empowered thousands of freelance bikers to join the platform, drastically lowering operational costs and enabling rapid scale-up. Pathao further expanded into food delivery, courier services, and digital payments through Pathao Pay, creating a multi-sided digital ecosystem. Each service disrupted a separate traditional sector: restaurant deliveries, parcel logistics, and even cash-based transactions. Its pricing transparency, quick service, and user-friendly app attracted a large urban young population, while conventional transport operators and courier companies faced declining demand and competitive pressure. By leveraging local market insights—such as acceptance of mobile payments and the widespread use of affordable smartphones—Pathao adapted global ride-sharing concepts to Bangladeshi realities. The company's disruptive approach not only redefined urban mobility but also generated income opportunities for tens of thousands of riders and delivery agents, reshaping Bangladesh's digital economy.

    Required:

    • i) What made Pathao's business model disruptive in Bangladesh?
    • ii) Which traditional industries were most affected by Pathao's expansion?
    • iii) What local factors enabled Pathao's success in the Bangladeshi market?

  • (c) Briefly point out the main contents of a business plan.

  • (d) In 2022, three graduates from Chattogram noticed that many farmers in rural Bangladesh struggled to sell fresh vegetables at fair prices because of multiple intermediaries. At the same time, urban consumers often paid high prices for vegetables that were not always fresh. Seeing this gap, the entrepreneurs developed a business plan for Aamra Fresh Agro, a startup that would connect farmers directly with consumers through a mobile app and delivery network. The business plan included a market analysis showing increasing demand for safe and fresh food in major cities such as Dhaka and Chattogram. The founders estimated an initial investment of BDT 15 lakh for app development, cold-storage facilities, and delivery vehicles. Revenue would come from a commission on each sale and subscription packages for regular customers. The plan identified potential risks, including transportation delays, seasonal fluctuations in production, and competition from established online grocery platforms. To address these challenges, the entrepreneurs planned partnerships with local farmer cooperatives and logistics providers. They also projected that the business would break even within three years if monthly sales grew by 10%. After presenting the business plan to investors, the startup secured seed funding and launched operations. Within the first year, it supplied fresh vegetables to more than 2,000 households and increased participating farmers' incomes by approximately 20%.

    Required:

    • i) What were the main sources of revenue for Aamra Fresh Agro?
    • ii) What risks were identified in the business plan?

Question 4

  • (a) What are the prerequisites for building a strong ethical culture in a firm?

  • (b) Define the concept liability of newness. What can a new venture do to overcome the liability of newness?

  • (c) Why does having relevant industry experience help the founder of a firm to be successful?

  • (d) FreshBrew is a mobile coffee truck business started by college friends Mia and Arif. They noticed many commuters near their city's business district lacked quick access to quality coffee. After securing a small investor, they launched the truck and hired an assistant manager. Although they had a great product, the business struggled. Customers sometimes waited too long, and social media feedback was slow. Mia realised that they needed more help with operations and marketing to grow. They also felt inexperienced running a business and had no credible reputation with suppliers yet. Mia and Arif invited two industry advisors with café experience to join their team to help with training and supplier contracts.

    Required:

    • i. What concept explains FreshBrew's early struggles because the founders lacked experience and reputation?
    • ii. Why might adding industry advisors help FreshBrew?
    • iii. What is the benefit of having more than one founder or team member?

Question 5

  • (a) Define acquisition with an example. Why do acquisitions fail sometimes?

  • (b) What challenges do second-generation entrepreneurs face when taking over established family business?

  • (c) Amena Akter, a resident of Cumilla, started a small online clothing business in 2019. She sold traditional Bangladeshi dresses through social media and initially operated from her home with a small amount of personal savings. Her products gained popularity among local customers, and she planned to expand her business by opening a boutique and hiring employees. However, Amena faced several challenges common to many women entrepreneurs in Bangladesh. One major challenge was limited access to finance. Banks often required collateral and lengthy documentation, which made it difficult for her to obtain a business loan. She also encountered social barriers, as some family and community members believed that women should prioritise household responsibilities over business activities. Another challenge was balancing family and business commitments. As a mother of two children, Amena had to manage household duties while handling customer orders, supplier negotiations, and marketing activities. Additionally, she faced difficulties in building professional networks, which are often crucial for obtaining business information and opportunities. The COVID-19 pandemic further disrupted her business by reducing customer demand and creating supply-chain problems. Despite these obstacles, Amena adapted by increasing her online marketing efforts, offering home delivery services, and participating in entrepreneurship training programmes organised by local support organisations. Today, her business employs six women and serves customers across Bangladesh. Amena's story demonstrates both the significant challenges faced by women entrepreneurs and their ability to overcome barriers through resilience, innovation, and determination.

    Required:

    • i) What financial challenge did Amena face?
    • ii) What social barrier affected her business growth?
    • iii) How did family responsibilities impact her entrepreneurship?
    • iv) What strategies helped her overcome challenges?

  • (b) What are biographical characteristics? Identify and explain how they affect Organisational Behaviour.

  • (c) What causes job satisfaction? Compare the relative importance of pay versus the work itself.

  • (a) What are the three components of attitude? How can managers use an understanding of attitudes to predict employee behaviour?

  • (b) What is Emotional Labour? Distinguish between surface acting and deep acting with examples from a customer service job.

  • (c) GreenMart Superstore employs a large team of cashiers and customer service representatives. In the past six months, management noticed rising absenteeism, customer complaints, and employee turnover. Exit interviews revealed employees felt unappreciated, emotionally exhausted, and dissatisfied with their work. Several employees stated they had to 'fake smiles' all day despite dealing with rude customers and heavy workloads.

    Required:

    • i. Which major job attitudes—job satisfaction, organisational commitment, or employee engagement—seem most affected at GreenMart? Explain with reference to the case.

In the first few weeks, members were polite but uncertain about roles. By the second month, open conflicts arose over who should lead. Members from different departments argued about priorities, and the project fell behind schedule.

    Required:

  • (b) Define power. What are the five bases of power explored in the text? Which base of power is most sustainable in the long run, and why?

  • (c) Mr. X was recently promoted to Factory Manager at DynaFab Ltd. He noticed that several senior workers ignored his instructions but quickly obeyed orders from the previous manager, who had worked at DynaFab for 20 years. Mr. X relies mostly on his formal authority (position) to gain compliance, but the results have been poor. Workers seem to respect only those they consider experienced and trustworthy.

    Required:

    • i. Which type of power is Mr. X relying on, and which power bases does the previous manager likely possess? Use the case to support your answer.

A group of senior tellers refused to adopt the new software system, causing operational delays.
