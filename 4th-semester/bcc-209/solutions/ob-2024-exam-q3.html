OB 2024 Exam Q3

(a)

What is personality? How do we typically measure it? What factors determine personality?

Definition of Personality Personality is defined as the sum total of ways in which an individual reacts to and interacts with others. It is most often described in terms of the measurable traits a person exhibits. In organizational behavior, it is viewed as the dynamic organization within the individual of those psychophysical systems that determine their unique adjustments to their environment.

How Personality is Measured The two primary methods used to measure personality in organizational settings are:

  • Self-Report Surveys: These are the most common means of measuring personality, where individuals evaluate themselves by rating their agreement with a series of statements. While efficient, a major disadvantage is that respondents can falsify answers to create a favorable impression (impression management) or their accuracy can be compromised by their current mood.
  • Observer-Rating Surveys: These surveys are completed by an independent observer (e.g., a coworker, manager, or trained psychologist) who rates the individual's personality. Observer ratings tend to be more accurate predictors of job success because they avoid self-enhancement bias.

Factors Determining Personality An individual's personality is generally determined by two overarching factors:

  • Heredity: This refers to factors determined at conception. Physical stature, facial attractiveness, gender, temperament, muscle composition and reflexes, energy level, and biological rhythms are generally considered to be either completely or substantially influenced by parentage (genetic makeup). The heredity approach argues that the ultimate explanation of an individual's personality is the molecular structure of the genes, located in the chromosomes.
  • Environment: This includes the culture in which individuals are raised, early conditioning, norms among family, friends, and social groups, and other situational influences that shape the manifestation of hereditary potentials.

(b)

How do the Big Five traits predict work behavior?

The Big Five Personality Model identifies five basic dimensions that underlie human personality. Each trait significantly predicts specific aspects of work behavior and organizational outcomes:

  • Conscientiousness (Measure of reliability):
    • Prediction: This is the strongest predictor of job performance across almost all occupations.
    • Work Behavior: Highly conscientious individuals are dependable, organized, and persistent. They develop higher levels of job knowledge, exert greater effort, and demonstrate strong organizational citizenship behaviors (OCBs). Conversely, low conscientiousness leads to higher counterproductive work behaviors (CWBs).
  • Emotional Stability / Neuroticism (Ability to withstand stress):
    • Prediction: Strongly related to high job satisfaction and low stress levels.
    • Work Behavior: Employees with high emotional stability are better equipped to handle daily demands, adapt to unexpected changes, and experience fewer negative emotions. Those low in stability (high neuroticism) are prone to burnout, high absenteeism, and reduced productivity under pressure.
  • Extraversion (Comfort level with relationships):
    • Prediction: Predicts success in positions requiring significant interpersonal interaction and leadership.
    • Work Behavior: Extraverts are assertive, social, and energetic. They perform exceptionally well in sales, management, and customer-facing roles. They are also more likely to take charge, be perceived as leaders, and thrive in team-oriented environments, though they can sometimes exhibit impulsive decision-making.
  • Openness to Experience (Range of interests and fascination with novelty):
    • Prediction: Predicts training proficiency, adaptability to change, and creative performance.
    • Work Behavior: Open individuals are curious, imaginative, and artistically sensitive. In the workplace, they cope effectively with organizational transformations, generate innovative solutions to unstructured problems, and excel in continuous learning environments.
  • Agreeableness (Propensity to defer to others):
    • Prediction: Predicts interpersonal compatibility, effective teamwork, and service orientation.
    • Work Behavior: Agreeable individuals are cooperative, warm, and trusting. They excel in collaborative projects, show higher compliance with organizational rules, and are less likely to engage in workplace interpersonal conflicts or deviant behaviors.

(c)

What is perception, and what factors influence our perception?

Definition of Perception Perception is a process by which individuals organize and interpret their sensory impressions in order to give meaning to their environment. In organizational behavior, perception is crucial because people's behavior is based on their perception of what reality is, not on reality itself. Factors Influencing Perception A number of factors operate to shape and sometimes distort perception. These factors can reside in the perceiver, in the object or target being perceived, or in the situation where the perception occurs:

  • Factors in the Perceiver: When an individual looks at a target, their interpretation is heavily influenced by personal characteristics, including:
    • Attitudes and Motives: Unmet needs or deep-seated biases alter what is noticed.
    • Interests: What matters to the individual directs their attention.
    • Past Experiences: Familiarity or past trauma shapes current interpretations.
    • Expectations: Individuals often see what they expect to see.
  • Factors in the Target: The characteristics of the target being observed can affect what is perceived:
    • Novelty and Motion: Loud, moving, or highly unusual targets stand out.
    • Sound and Size: Extremely large objects or loud sounds command focus.
    • Background, Proximity, and Similarity: Targets are not viewed in isolation; their relationship to their background and proximity to other objects shapes group perception (e.g., grouping similar-looking employees together).
  • Factors in the Situation: The context in which an object or event is seen matters:
    • Time: The hour or season can alter attention and meaning.
    • Work Setting: Normal actions in a casual setting may look out of place on a formal factory floor.
    • Social Setting: Dressing formally is perceived differently at a party versus an executive board meeting.

(d)

What is the link between perception and decision-making? How does one affect the other?

The Link Between Perception and Decision-Making Individuals in organizations make decisions—choosing from two or more alternatives—based on their perceptual inputs. Decision-making occurs as a reaction to a problem, which is a discrepancy between a current state of affairs and some desired state. Because problems must be identified and interpreted, the entire decision-making process relies entirely on the perceptual process of the decision-maker. How One Affects the Other Perception heavily biases and shapes every step of organizational decision-making in the following ways:

  • Problem Identification and Awareness: Before a decision is made, a problem must be perceived to exist. What one manager views as a critical issue (e.g., a 2% drop in quarterly sales), another might perceive as a routine fluctuation. Perceptual distortions can cause managers to ignore real problems or misinterpret benign situations as crises.
  • Information Screening and Processing: Decision-makers gather large volumes of data to analyze alternatives. Perception filters this information through selective perception. Managers screen out information that contradicts their existing beliefs and selectively highlight data that supports their preferred outcomes.
  • Development and Evaluation of Alternatives: When generating options, decision-makers interpret potential risks and outcomes through their perceptual lens. Perceptual errors like the halo effect (overvaluing an alternative due to one positive trait) or contrast effects (misjudging an option because it is presented right after a terrible one) lead to flawed evaluations and biased final choices.
