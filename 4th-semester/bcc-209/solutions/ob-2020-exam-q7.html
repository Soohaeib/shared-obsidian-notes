Levels of Conflict in the Organization and Management of Conflicting Situations

Organizations are composed of individuals and groups with diverse backgrounds, values, and goals, making conflict an inevitable aspect of organizational life. Understanding the levels of conflict and the strategies to manage them effectively is crucial for maintaining organizational health and performance.

Levels of Conflict in an Organization

Conflict can occur at several interconnected levels within an organization, ranging from an individual's internal struggle to friction between different organizations:

  1. Intrapersonal Conflict:
    • This occurs within an individual. It often arises from role conflict, goal conflict, or cognitive dissonance. For example, an employee might experience conflict when asked to perform a task that violates their ethical standards (value conflict), or when faced with competing demands from multiple supervisors (role conflict).
  2. Interpersonal Conflict:
    • This occurs between two or more individuals who perceive their goals, values, or styles to be incompatible. It is often driven by personality clashes, communication breakdowns, competition for scarce resources, or differences in perceptions and backgrounds.
  3. Intragroup Conflict:
    • This conflict happens within a single department, team, or workgroup. It typically involves differences regarding how tasks should be accomplished (task conflict), how work should be coordinated (process conflict), or interpersonal friction among group members (relationship conflict).
  4. Intergroup Conflict:
    • This occurs between different departments, teams, or divisions within the organization (such as between Marketing and Production, or Research & Development and Finance). It is usually caused by competition for budget allocations, differing departmental goals, interdependence of tasks, or divergent viewpoints on organizational priorities.
  5. Interorganizational Conflict:
    • This takes place between different corporate entities or organizations. Examples include disputes between manufacturers and suppliers, or competition between rival firms within the same industry over market share.
How an Effective Manager Handles Conflicting Situations

An effective manager acts as a mediator and strategic decision-maker to resolve or channel conflict constructively. According to established organizational behavior frameworks (such as the Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument), managers utilize five primary conflict-handling intentions based on two dimensions: assertiveness (the degree to which the individual attempts to satisfy their own concerns) and cooperativeness (the degree to which the individual attempts to satisfy the other person's concerns).

  1. Competing (Forcing):
    • Characteristics: Highly assertive and uncooperative. The manager pursues their own concerns at the other party's expense, using whatever power seems appropriate to win.
    • Appropriate Situations: When quick, decisive action is vital (e.g., emergencies), on important issues where unpopular actions need implementing (e.g., cost-cutting, enforcing unpopular rules), or on issues vital to company welfare when management knows they are right.
  2. Collaborating (Problem Solving):
    • Characteristics: Highly assertive and highly cooperative. The manager attempts to work with the other person to find an integrative solution that fully satisfies the concerns of both parties.
    • Appropriate Situations: To find an integrative solution when both sets of concerns are too important to be compromised, to merge insights from people with different perspectives, or to gain commitment by incorporating concerns into a consensus.
  3. Compromising:
    • Characteristics: Moderate in both assertiveness and cooperativeness. The objective is to find an expedient, mutually acceptable solution that partially satisfies both parties.
    • Appropriate Situations: When goals are important but not worth the effort or potential disruption of more assertive modes, when opponents with equal power are committed to mutually exclusive goals, or to achieve temporary settlements to complex issues.
  4. Avoiding:
    • Characteristics: Unassertive and uncooperative. The manager does not immediately pursue their own concerns or those of the other person, effectively sidestepping or postponing the conflict.
    • Appropriate Situations: When an issue is trivial or more important issues are pressing, when you perceive no chance of satisfying your concerns, or when potential disruption outweighs the benefits of resolution.
  5. Accommodating (Yielding):
    • Characteristics: Unassertive and highly cooperative. The manager neglects their own concerns to satisfy the concerns of the other person.
    • Appropriate Situations: When you find you are wrong—to allow a better position to be heard and show you are reasonable, when issues are much more important to others than to yourself, or to build social credits for later issues.
Structural Approaches for Managers

Beyond interpersonal styles, managers can employ structural interventions to handle conflict:

  • Altering Structural Variables: Redesigning jobs, transferring members, or creating coordinating positions to decouple interdependent units.
  • Expanding Resources: If the conflict is caused by a scarcity of resources (such as budget or space), expanding the resource pool can create a win-win environment.
  • Creating Superordinate Goals: Establishing a shared goal that cannot be achieved without the cooperation of all conflicting parties.

Key Players in the Change Process and Approaches to Manage Resistance to Change

Organizational change is a complex process that requires deliberate planning, execution, and management. Successfully navigating change depends heavily on identifying key stakeholders and proactively managing the inevitable human resistance that accompanies it.

Key Players in the Change Process
  1. Change Agents:
    • These are the individuals who act as catalysts and assume the responsibility for managing change activities. They can be internal managers, employees specialized in organizational development, or external consultants hired for their expertise. Change agents diagnose problems, develop strategic interventions, and facilitate execution.
  2. Change Sponsors (Top Management):
    • High-level executives who possess the formal authority and resources to approve, fund, and legitimize the change initiative. Without strong, visible sponsorship from top leadership, change initiatives often fail due to a lack of institutional backing and resource constraints.
  3. Change Targets (Recipients):
    • The employees, teams, or managers who are directly impacted by the change and must alter their daily work processes, routines, skills, or behaviors. Their acceptance and adoption of the new state determine the ultimate success of the change effort.
  4. Resisters and Critics:
    • Individuals or coalitions within the organization who actively or passively oppose the change initiative. While often viewed negatively, critics can provide valuable feedback by highlighting flaws in the change strategy or uncovering operational risks that management overlooked.
Approaches to Deal with Resistance to Change

Managers can draw upon classic frameworks, such as Kotter and Schlesinger’s strategies, to overcome resistance to organizational change:

Approach When to Use Advantages Drawbacks
Education and Communication Where there is a lack of information or inaccurate information and analysis. Once persuaded, people will often help with the implementation of the change. Can be very time-consuming if lots of people are involved.
Participation and Involvement Where the initiators do not have all the information needed to design the change and where others have considerable power to resist. People who participate will be committed to implementing change, and any relevant information they have will be integrated into the plan. Can be time-consuming; participants could design an inappropriate change.
Facilitation and Support Where people are resisting because of adjustment problems or high anxiety. No other approach works as well with adjustment problems. Can be time-consuming, expensive, and still fail.
Negotiation and Agreement Where someone or some group will clearly lose out in a change and where that group has considerable power to resist. Sometimes it is a relatively easy way to avoid major resistance. Can be too expensive if it alerts others to negotiate for compliance.
Manipulation and Co-optation Where other tactics will not work or are too expensive. It can be a relatively quick and inexpensive solution to resistance problems. Can lead to future problems if people feel they were manipulated.
Explicit and Implicit Coercion Where speed is essential and the change initiators possess considerable power. It is speedy and can overcome any kind of resistance. Can be risky if it leaves people mad at the initiators.
Summary of Selection Strategies

An effective manager must assess the nature of the resistance, the source of power held by the resisters, the level of trust within the organization, and the time available to implement the change before selecting the appropriate mix of these approaches.
